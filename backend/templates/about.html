Why This Matters

The rapid proliferation of LLMs has introduced a systemic threat to global research — synthetic manuscripts indistinguishable to the human eye.

⚠ The Problem

Synthetic Scholarship at Scale

LLMs now generate grammatically perfect, structurally plausible academic papers from scratch — bypassing conventional plagiarism detectors entirely.

  • Up to 1–2% of conference submissions estimated to be AI-generated
  • Fabricated citations and hallucinated data poison the scholarly record
  • Volunteer reviewers waste finite expertise on non-existent science
  • Traditional similarity-detection tools cannot catch generated content
  • Generative tools grow more sophisticated every quarter
✓ The Solution

Forensic Fingerprint Detection

PaperTrap shifts from word-matching to deep linguistic analysis — catching the subtle patterns that LLMs cannot easily hide.

  • 60+ discriminative features across 4 signal categories
  • Ensemble RF + Gradient Boosting for robust, stable predictions
  • Perplexity scoring exposes AI's preference for high-probability tokens
  • SHAP explainability shows reviewers exactly why a paper was flagged
  • Validated against T5-paraphrasing adversarial attacks

How PaperTrap Works

A six-stage forensic pipeline transforms raw PDF into an explainable, confidence-calibrated verdict.

01
Ingestion Layer
PDF Parsing & Text Extraction

pdfplumber and PyPDF2 strip multi-column layouts, headers, page numbers, and metadata — isolating clean body text. The pipeline handles two-column academic formatting, inline equations, and footnotes without contaminating the NLP input.

pdfplumber PyPDF2 regex cleaning section detection
02
Linguistic Layer
Perplexity & Coherence Scoring

A language model scores token-sequence predictability. AI generators optimise for maximum-probability continuations, leaving a characteristic low-perplexity fingerprint. Human academics exhibit far higher token surprise — a signal that persists even through paraphrasing.

GPT-2 perplexity coherence ratio entropy scoring
03
Stylometric Layer
POS Tagging & Syntactic Analysis

spaCy annotates Part-of-Speech distributions, dependency parse depths, and clause-complexity ratios. LLMs produce "flat" syntax — repetitive structures with characteristic noun/verb ratios and shallow dependency trees — that diverge measurably from human academic prose.

spaCy POS tags dep. parse depth TTR (Type-Token Ratio) Flesch-Kincaid
04
Statistical Layer
N-gram Distribution & Structural Analysis

Bigram and trigram entropy profiles reveal AI's statistically smoother token distributions. Simultaneously, citation density, Abstract-Introduction-Conclusion section ratios, and paragraph length distributions expose structural irregularities common in hallucinated research.

bigram entropy citation density section ratio hapax rate
05
Ensemble Layer
Random Forest + Gradient Boosting (Soft Voting)

60 extracted features feed into a Soft Voting ensemble. Random Forest captures non-linear feature interactions; Gradient Boosting sequentially corrects residual errors. The soft-vote aggregates predicted probabilities — rather than hard labels — producing a more stable, calibrated confidence score than either classifier alone.

Random Forest Gradient Boosting Soft Voting hyperparameter tuning stratified k-fold
06
Explainability Layer
SHAP Values & Confidence Calibration

SHapley Additive exPlanations decompose each prediction into per-feature contributions. The dashboard renders a ranked bar chart showing which signals — low vocabulary diversity, shallow dependency depth, citation absence — drove the verdict. Borderline scores trigger a "manual review recommended" flag rather than a hard binary.

SHAP TreeExplainer force plots confidence calibration borderline flagging

Frequently Asked Questions

PaperTrap achieves 92% accuracy on a 500-paper stratified test set, with 96% precision (low false-positive rate) and 88% recall. Key limitations: it is currently scoped to English-language computer science papers only; it does not detect partial AI editing or "hybrid" documents; and it does not verify citation accuracy or detect traditional plagiarism. Results should be treated as probabilistic screening signals, not definitive verdicts.
PaperTrap includes an adversarial robustness filter validated against T5-paraphrased content and SCIgen-generated baselines. Minor synonym swapping or sentence restructuring does not significantly change the underlying perplexity score, POS distribution, or n-gram entropy profiles — the signals the system relies on. However, heavy manual rewriting by a skilled human can degrade detection confidence, which is why borderline cases are flagged for expert review rather than hard-classified.
Random Forest excels at capturing non-linear feature interactions and is robust to noisy features, while Gradient Boosting iteratively corrects residual errors from previous trees, giving strong performance on structured tabular feature data. The Soft Voting ensemble aggregates predicted probabilities rather than hard label votes — producing a smoother, better-calibrated confidence score than either model alone. Together they achieve ~3% better accuracy than either classifier in isolation.
SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) is a game-theory-grounded method for attributing a model prediction to its input features. For PaperTrap, this is critical: a journal editor or conference chair cannot act on a black-box "94.7% AI" score alone. SHAP shows exactly which signals — "perplexity was unusually low", "vocabulary diversity far below human baseline", "citation density near zero" — contributed to the verdict, enabling reviewers to validate the system's reasoning independently and make informed decisions.
In its current demonstration scope, uploaded files are processed in-memory only and are not persisted to disk or transmitted to external servers. For a production deployment, a clear data-retention policy and privacy impact assessment would be required before handling any unpublished manuscripts, which may constitute confidential pre-publication research.
Training data was generated using GPT-2 and T5 to ensure the feature set captures a breadth of generation styles. The linguistic fingerprints targeted — high token predictability, flat syntactic structures, low vocabulary diversity — are architectural properties of autoregressive language models in general, not specific to any one model family. In practice the detector generalises reasonably to GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 class outputs, though detection confidence is somewhat lower for very large models that produce more varied text.
