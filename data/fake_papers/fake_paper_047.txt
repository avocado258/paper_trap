### Abstract
Despite the empirical success of Transformer-based architectures in natural language processing (NLP), the interpretability of self-attention mechanisms remains a subject of intense debate. Standard attention weights often fail to provide faithful causal explanations due to the non-linear interaction of hidden states across layers. In this paper, we propose *Latent Attention Traversal* (LAT), a novel framework that treats the propagation of information through attention heads as a flow optimization problem. By enforcing a sparsity-constrained path integral over the attention graph, LAT identifies "information bottlenecks" that disproportionately influence final classification logits. We provide theoretical evidence that LAT satisfies the axioms of faithfulness and local sensitivity. Experimental results on the GLUE benchmark and custom sentiment analysis datasets demonstrate that LAT outperforms gradient-based attribution methods in both human-annotated alignment and robustness against adversarial perturbations.

**Keywords:** Explainable AI, Self-Attention, Causal Inference, Information Flow, NLP Interpretability.

---

### 1. Introduction
The ubiquity of large language models (LLMs) has necessitated a shift from predictive accuracy toward interpretability. While self-attention is touted as the cornerstone of Transformers, research suggests that raw attention scores are poor indicators of model decision-making [1]. This decoupling between attention weights and prediction performance stems from the fact that attention matrices are learned representations of token interactions, rather than explicit mappings of causal influence [2].

To address this, existing literature has moved toward post-hoc attribution methods. However, techniques like Integrated Gradients (IG) or LIME often suffer from high variance and computational overhead [3]. Furthermore, they frequently ignore the layered nature of Transformers, treating the network as a black-box function rather than a sequence of information-processing stages [4]. 

We introduce the Latent Attention Traversal (LAT) framework. LAT conceptualizes the Transformer as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of latent states. By applying an optimal transport objective to the attention matrices, we identify the most influential paths, or "traversal sequences," that dictate the final classification. Our contribution is threefold: (1) we formalize the attention flow problem as a constrained optimization; (2) we introduce an efficient algorithm for path extraction; and (3) we validate the framework against state-of-the-art baselines on high-stakes classification tasks.

---

### 2. Related Work
Interpretability in deep learning has historically been dominated by gradient-based saliency methods [5]. Early works sought to attribute importance to input pixels or tokens by calculating partial derivatives [6]. However, these methods are often sensitive to local noise and fail to capture global contextual dependencies [7].

Attention-based interpretability emerged with the advent of the Transformer architecture [8]. Bahdanau et al. [9] initially argued that attention weights could serve as soft alignments. Subsequent critiques by Jain and Wallace [10] challenged this, demonstrating that attention weights are frequently uncorrelated with gradient-based feature importance. Our work builds upon the "Attention Rollout" paradigm [11], extending it to a differentiable flow problem that incorporates cross-layer dependencies.

Recent advances in causal interpretability [12] have proposed interventions on latent variables. These methods, while robust, are often prohibitively slow [13]. LAT distinguishes itself by providing a closed-form approximation of these causal impacts, bridgeing the gap between efficient saliency mapping and rigorous causal analysis.

### 3. Background / Preliminaries
Let $X = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ be an input sequence of tokens. A Transformer model $\mathcal{F}$ maps $X$ to a logit vector $y \in \mathbb{R}^K$ via a series of $L$ layers. Each layer $l$ contains $H$ attention heads. The attention matrix $A^{(l,h)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ denotes the interaction weights between tokens in layer $l$, head $h$.

The standard attention computation is defined as:
$$A^{(l,h)} = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \quad (1)$$
where $Q$ and $K$ are the query and key projections. The state update is given by $V^{(l)} = \sum_{h=1}^H \text{Attn}(A^{(l,h)})W_O^{(l,h)}$. We define the *Total Attention Influence* (TAI) as the cumulative weight assigned to a token path $\pi = \{t_0, t_1, \dots, t_L\}$, where $t_l$ is the index of the token attended to at layer $l$.

---

### 4. Methodology / Proposed Approach
LAT operates by augmenting the attention matrices with a regularization term $\Omega(A)$ that penalizes dense connectivity, forcing the model to select a sparse set of high-influence paths. We define the flow objective as minimizing the divergence between the latent state transition and the primary logit direction.

Let $\mathbf{z}_l$ be the hidden representation at layer $l$. We define the traversal cost for a path $\pi$ as:
$$J(\pi) = \sum_{l=1}^L \| \mathbf{z}_l - \text{proj}(\mathbf{z}_{l-1}, A^{(l)}) \|_2^2 + \lambda \Omega(A^{(l)}) \quad (2)$$
where $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter balancing sparsity and reconstruction fidelity.

#### 4.1 The LAT Algorithm
The algorithm seeks to identify the path $\pi^*$ that maximizes the logit contribution $\nabla_{\mathbf{z}_L} y$. 

**Algorithm 1: Latent Attention Traversal**
1. **Input:** Input embeddings $X$, Model $\mathcal{F}$, Sparsity factor $\lambda$.
2. **Forward Pass:** Compute all $A^{(l,h)}$ and hidden states $H^{(l)}$.
3. **Backpropagation:** Compute importance scores $\alpha_{i,l} = \frac{\partial y}{\partial A_{i,l}}$.
4. **Path Optimization:** Solve the constrained flow:
   $$\min_{\pi} \sum_{l} \alpha_{l} \cdot A^{(l)}_{\pi(l), \pi(l+1)}$$
   subject to sparsity constraint $\sum_{l} \text{sparsity}(A^{(l)}) < \tau$.
5. **Output:** A ranked set of paths $\Pi^*$.

---

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
**Theorem 1 (Faithfulness):** *Given an optimal traversal path $\pi^*$, the gradient of the logit $y$ with respect to the input $x_i$ is linearly proportional to the cumulative flow along $\pi^*$.*

*Proof Sketch:* By the chain rule, $\frac{\partial y}{\partial x_i} = \sum_{\pi} \prod_{l=1}^L \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}_l}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{l-1}} \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}_{l-1}}{\partial x_i}$. Since LAT restricts the summation to sparse paths, the approximation error $||\nabla y - \sum_{\pi^*} \dots||$ is bounded by the residual variance of the pruned attention weights. Given the contractive property of the softmax operator, the error remains $O(1/L)$ as $L \to \infty$.

**Computational Complexity:** The path optimization step is formulated as a Viterbi-like search, resulting in $O(L \cdot H \cdot n^2)$ complexity, where $n$ is sequence length. This makes LAT suitable for deployment in large-scale inference pipelines.

### 6. Experiments
#### 6.1 Datasets
We evaluate LAT on the **SST-2 (Sentiment Analysis)** and **QNLI (Question-Answering)** datasets from the GLUE benchmark. We also employ the **ERASER** benchmark for human-annotated rationale evaluation [14].

#### 6.2 Baselines
We compare LAT against:
1. **Raw Attention (RA):** Unmodified softmax weights.
2. **Attention Rollout (AR):** Cumulative multiplication of matrices.
3. **Integrated Gradients (IG):** Path-integral gradient attribution.
4. **LIME:** Perturbation-based local linear explanations.
5. **Shapley Value Sampling (SVS):** Game-theoretic feature importance.

#### 6.3 Results
Results are measured by *Faithfulness Correlation* (FC) and *AUPRC* (Area Under Precision-Recall Curve) for rationale alignment.

**Table 1: Performance on SST-2 and QNLI Benchmarks**
| Method | SST-2 (FC) | QNLI (FC) | AUPRC (Alignment) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| RA | 0.52 | 0.48 | 0.41 |
| AR | 0.61 | 0.55 | 0.50 |
| IG | 0.74 | 0.68 | 0.62 |
| LIME | 0.69 | 0.64 | 0.58 |
| SVS | 0.76 | 0.71 | 0.65 |
| **LAT (Ours)** | **0.82** | **0.79** | **0.74** |

### 7. Ablation Study
We analyze the sensitivity of LAT to the sparsity parameter $\lambda$ and the number of layers $L$.

**Table 2: Ablation of LAT Components**
| Configuration | FC (SST-2) | Latency (ms) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| LAT (No Sparsity) | 0.71 | 45 |
| LAT (Fixed Path) | 0.75 | 12 |
| LAT (Full Framework) | 0.82 | 28 |

Observations indicate that while the full framework is slightly slower than a static path approximation, the gain in faithfulness is significant. Sparsity is essential for preventing "attention smearing" across irrelevant context.

### 8. Discussion / Insights
The experimental results confirm that LAT provides a more robust explanation of Transformer behavior than traditional saliency methods. By explicitly accounting for the cross-layer flow of information, LAT avoids the pitfalls of attributing importance to "noisy" layers that do not contribute to the final logit. The superiority of LAT on the ERASER benchmark highlights its alignment with human intuition regarding causal tokens.

Furthermore, we observed that LAT consistently identified linguistic markers (e.g., negation tokens in sentiment analysis) that other models missed. This suggests that the latent flow captured by our framework is better aligned with the semantic structure of the language, rather than just the statistical associations captured by raw attention.

The trade-off between latency and accuracy remains a critical consideration. While LAT is significantly faster than Shapley-based approximations, it introduces a 15-20% overhead compared to raw attention. Future iterations will explore weight quantization to further reduce the inference burden of the traversal optimization.

---

### 9. Limitations & Future Work
Despite its effectiveness, LAT has limitations. First, the current formulation assumes a linear composition of attention flows. While this provides a strong approximation, deep non-linearities in the MLP blocks of Transformers (GELU activations) may introduce higher-order interactions that our model currently approximates as noise.

Second, LAT is currently optimized for post-hoc explanation. Integrating the LAT objective directly into the model training phase as a regularization term could yield "intrinsically interpretable" models that maintain high performance without sacrificing transparency. 

Future work will investigate the scalability of LAT to models with billions of parameters (e.g., Llama-3, GPT-4). We hypothesize that the sparsity-constrained flow approach will prove even more critical in deeper architectures where attention diffusion is a major source of interpretability decay.

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this work, we presented Latent Attention Traversal (LAT), a novel framework for interpreting Transformer models. By modeling information propagation as a sparse flow problem, we achieved superior faithfulness in attribution compared to baseline methods. Our theoretical analysis and experimental validation demonstrate that LAT is a robust tool for XAI in NLP, bridging the gap between statistical performance and causal understanding.
