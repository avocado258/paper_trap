### Abstract
Tabular data augmentation remains a significant bottleneck in machine learning pipelines, particularly in domains characterized by high-dimensional sparsity and categorical imbalances. While Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have demonstrated success in image synthesis, their application to tabular data is often hindered by mode collapse and the inability to preserve intricate feature correlations. We propose a novel architecture, the Conditional Manifold-Aligned Wasserstein GAN (CMA-WGAN), which leverages a dual-stream latent space alignment strategy to enforce structural consistency between conditional inputs and generated tabular records. By integrating a Wasserstein distance objective with a gradient penalty and a secondary manifold-preservation loss, our model significantly reduces the distribution shift between synthetic and real-world samples. Empirical evaluations on four benchmark datasets (Adult, Credit Card Fraud, Loan Default, and UCI Bank Marketing) demonstrate that CMA-WGAN outperforms state-of-the-art baselines in both downstream classification accuracy and structural fidelity. We further provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the manifold-alignment objective, establishing a tighter bound on the Earth Mover’s distance compared to standard conditional GANs.

**Keywords:** Tabular Data Augmentation, Wasserstein GAN, Manifold Alignment, Generative Modeling, Mode Collapse.

---

### 1. Introduction
The efficacy of deep learning models is fundamentally tied to the availability of large-scale, high-quality training data. In critical sectors such as finance, healthcare, and insurance, tabular datasets are frequently limited by privacy regulations, class imbalance, and significant missing-value artifacts [1]. Traditional data augmentation techniques, such as Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE), often fail to capture the complex, non-linear dependencies inherent in tabular distributions [2]. 

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) provide a powerful framework for density estimation and sample generation [3]. However, standard GAN formulations struggle with tabular data due to the mix of continuous and discrete variables, which disrupts the continuity assumptions required for gradient-based optimization [4]. Furthermore, conditional generation—the ability to generate samples belonging to specific classes or subgroups—is prone to mode collapse, where the generator ignores the conditional input to minimize the loss against the discriminator [5].

In this paper, we introduce the Conditional Manifold-Aligned Wasserstein GAN (CMA-WGAN). Our approach departs from traditional conditional GAN architectures by decoupling the feature generation process from the label-conditioning mechanism through a manifold alignment module. By optimizing the Wasserstein distance [6] while simultaneously minimizing a secondary manifold-preservation loss, we ensure that the generator respects the latent topology of the underlying real-world data. This innovation effectively mitigates mode collapse and improves the structural integrity of synthetic records.

The primary contributions of this work are as follows:
1. A novel GAN architecture (CMA-WGAN) that utilizes manifold alignment to stabilize conditional generation in tabular spaces.
2. A rigorous formalization of the manifold-preservation loss, providing theoretical grounding for the observed improvement in feature correlation retention.
3. An extensive experimental evaluation demonstrating that CMA-WGAN achieves superior performance over five state-of-the-art baselines across multiple metrics, including Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistics and F1-score improvement in downstream classifiers.

### 2. Related Work
The literature on generative models for tabular data has evolved rapidly, moving from heuristic oversampling methods to deep latent variable models. The seminal work of SMOTE and its derivatives, such as Borderline-SMOTE and ADASYN, provided the foundation for addressing class imbalance, yet these methods remain limited by their inability to learn the underlying manifold of the data [7, 8].

Recent advancements in deep generative modeling have been dominated by GANs and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs). The MedGAN architecture was among the first to successfully apply GANs to high-dimensional medical records, utilizing an autoencoder pre-training phase to handle categorical features [9]. Subsequently, the TabGAN framework introduced a mechanism to incorporate labels directly into the generator, though it remained susceptible to training instability [10]. 

The introduction of the Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) offered a robust alternative to Jensen-Shannon divergence-based GANs by providing a smoother gradient flow, which is crucial for training stability [11]. The WGAN-GP variant further refined this by introducing a gradient penalty to enforce the Lipschitz constraint [12]. Despite these successes, applying WGAN-GP to tabular data remains challenging because the "Earth Mover's" distance is computed over Euclidean space, which may not align with the semantic manifold of structured data [13].

Several recent papers have attempted to bridge this gap using conditional mechanisms. The CTGAN model, for instance, utilizes a conditional generator and a mode-specific normalization technique to handle multi-modal continuous distributions [14]. Similarly, TVAE has shown that VAEs can outperform GANs in tabular settings by providing a more stable training objective [15]. However, these methods often struggle with high-dimensional cross-feature dependencies. Our work addresses these deficiencies by explicitly enforcing manifold alignment, a technique inspired by semi-supervised domain adaptation literature [16, 17, 18].

### 3. Background and Preliminaries
Let $\mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ be a tabular dataset consisting of $N$ samples, where each sample $x \in \mathcal{X}$ is a vector of $d$ attributes. We assume $\mathcal{X}$ is associated with a label set $\mathcal{Y}$. The goal of tabular data augmentation is to learn a mapping from a latent noise space $z \sim \mathcal{P}_z$ and a conditional label $y \in \mathcal{Y}$ to a synthetic sample $\hat{x} = G(z, y)$.

The Wasserstein distance between the real distribution $\mathbb{P}_r$ and the synthetic distribution $\mathbb{P}_g$ is defined as:
$$W(\mathbb{P}_r, \mathbb{P}_g) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mathbb{P}_r, \mathbb{P}_g)} \mathbb{E}_{(x, \hat{x}) \sim \gamma} [\|x - \hat{x}\|]$$
where $\Pi(\mathbb{P}_r, \mathbb{P}_g)$ denotes the set of all joint distributions whose marginals are $\mathbb{P}_r$ and $\mathbb{P}_g$, respectively [11].

To approximate this objective, the Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality is employed, requiring the discriminator (or critic) $D$ to be a 1-Lipschitz function. The standard WGAN-GP objective is:
$$\min_G \max_{D \in \mathcal{D}} \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathbb{P}_r} [D(x|y)] - \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x} \sim \mathbb{P}_g} [D(\hat{x}|y)] - \lambda \mathbb{E}_{\hat{x} \sim \hat{\mathbb{P}}_x} [(\|\nabla_{\hat{x}} D(\hat{x}|y)\|_2 - 1)^2]$$
where $\lambda$ is the gradient penalty coefficient and $\hat{\mathbb{P}}_x$ is the sampling distribution along lines between real and synthetic samples.

### 4. Methodology: The CMA-WGAN
The CMA-WGAN architecture consists of a conditional generator $G$ and a dual-stream critic $D$. The primary innovation is the introduction of a Manifold Alignment Module (MAM) that operates on the penultimate layer of the critic.

#### 4.1. The Manifold Alignment Module
The MAM forces the generator to maintain the structural topology of the real data by minimizing the discrepancy between the latent representations of real and synthetic samples. Given a batch of real samples $X$ and synthetic samples $\hat{X}$, we define the manifold loss $\mathcal{L}_{man}$ as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{man} = \frac{1}{N^2} \sum_{i,j} \left| \text{sim}(x_i, x_j) - \text{sim}(\hat{x}_i, \hat{x}_j) \right|^2$$
where $\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ is a kernel-based similarity function defined in the latent space of the critic.

#### 4.2. Algorithmic Formulation
The training procedure involves iteratively updating the critic to maximize the Wasserstein distance while minimizing the manifold discrepancy, followed by updating the generator to minimize the joint objective.

**Algorithm 1: CMA-WGAN Training**
1. **Input:** Data $\mathcal{X}$, labels $\mathcal{Y}$, batch size $m$, iterations $K$.
2. **Initialize:** $G_\theta, D_\phi$ with random weights.
3. **For** $t = 1 \dots K$:
    a. Sample $\{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathbb{P}_r$, $\{z^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^m \sim \mathcal{P}_z$.
    b. Generate $\hat{x}^{(i)} = G_\theta(z^{(i)}, y^{(i)})$.
    c. Calculate Critic Loss: $\mathcal{L}_D = \mathbb{E}[D_\phi(x|y)] - \mathbb{E}[D_\phi(\hat{x}|y)] + \lambda \text{GP} + \gamma \mathcal{L}_{man}$.
    d. Update $\phi$ via gradient ascent.
    e. Update $\theta$ via gradient descent on $-\mathbb{E}[D_\phi(\hat{x}|y)]$.
4. **End For**

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
We claim that the inclusion of $\mathcal{L}_{man}$ provides a regularization effect that prevents the critic from assigning arbitrary values to synthetic samples that are distant from the real manifold. 

*Theorem 1:* Let $\mathbb{P}_r$ be a distribution supported on a $k$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. If $D$ is $L$-Lipschitz, then the inclusion of $\mathcal{L}_{man}$ reduces the upper bound on the approximation error of the Wasserstein distance by a factor of $\mathcal{O}(\epsilon)$, where $\epsilon$ is the local curvature of $\mathcal{M}$.

*Proof Sketch:* The manifold alignment loss acts as a spectral constraint on the Gram matrix of the critic’s activations. By bounding the Frobenius norm of the difference between the real and synthetic Gram matrices, we effectively constrain the push-forward measure $G_\# \mathbb{P}_z$ to remain in the neighborhood of $\mathcal{M}$. This limits the "mass leakage" of the generator into low-density regions of the ambient space, thereby stabilizing the Wasserstein gradient.

### 6. Experiments
We evaluate the CMA-WGAN against five baselines: Vanilla GAN [3], WGAN-GP [12], CTGAN [14], TVAE [15], and SMOTE [7].

#### 6.1. Experimental Setup
All models were trained for 500 epochs with a batch size of 256. Adam optimizer was used with learning rates set to $2 \times 10^{-4}$ for the generator and $1 \times 10^{-4}$ for the critic. The gradient penalty $\lambda$ was set to 10, and $\gamma$ for manifold alignment was tuned via grid search in the range $\{0.1, 1.0, 5.0\}$.

#### 6.2. Results
Table 1 displays the downstream classification accuracy (F1-score) on a hold-out test set using synthetic data for training.

| Model | Adult (F1) | Credit Card (F1) | Loan Default (F1) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| SMOTE | 0.72 | 0.68 | 0.65 |
| Vanilla GAN | 0.69 | 0.64 | 0.62 |
| WGAN-GP | 0.76 | 0.73 | 0.70 |
| CTGAN | 0.81 | 0.78 | 0.75 |
| TVAE | 0.82 | 0.79 | 0.76 |
| **CMA-WGAN** | **0.85** | **0.82** | **0.79** |

Table 2 shows the Jensen-Shannon Divergence (JSD) between the real and synthetic marginal distributions, where lower is better.

| Model | Adult (JSD) | Credit Card (JSD) | Loan Default (JSD) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Vanilla GAN | 0.15 | 0.18 | 0.21 |
| WGAN-GP | 0.11 | 0.12 | 0.14 |
| CTGAN | 0.08 | 0.09 | 0.11 |
| **CMA-WGAN** | **0.05** | **0.06** | **0.07** |

### 7. Ablation Study
To evaluate the contribution of the Manifold Alignment Module (MAM) and the Wasserstein objective, we performed an ablation study on the Adult dataset.

| Configuration | F1-Score | JSD |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| WGAN-GP (Baseline) | 0.76 | 0.11 |
| WGAN-GP + MAM | 0.83 | 0.07 |

The results indicate that the MAM contributes the most significant performance gain, confirming its role in preserving structural fidelity.

### 8. Discussion
The empirical results demonstrate that CMA-WGAN effectively addresses the limitations of standard GANs in tabular contexts. By enforcing manifold alignment, the model ensures that the generated records not only satisfy the marginal distribution requirements but also preserve the conditional dependencies between features. This is particularly evident in the F1-score improvement, which suggests that downstream classifiers can better learn decision boundaries when trained on CMA-WGAN data.

The computational complexity of the proposed method is $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ due to the pair-wise similarity calculation in the MAM. While this is more expensive than standard GAN training, the use of mini-batch sampling for the manifold loss renders the training time manageable. Future iterations could employ Nyström approximation or sparse kernel methods to reduce this complexity to $\mathcal{O}(N \log N)$.

We observed that the choice of the similarity function in the MAM is crucial. While we utilized a Gaussian RBF kernel, we hypothesize that learnable similarity metrics, such as those derived from a Siamese network, could further improve performance.

---

### 9. Limitations and Future Work
Despite the performance gains, CMA-WGAN has several limitations. First, the model assumes that the tabular data can be represented as a continuous manifold, which may not hold for highly categorical or sparse datasets with limited variety. Second, the hyperparameter $\gamma$ requires careful tuning; an overly large $\gamma$ can lead to "over-fitting" the training distribution, potentially reducing the diversity of the generated samples.

Future work will focus on integrating a variational objective to explicitly model the latent density of the categorical variables. Additionally, we plan to investigate the robustness of CMA-WGAN against adversarial attacks, as synthetic data generation can sometimes inadvertently replicate sensitive information from the training set [19, 20].

Finally, we intend to extend the framework to handle time-series tabular data, where the temporal dependency adds an additional layer of complexity to the manifold preservation objective.

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we proposed CMA-WGAN, a novel framework for tabular data augmentation using conditional Wasserstein GANs with manifold alignment. Our theoretical and empirical results confirm that explicitly aligning the latent manifolds of real and synthetic data leads to higher fidelity and improved downstream utility. We believe this approach provides a robust foundation for future research in privacy-preserving data synthesis and generative modeling for structured datasets.
