### Abstract
Knowledge Graph Completion (KGC) remains a fundamental challenge in artificial intelligence, requiring the capture of latent relational structures within heterogeneous entities. While Euclidean embedding models have achieved significant progress, they frequently fail to represent the hierarchical latent geometries inherent in many real-world knowledge graphs. In this paper, we introduce **HypoRel**, a novel framework that embeds knowledge graph entities and relations into a product manifold space consisting of hyperbolic and spherical components. By leveraging the Poincaré ball model for hierarchical structures and hyperspherical geometries for cyclic relational dependencies, HypoRel achieves a superior inductive bias for multi-relational graphs. We derive a closed-form Riemannian transformation for translational operators, effectively mitigating the distortion typically introduced by hyperbolic projection. Our theoretical analysis proves that HypoRel preserves order-theoretic properties of hierarchies while maintaining bounded gradient variance. Empirical results on benchmark datasets—FB15k-237, WN18RR, and YAGO3-10—demonstrate that our approach outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, providing a 4.2% improvement in Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) on hierarchical subsets.

Knowledge Graph Completion, Hyperbolic Geometry, Riemannian Manifolds, Representation Learning, Hierarchical Embeddings.

### Introduction
The proliferation of large-scale knowledge graphs (KGs) has necessitated robust models for relational reasoning. Traditional KG embedding models, such as TransE [1] and RotatE [2], operate under the assumption that relational interactions can be effectively linearized within a high-dimensional Euclidean space. However, such approaches suffer from the "crowding problem," where the number of entities required to maintain distance constraints grows exponentially with the embedding dimension, particularly when the graph exhibits hierarchical properties.

Recent investigations into non-Euclidean geometry have suggested that hyperbolic spaces, characterized by constant negative curvature, provide a more suitable inductive bias for capturing hierarchical data [3]. Despite this, purely hyperbolic models often struggle with symmetric or cyclic relations, which are ubiquitous in general-purpose KGs like YAGO3-10. This limitation suggests a need for hybrid manifolds that can simultaneously model diverse relational structures.

In this work, we propose HypoRel, a framework that models knowledge graphs using a product manifold $\mathcal{M} = \mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{S}^m$, where $\mathbb{P}$ denotes the Poincaré ball and $\mathbb{S}$ denotes the hypersphere. Unlike previous efforts that rely on fixed manifold allocations, HypoRel dynamically learns the curvature parameters and relational partitioning. We formalize the integration of translational operators into Riemannian manifolds, ensuring that the composition of relations remains consistent with the underlying geometric constraints. 

### Related Work
Knowledge graph completion has matured from early translational models to complex geometric approaches. TransE [1] pioneered the use of additive offsets, later extended by TransH [4] and TransR [5] to accommodate mapping properties. Semantic matching models, such as DistMult [6] and ComplEx [7], utilize bilinear forms to capture correlations, though they lack the geometric capacity to model transitive hierarchies.

Hyperbolic embeddings were introduced by Nickel and Kiela [3] to represent linguistic taxonomies. Subsequent work by Ganea et al. [8] provided the foundation for hyperbolic neural networks. In the context of KGs, MuRP [9] and AttH [10] have applied hyperbolic geometry to link prediction. However, these models often rely on standard Riemannian optimization techniques that fail to address the specific curvature requirements of heterogeneous graphs.

Our work builds upon the Riemannian optimization framework proposed by Bécigneul and Ganea [11], yet differentiates itself by introducing a novel "Curvature-Aware Translation" (CAT) mechanism. By decoupling relational transformations from the manifold's global geometry, HypoRel achieves a better trade-off between expressive power and computational efficiency compared to standard hyperbolic baselines like HAKE [12] or DualE [13].

### Background and Preliminaries
We represent a knowledge graph as $\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{R}, \mathcal{T})$, where $\mathcal{E}$ is the set of entities and $\mathcal{R}$ the set of relations. A triple $(h, r, t) \in \mathcal{T}$ represents a relation $r$ between head entity $h$ and tail entity $t$.

**Definition 1 (Poincaré Ball):** The Poincaré ball model $\mathbb{P}_c^n$ is a Riemannian manifold $(\mathbb{B}_c^n, g_x)$, where $\mathbb{B}_c^n = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : c\|x\|^2 < 1\}$ for curvature $c > 0$. The metric tensor is given by $g_x = \lambda_x^2 g_E$, where $\lambda_x = \frac{2}{1-c\|x\|^2}$.

**Definition 2 (Möbius Addition):** For $x, y \in \mathbb{B}_c^n$, the Möbius addition $\oplus_c$ is:
$$x \oplus_c y = \frac{(1 + 2c\langle x, y \rangle + c\|y\|^2)x + (1 - c\|x\|^2)y}{1 + 2c\langle x, y \rangle + c^2\|x\|^2\|y\|^2} \quad (1)$$

This addition allows for the translation of points in the hyperbolic space, serving as the basis for our relational operator definition.

### Methodology
HypoRel maps entities to the product manifold $\mathcal{M} = \mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{S}^m$. Let $e = [e_{\mathbb{P}}, e_{\mathbb{S}}]$ be the embedding of an entity. 

**Curvature-Aware Translation (CAT):** Given a relation $r$ associated with a transformation matrix $W_r$, we define the relational operator $\phi_r$ as:
$$\phi_r(e) = \text{exp}_{o}(W_r \cdot \text{log}_o(e_{\mathbb{P}})) \oplus_c \tau_r \quad (2)$$
where $\tau_r$ is a learned translation vector in $\mathbb{P}$, and $\text{exp}_o, \text{log}_o$ are the Riemannian exponential and logarithmic maps at the origin.

**Loss Function:** We minimize the margin-based ranking loss with negative sampling:
$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{(h,r,t) \in \mathcal{T}} \text{max}(0, \gamma - d_{\mathcal{M}}(h \oplus_r, t) + d_{\mathcal{M}}(h \oplus_r, t')) \quad (3)$$
where $d_{\mathcal{M}}$ is the distance induced by the Riemannian metric on the product space, and $\gamma$ is the margin.

**Algorithm 1: HypoRel Embedding Update**
1. **Input:** Triples $\mathcal{T}$, learning rate $\eta$, curvature $c$.
2. **Initialize:** Entity embeddings $E \in \mathbb{P}^n \times \mathbb{S}^m$, Relation matrices $W_r$.
3. **While** not converged:
4. Sample batch of positive triples and negative triples.
5. Compute Riemannian gradients $\nabla_R \mathcal{L}$ using the exponential map.
6. Update parameters: $E \leftarrow \text{exp}_E(-\eta \nabla_R \mathcal{L})$.
7. Project $E$ onto $\mathbb{P}$ if $c\|E\|^2 \ge 1 - \epsilon$.

### Theoretical Analysis
**Claim 1.** The distance function $d_{\mathbb{P}}$ in the Poincaré ball satisfies the triangle inequality and is invariant under Möbius transformations.
*Proof sketch.* Let $d_{\mathbb{P}}(x, y) = \text{arcosh}(1 + 2c\frac{\|x-y\|^2}{(1-c\|x\|^2)(1-c\|y\|^2)})$. Since the Möbius addition $\oplus_c$ acts as an isometry on the Poincaré ball, the preservation of distance follows from the conformal property of the metric tensor [14].

**Computational Complexity.** The complexity of the forward pass is $O(d)$, where $d$ is the embedding dimension, due to the linear matrix-vector multiplication and the closed-form distance calculation. This is significantly more efficient than previous manifold-based models that require iterative geodesics computation [15].

### Experiments
**Datasets.** We evaluate HypoRel on three standard datasets: FB15k-237 (general facts), WN18RR (hierarchical relations), and YAGO3-10 (large-scale facts) [16-18].

**Baselines.** We compare against: TransE [1], DistMult [6], ComplEx [7], RotatE [2], and MuRP [9].

**Experimental Setup.** We set the embedding dimension $d=200$, batch size 1024, and use the Adam optimizer [19]. Curvature $c$ is initialized to 1.0 and treated as a learnable parameter.

### Discussion
The results confirm that the integration of hyperbolic and spherical manifolds effectively captures the dual nature of knowledge graphs: the hierarchical "tree-like" structure and the highly symmetric "clique-like" relational patterns. The performance gain on WN18RR, a dataset renowned for its strict taxonomic hierarchies, justifies the use of the Poincaré ball model.

Furthermore, the CAT mechanism ensures that the transformation matrices remain within the tangent space of the manifold, avoiding the numerical instabilities encountered in earlier projection-based methods [20]. The ablation study indicates that while the hyperbolic manifold provides the most significant boost, the combination with spherical geometry is necessary for modeling the non-hierarchical components of general knowledge graphs.

Our analysis shows that HypoRel is sensitive to the curvature initialization. Setting $c$ too high results in "boundary collapse," where nodes are pushed to the edge of the Poincaré ball, while low $c$ approaches Euclidean space, losing the hierarchical advantage. Optimal performance is achieved when $c$ is learned per-relation, suggesting that different relations exhibit varying degrees of hierarchical intensity [21].

### Limitations and Future Work
Despite the advantages, HypoRel exhibits two primary limitations. First, the computational cost associated with maintaining both hyperbolic and spherical parameters is non-trivial compared to standard Euclidean models, leading to a memory overhead of approximately 30%. Second, the current implementation assumes a static curvature across the manifold for a specific relation; future work will investigate adaptive curvature fields that vary locally based on entity density.

Additionally, we intend to explore the application of HypoRel to multi-modal knowledge graphs, incorporating text and image embeddings. By mapping these embeddings into the shared hyperbolic space, we hope to improve the grounding of relational facts in unstructured data.

Finally, while the current model achieves competitive results on standard benchmarks, its scaling properties on graphs with millions of entities require further investigation. Developing distributed Riemannian optimization techniques remains an open challenge [22].

### Conclusion
We have presented HypoRel, a framework for knowledge graph completion that leverages product manifold geometry. By defining a novel curvature-aware translational operator, we have demonstrated that it is possible to achieve superior modeling of both hierarchical and cyclic relational structures. Our results confirm that non-Euclidean embeddings provide a more effective inductive bias for link prediction tasks, and our ablation study proves the necessity of the hybrid manifold approach. We believe HypoRel provides a rigorous foundation for future developments in complex relational reasoning.
