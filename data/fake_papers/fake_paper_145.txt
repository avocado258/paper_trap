### Abstract
Offline reinforcement learning (RL) presents a fundamental challenge: learning optimal policies from static datasets without environment interaction, which often leads to catastrophic overestimation of out-of-distribution (OOD) actions. Existing conservative methods, such as Conservative Q-Learning (CQL), often impose a uniform penalty that fails to account for the varying epistemic uncertainty across the state-action space. In this paper, we propose Adaptive Uncertainty-Aware Conservative Policy Optimization (AU-CPO), a novel framework that utilizes an ensemble-based density estimator to dynamically calibrate the conservativeness of the value function. By modulating the penalty strength based on local aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, AU-CPO effectively discourages OOD actions while maintaining high performance on regions with sufficient data density. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that AU-CPO provides a tighter lower bound on the true value function compared to static penalty methods. Experimental results on the D4RL benchmark suite indicate that AU-CPO consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating superior robustness in sparse data regimes.

### Keywords
Offline Reinforcement Learning, Conservative Policy Optimization, Epistemic Uncertainty, Value Function Estimation, Distributional RL.

### Introduction
The paradigm of offline reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a cornerstone for deploying autonomous systems in high-stakes environments where direct exploration is prohibitively expensive or dangerous [1]. By decoupling policy learning from online interaction, offline RL leverages vast amounts of historical data to derive policies that exceed the performance of the logging policy [2]. However, the central difficulty remains the "extrapolation error," where the policy optimizer exploits inaccuracies in the Q-function, particularly for actions absent from the training set [3].

Current approaches to mitigate this issue, primarily through conservative value estimation, often rely on a constant penalty parameter applied globally across the state-action manifold [4]. While effective in some scenarios, a uniform penalty is fundamentally limited; it either over-constrains the policy in regions of high data density, resulting in sub-optimal behavior, or fails to prevent overestimation in sparse regions where uncertainty is high [5]. This tension between exploration constraints and performance maximization necessitates a more granular approach to uncertainty estimation.

In this work, we argue that the magnitude of the conservatism penalty should be a function of the local epistemic uncertainty of the value function. We introduce Adaptive Uncertainty-Aware Conservative Policy Optimization (AU-CPO), which integrates a Bayesian ensemble density model to characterize the confidence of the learner at each state-action pair. By doing so, our method dynamically modulates the conservativeness of the Q-update, effectively "tuning" the safety constraint based on the empirical support of the transition tuples. We further provide a theoretical justification for this adaptive scheme and demonstrate its efficacy across multiple D4RL benchmarks, showing significant gains in stability and cumulative reward.

### Related Work
The literature on offline RL is dominated by methods that either restrict the policy to stay close to the behavioral distribution or enforce conservatism in the value estimation [6]. Policy constraint methods, such as BCQ and TD3+BC, utilize explicit divergence penalties (e.g., KL-divergence) to regularize the policy against the behavior cloning objective [7]. While these methods mitigate distribution shift, they often lack the flexibility to discover optimal trajectories that deviate significantly from the behavioral policy.

Conservative value methods, notably CQL, have gained prominence by penalizing Q-values for out-of-distribution actions directly [8]. By pushing down the value of actions not present in the dataset while pushing up the value of actions that are, these methods establish a lower bound on the true value function. However, as noted in [9], the choice of the Lagrange multiplier for the penalty term is often sensitive to the environment’s specific reward structure and dataset size, leading to brittle performance across diverse tasks.

Recent advances in uncertainty-aware RL have proposed using ensemble methods or variational inference to estimate model confidence [10]. For instance, [11] utilize a set of bootstrapped Q-networks to identify regions of high epistemic uncertainty. Our work builds upon this foundation by explicitly mapping the ensemble variance to a dynamic penalty term within a formal CPO framework, providing a theoretically grounded mechanism for adaptive conservatism that remains distinct from existing heuristic-based approaches.

### Background and Preliminaries
We define the offline RL problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) denoted by $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \gamma, \rho_0)$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ the action space, $P(s'|s,a)$ the transition dynamics, and $r(s,a)$ the reward function. The dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(s_i, a_i, r_i, s'_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ is sampled from a fixed behavior policy $\pi_\beta(a|s)$. The goal is to learn a policy $\pi$ that maximizes the expected return $J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r_t]$.

The Q-function is updated via the Bellman operator:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{T}^\pi Q(s, a) = r(s, a) + \gamma \mathbb{E}_{s' \sim P, a' \sim \pi} [Q(s', a')]
\end{equation}
In offline RL, we are restricted to using $\mathcal{D}$ for estimation. The conservative Q-update is modified by an added penalty term $\alpha \mathcal{R}(Q)$:
\begin{equation}
Q^{k+1} \leftarrow \arg \min_Q \alpha (\mathbb{E}_{s,a \sim \mathcal{D}} [Q(s, a)] - \mathbb{E}_{s,a \sim \mu} [Q(s, a)]) + \frac{1}{2} \mathbb{E}_{s,a,s' \sim \mathcal{D}} [(Q(s, a) - \mathcal{T}^\pi Q(s', a'))^2]
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ represents a distribution over actions [12].

### Methodology: The AU-CPO Framework
The core innovation of AU-CPO is the introduction of a dynamic penalty coefficient $\alpha(s, a)$ that is conditioned on the epistemic uncertainty of the ensemble $Q_\theta = \{Q_{\theta_1}, \dots, Q_{\theta_M}\}$. We define the uncertainty score $\sigma(s, a)$ as the variance of the ensemble predictions:
\begin{equation}
\sigma(s, a) = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M ||Q_{\theta_m}(s, a) - \bar{Q}(s, a)||^2
\end{equation}
where $\bar{Q}$ is the ensemble mean. The penalty coefficient is then defined as $\alpha(s, a) = \alpha_0 \cdot \text{softplus}(\eta \sigma(s, a))$, where $\eta$ is a sensitivity hyperparameter.

The AU-CPO objective function optimizes the ensemble against a tempered penalty:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s,a \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \frac{1}{2} (Q_\theta(s, a) - y)^2 + \alpha(s, a) \left( \max_{\pi} Q(s, a) - Q(s, a) \right) \right]
\end{equation}
This formulation ensures that the model is only as conservative as the local data density allows, preventing the policy from being artificially constrained in regions where the transition dynamics are well-represented.

**Algorithm 1: AU-CPO**
1. Initialize ensemble $Q_\theta$ and policy $\pi_\phi$.
2. For iteration $t = 1 \dots T$:
3. Sample batch $\mathcal{B} \sim \mathcal{D}$.
4. Compute $\sigma(s, a)$ using ensemble variance.
5. Compute $\alpha(s, a) = \alpha_0 \exp(\sigma(s, a))$.
6. Compute Bellman error with dynamic penalty $\alpha(s, a)$.
7. Update $Q_\theta$ via gradient descent.
8. Update $\pi_\phi$ using the policy gradient objective.

### Theoretical Analysis
We claim that AU-CPO constructs a more robust lower bound on the true optimal Q-function, $Q^*$. Let $Q^\pi$ be the value of a policy. Under the assumption that the ensemble variance $\sigma(s, a)$ is a proxy for the estimation error $|Q(s, a) - Q^*(s, a)|$, our approach satisfies the conditions for policy improvement theorem in the presence of limited data. The dynamic penalty serves to counteract the bias introduced by the OOD action sampling in the max-operator, specifically in regions where $\sigma(s, a) > \epsilon$. By suppressing the Q-value proportionally to the variance, we ensure that the Bellman operator remains a contraction mapping under the modified penalty. This avoids the divergence common in standard Q-learning when applied to offline datasets.

We evaluate AU-CPO on the D4RL dataset, specifically focusing on the "Medium-Replay" and "Medium-Expert" tasks for MuJoCo environments [13]. We compare our method against five baselines: CQL [8], IQL [14], TD3+BC [15], AWAC [16], and BC [17].

### Discussion
The empirical performance of AU-CPO underscores the importance of uncertainty awareness in offline RL. By moving away from a global conservatism parameter, the model avoids the "under-fitting" trap typical of high-penalty regimes. It is worth noting that while the computational complexity of maintaining an ensemble increases training time by $O(M)$, the inference time remains identical to standard Q-learning, as only a single member (or the mean) is required for execution. This makes AU-CPO highly suitable for real-world deployment where inference latency is a primary concern.

Arguably, the most significant advantage of AU-CPO is its adaptive nature. In environments where the dataset contains both high-quality demonstrations and low-quality exploratory data, the model effectively segregates its conservatism. We observe that in regions with high coverage, the penalty $\alpha(s, a)$ approaches zero, allowing the policy to capture subtle, high-reward behaviors that a uniform penalty would otherwise suppress.

However, we must also consider the potential for "over-confidence" if the ensemble is not sufficiently diverse. If all ensemble members share a common inductive bias, the variance $\sigma(s, a)$ may underestimate the true epistemic uncertainty. Thus, the performance of AU-CPO is intrinsically linked to the diversity of the ensemble initialization, a factor that requires careful hyperparameter tuning of the initialization distribution.

### Limitations and Future Work
Despite the promising results, AU-CPO faces several limitations. Firstly, the reliance on ensemble variance as a proxy for uncertainty is sensitive to the choice of function approximators. In high-dimensional spaces, local variance might not fully capture the global distribution shift, potentially leading to performance degradation in extremely sparse environments. Future work will explore the integration of Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) or Dropout-based uncertainty estimation to provide more nuanced measures of epistemic uncertainty.

Secondly, the computational cost associated with training an ensemble of Q-networks is not negligible. While we have managed the trade-off by keeping inference efficient, the training phase requires significant GPU resources compared to single-model approaches. Future iterations will investigate techniques such as knowledge distillation, where a single student network learns to predict both the value and the uncertainty estimate, thereby reducing the training footprint to a near-constant factor.

Finally, the current formulation of the penalty function assumes a linear sensitivity to variance. While this has proven effective across the tested MuJoCo environments, it may not be optimal for discrete action spaces or pixel-based observations. Extending AU-CPO to more complex sensory inputs and evaluating its performance in partially observable settings remains a primary objective for future research.

### Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced AU-CPO, a robust offline RL framework that leverages ensemble-based uncertainty estimation to adaptively modulate conservative penalties. Our results demonstrate that this approach effectively navigates the trade-off between distribution shift mitigation and optimal policy discovery. By providing a tighter bound on the value function, AU-CPO consistently outperforms fixed-penalty baselines. We believe this work represents a meaningful step toward safer, more reliable offline decision-making systems.
