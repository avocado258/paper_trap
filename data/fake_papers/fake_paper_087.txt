# Entropic-Regularized Variational Quantum Circuits for Binary Classification on NISQ Devices

**Authors:**
Elena V. Sterling, Department of Physics and Quantum Engineering, Institute of Advanced Computing;
Marcus R. Thorne, AI Research Lab, Silicon Valley Institute of Technology;
Julianne F. Moreau, Laboratory for Quantum Information Science, University of Zurich.

### Abstract
The advent of Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices has necessitated the development of computationally efficient and noise-resilient variational quantum algorithms. We introduce Entropic-Regularized Quantum Variational Circuits (ER-QVC), a novel framework that incorporates a Von Neumann entropy penalty term into the objective function to mitigate the effects of barren plateaus and over-parameterization in high-dimensional Hilbert spaces. By promoting parameter distributions that yield lower circuit complexity, our approach enhances the trainability of circuits in the presence of dephasing noise. We demonstrate, through comprehensive empirical evaluations on the Iris and Breast Cancer Wisconsin datasets, that our method achieves superior classification accuracy compared to standard Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQC) and Quantum Neural Networks (QNN). Our theoretical analysis confirms that the inclusion of the entropic regularizer effectively constraints the optimization landscape, leading to a faster convergence rate of $O(\epsilon^{-1})$ in gradient descent iterations. This research underscores the potential of hybrid quantum-classical architectures in overcoming the inherent limitations of current quantum hardware.

### Keywords
Quantum Machine Learning, Variational Quantum Circuits, NISQ, Von Neumann Entropy, Binary Classification, Barren Plateaus.

### Introduction
The emergence of quantum computing paradigms has catalyzed a paradigm shift in machine learning, offering the potential to transcend classical computational bottlenecks through the exploitation of quantum entanglement and superposition [1]. Within the NISQ era, Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) represent the most viable pathway toward practical quantum advantage, utilizing hybrid quantum-classical optimization loops to approximate target functions [2]. However, these circuits are fundamentally constrained by decoherence, gate fidelity limitations, and the phenomenon of barren plateaus—where gradient information vanishes exponentially as the number of qubits increases [3].

Current literature suggests that the optimization landscape of high-dimensional quantum circuits is often ill-conditioned, leading to sub-optimal convergence and reliance on massive datasets to overcome noise-induced bias [4]. While classical neural networks have benefited significantly from regularization techniques such as $L_1/L_2$ norms and dropout, the mapping of such techniques onto quantum architectures remains an open problem due to the non-commutative nature of quantum operators and the unitary constraints of quantum evolution [5].

In this paper, we propose Entropic-Regularized Variational Quantum Circuits (ER-QVC), a framework designed to regularize the quantum information geometry of the circuit. By introducing an entropic penalty that penalizes excessive spreading of the density matrix, we restrict the search space to regions of the Hilbert space that are inherently more robust to local noise models. This paper provides a rigorous mathematical foundation for the implementation of this regularizer, offers a complexity analysis of the proposed training objective, and provides experimental validation against five baseline architectures on standard benchmark datasets.

### Related Work
The landscape of Variational Quantum Classifiers (VQC) has evolved rapidly since the seminal work of Havlíček et al. [6], who established the foundational framework for kernel-based quantum classification. Subsequent research by Schuld et al. [7] elucidated the connection between quantum circuits and classical kernels, demonstrating that VQCs can implicitly map data into high-dimensional feature spaces. Despite these advancements, the issue of trainability remains a critical bottleneck.

McClean et al. [8] characterized the barren plateau problem, identifying the exponential concentration of gradients in randomly initialized circuits as a primary hurdle for deep VQAs. Various strategies have been proposed to circumvent this, including layer-wise training [9], parameter initialization strategies [10], and the introduction of local cost functions [11]. However, these methods often necessitate structural changes to the circuit topology, which may increase the gate count and exacerbate hardware-level decoherence.

Entropy-based regularizers have seen limited exploration in quantum settings. Previous attempts at leveraging quantum entropy primarily focused on state preparation and entanglement distillation [12]. Recent works by Cerezo et al. [13] have suggested that cost functions that measure entanglement may serve as proxies for state complexity, yet these approaches often require heavy computational overhead. Our work diverges by proposing an efficient estimation of the Von Neumann entropy as an auxiliary loss term that can be computed through shadow tomography [14].

### Background / Preliminaries
We consider a binary classification task where inputs $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are mapped to labels $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$. In a quantum setting, we define a feature map $\phi(x)$ that encodes classical data into a quantum state $|\psi(x)\rangle = U_{\phi}(x)|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$. The variational classifier is defined by a parameterized unitary $V(\theta)$, where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^m$. The final state is:
$$|\psi(x, \theta)\rangle = V(\theta)U_{\phi}(x)|0\rangle^{\otimes n}$$
The prediction $f(x, \theta)$ is obtained by measuring an observable $\hat{M}$ such that:
$$f(x, \theta) = \text{Tr}(\hat{M} \rho(x, \theta))$$
where $\rho(x, \theta) = |\psi(x, \theta)\rangle\langle\psi(x, \theta)|$ is the density matrix of the system [15]. For binary classification, we apply a sigmoid activation function $\sigma(\cdot)$ over the expectation value, yielding the predicted probability $p(y=1|x, \theta) = \sigma(f(x, \theta))$. The standard empirical risk minimization involves minimizing the loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_i \ell(y_i, f(x_i, \theta))$.

### Proposed Approach: Entropic-Regularized VQC
To address the instability of the training process, we define the ER-QVC loss function as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{ER}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{class}(\theta) + \lambda \cdot S(\rho(\theta))$$
where $S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)$ is the Von Neumann entropy of the system, and $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter controlling the regularization strength [16]. The inclusion of the entropy term effectively penalizes configurations where the circuit entangles too many qubits in a chaotic, noise-sensitive manner, forcing the optimizer toward states with higher degrees of purity or structural stability [17].

**Algorithm 1:** Entropic-Regularized Variational Training
1. **Input:** Dataset $\{(x_i, y_i)\}$, Circuit $V(\theta)$, Hyperparameter $\lambda$.
2. **Initialize:** $\theta^{(0)}$ using Glorot initialization for quantum parameters.
3. **While** not converged:
    a. Sample mini-batch $\{x_B, y_B\}$.
    b. Compute gradients of $\mathcal{L}_{class}$ using parameter shift rules.
    c. Estimate $S(\rho)$ via shadow tomography (or local trace estimation).
    d. Update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{ER}$.
4. **Return:** $\theta^*$.

The gradient of the entropy term $\nabla_\theta S(\rho)$ can be efficiently estimated using the property that $\nabla_\theta S(\rho) = -\text{Tr}((\log \rho + I) \nabla_\theta \rho)$. In practice, we utilize a second-order approximation based on the purity $\text{Tr}(\rho^2)$ to reduce the sampling complexity from exponential to $O(k^2)$ where $k$ is the number of local observables [18].

### Theoretical Analysis
We posit that the inclusion of the entropic regularizer provides a stabilizing effect on the Hessian of the loss function, $\mathcal{H}_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}$. In standard QVCs, the Hessian often exhibits a wide distribution of eigenvalues, including many near zero, which is indicative of the barren plateau phenomenon [19]. 

**Theorem 1:** *Given a quantum circuit with sufficient depth, the ER-QVC objective function maintains a strictly positive-definite lower bound on the Hessian spectrum, $ \lambda_{min}(\mathcal{H}) \geq \gamma > 0$, provided $\lambda$ is chosen such that the state purity is constrained.* 

Proof sketch: By constraining the Von Neumann entropy, we effectively limit the dimensionality of the state space that can be reached by $V(\theta)$. Since the gradient magnitude is bounded by the variance of the observable, and entropy effectively bounds the local entanglement, the variance of the gradients $\text{Var}[\partial_\theta \mathcal{L}]$ is prevented from decaying exponentially with respect to the qubit count [20]. Consequently, the optimization landscape remains flatter, permitting faster convergence in $O(\epsilon^{-1})$ iterations, whereas standard VQCs converge at $O(\epsilon^{-2})$ [21].

### Experiments
We evaluate the ER-QVC model on the Iris and Breast Cancer Wisconsin datasets, pre-processed via principal component analysis to fit the quantum feature maps. We compare against:
1. Standard VQC (Vanilla).
2. Quantum Neural Network (QNN) with Layer-wise Training [22].
3. Support Vector Machine (SVM) with RBF kernel (classical baseline).
4. Quantum Kernel Estimator (QKE).
5. Randomized Benchmarking-Regularized QVC (RB-QVC).

**Table 1: Accuracy Comparison (%) on Benchmarked Datasets**

| Method | Iris (Acc) | Breast Cancer (Acc) | Training Time (s) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| SVM (Classic) | 96.2 | 94.8 | 0.4 |
| VQC (Vanilla) | 88.5 | 82.1 | 124.5 |
| QNN (Layer-wise) | 91.2 | 86.4 | 158.2 |
| QKE | 93.5 | 89.2 | 210.3 |
| RB-QVC | 92.8 | 88.9 | 145.2 |
| **ER-QVC (Proposed)** | **95.1** | **92.3** | 138.7 |

The experimental results indicate that while ER-QVC does not outperform classical SVMs, it maintains significant quantum utility with accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art quantum approaches, while exhibiting improved robustness to noise in simulated hardware runs [23].

### Ablation Study
To isolate the contribution of the entropy regularization, we evaluate the ER-QVC performance across varying values of $\lambda$.

**Table 2: Ablation Study of Entropy Regularizer ($\lambda$)**

| Regularizer Strength ($\lambda$) | Train Acc | Val Acc | Gradient Variance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0.00 (No Reg) | 98.2 | 82.1 | $10^{-6}$ |
| 0.01 | 94.5 | 90.2 | $10^{-3}$ |
| 0.10 | 92.1 | 92.3 | $10^{-2}$ |
| 1.00 | 85.0 | 84.1 | $10^{-2}$ |

As observed, a moderate value of $\lambda = 0.10$ achieves the best trade-off between training accuracy and generalizability, suggesting that the entropic penalty effectively prevents overfitting to the noise inherent in the measurement outcomes [24].

### Discussion
The empirical success of the ER-QVC framework suggests that quantum information-theoretic quantities can provide a meaningful guide for the optimization of variational circuits. By penalizing high-entropy states, the model implicitly favors circuits that maintain structural coherence, which is essential for preserving the quantum features of the input data in the presence of environmental noise. It is worth noting that the computational cost of estimating entropy is not trivial; however, the reliance on shadow tomography allows for a tractable approximation that fits within the current capabilities of NISQ hardware [25].

Arguably, the most significant insight from our results is the observation that regularizing the Hilbert space geometry is more effective than attempting to improve gate fidelity alone. While physical hardware improvements are necessary, our results demonstrate that algorithmic regularization can bridge the gap in classification performance for current systems. We speculate that this technique could be extended to multi-class classification and quantum reinforcement learning, where the stability of the policy is equally sensitive to the underlying state representation.

Finally, one must consider the scalability of the proposed method. As the number of qubits increases, the estimation of the Von Neumann entropy may become computationally intensive. However, given that current NISQ devices are limited to fewer than 100 qubits, the proposed approximation remains well-within the threshold of feasibility for near-term applications.

### Limitations & Future Work
Despite the reported successes, several limitations merit discussion. First, the hyperparameter $\lambda$ currently requires manual tuning, which is non-trivial given the lack of validation data in pure quantum settings. Second, the reliance on second-order approximations for entropy limits the precision of the regularizer in highly entangled systems where the state representation deviates significantly from a product state.

Future work will focus on dynamic scheduling of $\lambda$ throughout the training process—akin to simulated annealing—to optimize convergence trajectories. Additionally, we intend to explore the integration of ER-QVC with error mitigation protocols, such as Zero-Noise Extrapolation (ZNE), to further insulate the variational parameters from decoherence [26]. Finally, extending this framework to fault-tolerant architectures where surface code error correction is available will be critical for assessing its long-term viability in large-scale quantum machine learning [27].

### Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced Entropic-Regularized Variational Quantum Circuits (ER-QVC) as a robust solution to the trainability and noise-sensitivity issues prevalent in NISQ-era quantum machine learning. By utilizing an entropy-based penalty, we effectively constrained the search space of the optimization landscape, leading to more stable and accurate binary classification results. Our theoretical and empirical findings validate that quantum information-theoretic metrics are potent tools for guiding the development of practical variational algorithms. We believe this research provides a constructive step toward achieving reliable quantum classification and offers a scalable framework for future quantum algorithmic design.
