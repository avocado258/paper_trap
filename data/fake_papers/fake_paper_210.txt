# Mitigating Demographic Bias in Large Language Models via Contrastive Fine-Tuning

### Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, yet they frequently exhibit persistent demographic biases rooted in their training corpora. While existing mitigation strategies, such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and adversarial debiasing, have shown promise, they often suffer from significant performance degradation on downstream tasks or fail to address intersectional bias. In this paper, we introduce **Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA)**, a novel fine-tuning framework that utilizes contrastive learning to minimize the distance between representations of identical semantic content across diverse demographic markers. By optimizing a tripartite loss function that balances semantic fidelity with demographic parity, our method effectively decouples sensitive attributes from latent representations. We evaluate CDA across several benchmark datasets, including CrowS-Pairs and StereoSet, demonstrating a significant reduction in bias metrics (up to 28% improvement in the Bias Score) while maintaining competitive performance on GLUE benchmarks. Our theoretical analysis provides bounds on the generalization error of the debiased model, suggesting that CDA achieves a superior trade-off between fairness and utility compared to traditional post-hoc mitigation techniques.

### Keywords
Large Language Models, Algorithmic Fairness, Contrastive Learning, Representation Learning, Demographic Bias, Natural Language Processing.

### Introduction
The proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) across critical societal sectors—ranging from automated hiring pipelines to clinical decision support systems—has brought the issue of algorithmic bias to the forefront of machine learning research [1], [2], [3]. Despite their impressive utility, LLMs often internalize and perpetuate deep-seated societal prejudices, manifesting as discriminatory output patterns relative to protected demographic attributes such as gender, race, and religion [4], [5]. These biases are not merely artifacts of noisy data; they are deeply entrenched in the high-dimensional latent space of the models, often resisting superficial mitigation attempts [6], [7].

Current approaches to mitigate bias, such as standard fine-tuning on curated datasets or the implementation of RLHF with fairness-oriented reward models, frequently encounter a "fairness-utility trade-off," where the reduction in bias comes at the direct expense of model perplexity and task-specific accuracy [8], [9], [10]. Furthermore, these methods often struggle to resolve intersectional biases, where the compounding of multiple protected attributes exacerbates the skewed output distributions [11], [12]. There is, consequently, a pressing need for a framework that can disentangle semantic intent from demographic signaling without compromising the generative capacity of the model.

In this work, we propose Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA), a methodology that leverages contrastive representation learning to enforce demographic invariance. By constructing pairs of semantically equivalent prompts that vary only in their demographic descriptors, we force the model to map these inputs to proximal points in the latent space. This approach effectively minimizes the mutual information between the model’s internal representations and sensitive demographic variables, thereby neutralizing bias at the architectural level [13], [14], [15]. We hypothesize that by imposing this structural constraint during the final stages of pre-training or fine-tuning, the model learns to prioritize semantic signal over demographic correlations, yielding a more robust and equitable system.

### Related Work
The literature on bias mitigation in NLP is extensive, ranging from word embedding debiasing to adversarial training [16], [17], [18]. Bolukbasi et al. [19] pioneered the concept of gender-subspace projection, showing that word embeddings could be geometrically transformed to remove gendered associations. However, these methods are largely inapplicable to contextualized representations in Transformer-based architectures, which require more sophisticated, non-linear interventions [20], [21]. 

Adversarial debiasing, as proposed by Zhang et al. [22] and Zhao et al. [23], involves training a discriminator to predict demographic attributes from model activations, while the primary model is optimized to maximize the discriminator's uncertainty. While conceptually sound, these adversarial regimes are notoriously difficult to stabilize and often lead to mode collapse or catastrophic forgetting of task-specific knowledge [24], [25]. Recent advancements in contrastive learning, particularly works by Chen et al. [26] and He et al. [27], have demonstrated the utility of pulling semantically similar representations together in the embedding space. Our work builds upon this foundation by extending contrastive learning to the domain of fairness, utilizing demographic-invariant pairs as anchors for representation alignment.

### Background and Preliminaries
Let $\mathcal{X}$ be the input space of text sequences, and $\mathcal{Y}$ be the output space. An LLM is defined by a parameter set $\theta$ that maps an input $x \in \mathcal{X}$ to a probability distribution $p_\theta(y|x)$. Let $A$ be a set of protected demographic attributes (e.g., gender, ethnicity). Our goal is to achieve demographic parity, such that the distribution of outputs $p_\theta(y|x)$ is independent of the attribute $a \in A$ for any given semantic context [28], [29].

We define a "demographic pair" as a tuple $(x_i, x_j)$ such that $x_i$ and $x_j$ are semantically equivalent but differ in their demographic representation (e.g., "The doctor, who is a man, ..." vs. "The doctor, who is a woman, ..."). The objective is to minimize the distance between the latent representations $f_\theta(x_i)$ and $f_\theta(x_j)$ in the hidden state space [30], [31].

### Proposed Approach: Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA)
The CDA framework introduces a novel loss function $\mathcal{L}_{CDA}$ that integrates a standard language modeling objective with a contrastive fairness constraint. We define the loss as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{CDA} = \mathcal{L}_{LM} + \lambda \sum_{(x_i, x_j) \in \mathcal{P}} \left( 1 - \text{sim}(f_\theta(x_i), f_\theta(x_j)) \right)$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{LM}$ is the cross-entropy loss, $\mathcal{P}$ is the set of contrastive pairs, $\text{sim}(\cdot)$ denotes cosine similarity, and $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter controlling the strength of the debiasing constraint [32], [33].

**Algorithm 1: CDA Fine-Tuning**
1. **Input:** Pre-trained model $M_\theta$, demographic dataset $\mathcal{D}$, pair generator $G$.
2. **Initialize:** Set $\lambda$, learning rate $\eta$, batch size $B$.
3. **For** each batch $B$ in $\mathcal{D}$:
    a. Generate semantic pairs $(x_i, x_j)$ using $G$.
    b. Compute $z_i = f_\theta(x_i)$ and $z_j = f_\theta(x_j)$.
    c. Calculate $\mathcal{L}_{LM}$ on batch inputs.
    d. Calculate $\mathcal{L}_{contrastive} = \text{dist}(z_i, z_j)$.
    e. Update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta (\mathcal{L}_{LM} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{contrastive})$.
4. **Return:** Debiased model $M_{\theta^*}$.

This approach is computationally efficient, exhibiting $O(N \cdot d^2)$ complexity where $N$ is sequence length and $d$ is embedding dimension, making it suitable for large-scale deployment [34], [35].

### Theoretical Analysis
The efficacy of CDA rests on the assumption that the demographic information is linearly separable in the latent space [36]. By minimizing the contrastive loss, we effectively perform a projection of the latent manifold into a subspace where the variance explained by $A$ is minimized. 

**Theorem 1:** *Given a sufficiently large set of contrastive pairs $\mathcal{P}$ that span the semantic space, the CDA objective function acts as a regularizer that bounds the mutual information $I(Z; A)$, where $Z$ is the hidden representation and $A$ is the demographic variable.*

Proof sketch: As $\lambda \to \infty$, the representations $f_\theta(x_i)$ and $f_\theta(x_j)$ converge to the same point, effectively rendering $I(Z; A)$ null. The trade-off is governed by the curvature of the loss surface near the global minimum of the language modeling objective [37], [38].

### Experiments
We evaluate our model on the CrowS-Pairs and StereoSet datasets. We utilize a GPT-2 Large base model and compare it against five baselines: (1) Standard fine-tuning, (2) Adversarial debiasing, (3) Counterfactual logit pairing, (4) RLHF with fairness reward, and (5) Null-space projection [39], [40], [41].

### Discussion
The results indicate that CDA achieves a robust reduction in demographic bias without the severe perplexity penalties associated with adversarial approaches. It is worth noting that the preservation of GLUE performance suggests that the model retains its core linguistic capabilities, implying that the contrastive constraint successfully isolates demographic signaling from task-relevant features [42], [43]. Arguably, this indicates that demographic bias in LLMs is largely a modular component of the latent space rather than an intrinsic property of the language distribution itself [44].

One may observe that the slight increase in perplexity is a necessary trade-off for the fairness gains achieved. While some may argue that any performance loss is unacceptable, we contend that the societal cost of biased model deployment far outweighs the marginal increase in cross-entropy loss [45], [46]. Future iterations could explore dynamic adjustment of $\lambda$ based on the sensitivity of the input context, potentially mitigating the perplexity increase even further [47], [48].

### Limitations and Future Work
Despite the promising results, CDA is limited by the availability of high-quality, balanced contrastive datasets. If the pair generation process $G$ introduces its own biases, the model may inadvertently learn to "re-bias" in favor of the generator's tendencies [49], [50]. Furthermore, our evaluation is limited to binary demographic categories; expanding the framework to intersectional and non-binary categories remains a significant challenge for future research [51], [52].

Future work will focus on integrating CDA into the pre-training stage rather than post-hoc fine-tuning, potentially preventing the internalization of bias altogether. Additionally, we plan to investigate the robustness of these debiased representations against adversarial attacks specifically designed to extract demographic information [53], [54].

### Conclusion
In this study, we presented Contrastive Demographic Alignment, a novel methodology for mitigating demographic bias in LLMs. By leveraging contrastive learning to enforce demographic invariance, we achieved superior fairness metrics while maintaining high utility across downstream tasks. Our findings suggest that structural constraints on latent representations are a viable and effective path toward more ethical artificial intelligence. We hope this work serves as a foundational step toward the development of inherently fair generative models.
