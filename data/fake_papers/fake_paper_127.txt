# Recursive Chain-of-Thought Distillation: Enabling Efficient Multi-Hop Reasoning in Compact Language Models

**Elias V. Thorne, Sarah J. Halloway, and Marcus R. Chen**
*Department of Computer Science, Institute for Advanced Neural Systems*
*{e.thorne, s.halloway, m.chen}@ians.edu*

### Abstract
Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in multi-hop reasoning through Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting. However, the prohibitive computational costs associated with high-parameter models limit their deployment in latency-sensitive environments. In this paper, we propose Recursive Chain-of-Thought Distillation (R-CoTD), a novel framework that transfers complex reasoning trajectories from teacher models to compact student architectures. Unlike traditional distillation, which focuses on output distributions, R-CoTD optimizes the student’s latent reasoning path by aligning intermediate hidden states with structured reasoning graphs generated by the teacher. We introduce a "Reasoning-Constraint Loss" that penalizes deviations from the teacher's causal chain, effectively compressing the multi-hop reasoning process. Our empirical results on the HotpotQA and 2WikiMultiHopQA datasets indicate that R-CoTD achieves a 14% improvement in F1-score over standard distillation baselines while reducing inference latency by a factor of 8. We further provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence bounds of R-CoTD, demonstrating that the alignment of intermediate reasoning steps significantly reduces the sample complexity required for student convergence.

Neural Reasoning, Knowledge Distillation, Chain-of-Thought, Multi-hop QA, Model Compression

### Introduction
The emergence of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has fundamentally altered the landscape of natural language processing, enabling models to decompose complex queries into intermediate logical steps [1, 2]. While these methods have achieved state-of-the-art performance on multi-hop question answering (QA) tasks, they rely heavily on massive model architectures that are often infeasible for deployment [3, 4]. The discrepancy between the reasoning capacity of frontier models (e.g., 175B+ parameters) and the operational requirements of edge devices has necessitated the development of efficient knowledge distillation techniques [5, 6].

Traditional knowledge distillation (KD) typically minimizes the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the teacher’s and student’s output logits [7, 8]. While effective for classification, this paradigm often fails to capture the intricate logical dependencies inherent in multi-hop reasoning [9]. As noted by recent studies, merely matching final output distributions does not guarantee that a model has learned the underlying reasoning process, leading to "surface-form" correlations rather than causal understanding [10, 11]. It is therefore essential to move beyond outcome-based distillation and toward process-oriented knowledge transfer.

In this work, we introduce Recursive Chain-of-Thought Distillation (R-CoTD). Our approach addresses the fundamental bottleneck of neural reasoning: the structural gap between the teacher’s multi-step derivation and the student’s limited latent space. By treating the reasoning path as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), R-CoTD explicitly aligns intermediate hidden states using a novel contrastive alignment objective. This allows compact student models to emulate the teacher’s logical decomposition without the need for exhaustive parameter replication. We hypothesize that by constraining the student’s intermediate representations to resemble the teacher’s reasoning trajectory, we can recover the reasoning capabilities of larger models within smaller, more efficient architectures.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the literature on reasoning and distillation. Section 3 formalizes the R-CoTD framework. Section 4 presents our experimental methodology, while Section 5 details the empirical results and ablation studies. We conclude with a discussion on the broader implications of our findings for efficient AI.

### Related Work
The literature on multi-hop question answering has evolved from symbolic graph-based methods to dense neural architectures [12, 13]. Early approaches relied on knowledge graph retrieval to bridge the gap between disjoint entities [14, 15]. With the advent of transformer-based models, neural reasoning has shifted toward end-to-end architectures that perform implicit reasoning over large text corpora [16, 17]. However, these models often struggle with compositional generalization, failing to maintain logical consistency over long chains of inference [18, 19].

Chain-of-Thought prompting has emerged as a primary mechanism to mitigate these failures [20, 21]. By forcing models to generate intermediate steps, CoT facilitates better performance on tasks requiring deductive reasoning [22, 23]. Despite this, the computational overhead remains a significant hurdle [24, 25]. Recent efforts in model distillation, such as TinyBERT and DistilBERT, have successfully compressed language models, yet these methods often ignore the reasoning process entirely [26, 27]. Our work builds upon the foundation of "Reasoning Distillation," wherein the student is trained to mimic not just the output but the hidden states associated with internal reasoning [28, 29].

### Background and Preliminaries
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{(q_i, a_i, c_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ be a dataset of query-answer pairs, where $c_i$ denotes the context (or knowledge base) necessary for derivation. A teacher model $T$ generates a reasoning trajectory $Z_T = \{z_1, z_2, \dots, z_k\}$, where each $z_j$ represents an intermediate logical step. The student model $S$, with significantly fewer parameters $\theta_S \ll \theta_T$, must produce an output $y_S$ that maximizes the probability of the correct answer $a_i$.

We define the reasoning trajectory as a sequence of hidden state representations. The objective is to minimize the divergence between the student’s latent reasoning path $H_S = \{h_1, h_2, \dots, h_m\}$ and the teacher’s path $Z_T$. We assume a mapping function $\phi: \mathbb{R}^{d_T} \to \mathbb{R}^{d_S}$ to project teacher representations into the student's latent space, where $d_T$ and $d_S$ denote the hidden dimensions of the teacher and student, respectively.

### Methodology
The R-CoTD framework comprises two primary components: the Reasoning Path Alignment (RPA) and the Recursive Constraint Objective.

#### Reasoning Path Alignment (RPA)
We define the alignment loss between the teacher's reasoning steps and the student's hidden states as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{align} = \sum_{j=1}^{\min(k, m)} \text{MSE}(\phi(z_j), h_j) + \lambda \cdot \text{CosineDist}(z_j, h_j)$$
where $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter balancing Euclidean distance and directional alignment.

#### Recursive Constraint Objective
To ensure that the student maintains logical consistency, we introduce a recursive penalty that enforces the transition between consecutive reasoning steps:
$$\mathcal{L}_{recur} = \sum_{j=2}^m \| \mathcal{T}(h_j) - \mathcal{T}(h_{j-1}) - \Delta z_j \|^2$$
where $\mathcal{T}$ represents the transformer block transformation, and $\Delta z_j$ is the teacher’s latent shift between steps $j-1$ and $j$. The total loss function is defined as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{CE}(y_S, a_i) + \alpha \mathcal{L}_{align} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{recur}$$
where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ control the distillation intensity.

**Algorithm 1: Recursive Chain-of-Thought Distillation**
1. **Input:** Dataset $\mathcal{D}$, Teacher $T$, Student $S$.
2. **Precompute:** Generate reasoning paths $\{Z_T\}$ for all $q \in \mathcal{D}$ using $T$.
3. **Initialize:** Randomly initialize $\theta_S$ or use pre-trained weights.
4. **For** each batch $(q, a, c, Z_T)$ **do**:
   a. Forward pass $S(q, c) \to H_S$.
   b. Compute $\mathcal{L}_{align}$ between $H_S$ and $Z_T$ via $\phi$.
   c. Compute $\mathcal{L}_{recur}$ to enforce transition dynamics.
   d. Compute $\mathcal{L}_{CE}$ for answer generation.
   e. Backpropagate total loss $\mathcal{L}_{total}$ and update $\theta_S$.
5. **Return:** Trained student $S$.

The computational complexity of R-CoTD is $O(N \cdot L \cdot d^2)$, where $L$ is the sequence length and $d$ is the model dimension. The reliance on the precomputed teacher paths allows for amortized training time, as the expensive reasoning generation occurs only once.

### Theoretical Analysis
We posit that the alignment of intermediate states provides a tighter bound on the student's generalization error compared to standard output distillation. Given that the teacher's reasoning path acts as a regularization constraint, the hypothesis space for the student is effectively reduced.

*Theorem 1:* Let $\epsilon_T$ be the error of the teacher model. The generalization error $\epsilon_S$ of the student trained via R-CoTD is bounded by $\epsilon_S \leq \epsilon_T + \mathcal{O}(\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} \cdot \|\mathcal{L}_{align}\|)$. This suggests that as the student converges to the teacher's latent reasoning trajectory, the performance gap is primarily dictated by the capacity mismatch and the fidelity of the alignment mapping $\phi$.

### Experiments

#### Datasets and Setup
We evaluate our method on the **HotpotQA** (multi-hop) and **2WikiMultiHopQA** datasets. These datasets require complex reasoning over multiple documents to synthesize an answer. We use a LLaMA-2-70B model as the teacher and a LLaMA-2-7B model as the student. 

#### Results
Table 1 compares R-CoTD with standard baselines including DistilBERT [26], TinyBERT [27], and standard KD [7].

| Model | HotpotQA (F1) | 2WikiMultiHop (F1) | Latency (ms) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Teacher (70B) | 78.4 | 76.1 | 480 |
| DistilBERT | 52.3 | 49.8 | 45 |
| Standard KD | 58.7 | 56.2 | 48 |
| R-CoTD (Proposed) | 72.1 | 70.4 | 52 |

As shown in Table 1, R-CoTD consistently outperforms standard distillation by a significant margin. The modest increase in latency (52ms vs 48ms) is an acceptable trade-off for the ~14% F1 gain.

#### Ablation Study
Table 2 isolates the contribution of the alignment and recursive components.

| Ablation Component | HotpotQA (F1) |
| :--- | :--- |
| Baseline (CE only) | 58.7 |
| + $\mathcal{L}_{align}$ | 66.4 |
| + $\mathcal{L}_{recur}$ | 72.1 |

The results confirm that while alignment contributes to performance, the recursive constraint ($\mathcal{L}_{recur}$) is critical for maintaining the coherence of the reasoning process across hops.

### Discussion
The empirical success of R-CoTD underscores the importance of latent state trajectory in neural reasoning. It appears that the student model, when forced to follow the teacher’s intermediate representations, bypasses the "black-box" nature of standard distillation. By structuring the hidden states to mimic the teacher's deduction process, the student effectively learns a compressed logic graph, which generalizes better to unseen multi-hop queries.

It is worth noting that the choice of mapping function $\phi$ is non-trivial. In our experiments, we employed a simple linear projection; however, more sophisticated non-linear mappings could potentially capture more complex structural nuances. This remains an area of active exploration.

Furthermore, the stability of R-CoTD suggests that reasoning pathways are somewhat invariant to the specific parameterization of the model, provided that the architectural depth allows for sufficient expressive power. This observation has profound implications for model democratization, as it demonstrates that high-reasoning capability is not exclusively a function of scale.

### Limitations and Future Work
Despite the performance gains, R-CoTD is not without limitations. First, the dependency on a teacher model's precomputed reasoning paths necessitates high initial computational investment. Second, the framework currently assumes that the teacher and student share a similar tokenizer and vocabulary, which may not always be the case in heterogeneous model distillation scenarios.

Future research will focus on "self-distillation" frameworks where the model generates its own chain-of-thought, subsequently refining its reasoning path through recursive reinforcement. Additionally, we intend to investigate the cross-lingual generalization of these reasoning paths, assessing whether a reasoning graph learned on English corpora can be effectively transferred to low-resource languages.

### Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced Recursive Chain-of-Thought Distillation (R-CoTD), a novel approach to transferring reasoning capabilities from large teacher models to compact student architectures. By explicitly aligning intermediate reasoning trajectories via a recursive constraint, we significantly improved performance on multi-hop QA benchmarks. Our results validate the hypothesis that process-oriented distillation is a critical path forward for efficient, high-performance neural reasoning.
