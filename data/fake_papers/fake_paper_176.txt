### Abstract
The automated extraction of Adverse Drug Events (ADEs) from unstructured clinical narratives remains a formidable challenge in medical informatics due to the high syntactic variability and implicit contextual dependencies inherent in physician documentation. Existing Transformer-based architectures often struggle with long-range dependency resolution and the semantic ambiguity of negations within clinical discourse. In this paper, we propose *MedAlign-Transformer* (MAT), a novel framework that integrates a dynamic latent-structure alignment module with a standard Transformer backbone to explicitly model the relational mapping between drug entities, adverse symptoms, and clinical status markers. By employing a contrastive attention mechanism that aligns latent syntactic trees with semantic embeddings, MAT achieves superior performance in capturing complex multi-token events. We evaluate our approach on the N2C2 2018 ADE extraction dataset, demonstrating a significant improvement over state-of-the-art baselines. Our findings suggest that explicit structural alignment mitigates the tendency of attention mechanisms to over-attend to noise, thereby enhancing the precision of clinical event extraction in high-stakes healthcare settings.

**Keywords:** Adverse Drug Events, Transformer Architectures, Clinical Natural Language Processing, Latent-Structure Alignment, Electronic Health Records.

### 1. Introduction
The transition toward digitized healthcare systems has generated vast repositories of longitudinal clinical narratives, which represent a gold mine for pharmacovigilance and patient safety research. However, the linguistic complexity of these narratives—characterized by idiosyncratic abbreviations, nested negations, and ellipsis—renders conventional information extraction (IE) systems suboptimal. Specifically, the task of identifying Adverse Drug Events (ADEs), which requires the accurate linkage of a drug, a corresponding reaction, and the temporal evidence thereof, remains a primary bottleneck in automated clinical surveillance [1].

Current advancements in pre-trained language models (PLMs) have fundamentally altered the landscape of clinical NLP. Models such as BioBERT and ClinicalBERT have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in token-level classification tasks [2]. Notwithstanding these successes, the transition from token-level entity identification to relation extraction remains problematic. Standard attention mechanisms, while powerful for global context aggregation, frequently lack the inductive bias necessary to navigate the hierarchical syntactic structure of medical sentences. Consequently, relational dependencies are often lost amidst the noise of irrelevant clinical tokens [3].

In this work, we introduce *MedAlign-Transformer* (MAT), a novel architectural framework designed to bridge the gap between semantic token representation and structural relational modeling. Our approach leverages a dynamic latent-structure alignment mechanism that enforces a consistency constraint between the implicit attention distributions of the Transformer and an explicitly derived dependency parser. By minimizing a joint objective function, MAT encourages the model to allocate attention weight to tokens that share syntactic proximity, thereby improving the extraction of multi-token ADE entities. We posit that this structural guidance acts as a regularizer, forcing the model to ignore spurious correlations commonly found in lengthy, poorly structured clinical notes. The remainder of this paper details the methodology, theoretical underpinnings, and extensive experimental validation of the MAT framework.

---

### 2. Related Work
The identification of ADEs has evolved from rule-based systems to deep learning frameworks. Early approaches relied heavily on hand-crafted patterns and dictionary-based lookups [4]. While these methods provided high precision in controlled vocabularies, they suffered from poor recall when confronted with the descriptive, colloquial nature of clinician notes [5]. The introduction of Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) marked a significant paradigm shift, allowing for the modeling of label dependencies, yet these models remained bounded by the linear sequence constraint [6].

With the advent of the Transformer architecture, attention-based models replaced recurrent neural networks (RNNs) as the standard for clinical text mining [7]. Recent studies have focused on domain-adaptive pre-training, wherein models are further trained on large corpora of EHR data to capture domain-specific terminology [8]. Despite these gains, purely attention-based models struggle with "attention leakage," where the model assigns excessive weight to irrelevant tokens in the periphery of a sentence, leading to erroneous relation extraction [9].

Hybrid approaches that incorporate external knowledge graphs or linguistic constraints have been proposed to mitigate these issues [10]. For instance, recent architectures have sought to combine Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) with Transformers to encode syntactic dependencies [11]. While promising, these models often incur substantial computational overhead, limiting their deployment in real-time clinical dashboards. Our proposed MAT framework seeks to alleviate this trade-off by utilizing a lighter, latent-structure alignment objective that does not require an explicit graph propagation step during inference.

### 3. Preliminaries
Given a clinical document $D = \{w_1, w_2, \dots, w_n\}$, our goal is to identify a set of triplets $T = \{(e_d, e_r, s)\}$, where $e_d$ is a drug entity, $e_r$ is the associated adverse reaction, and $s$ denotes the clinical status. We define the Transformer hidden states for document $D$ as $H \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$. The self-attention matrix at layer $l$ is denoted by $A^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$.

We define the objective function as a multi-task optimization problem:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{ADE} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{align}$$
where $\mathcal{L}_{ADE}$ represents the standard Cross-Entropy loss for token classification, and $\mathcal{L}_{align}$ is the proposed alignment loss, which penalizes the divergence between the model’s attention weights and the syntactic dependency tree $S \in \{0, 1\}^{n \times n}$. The hyperparameter $\lambda$ controls the regularization strength. We assume the existence of a high-accuracy clinical dependency parser capable of generating $S$ as a pre-processing step [12].

### 4. Methodology: The MedAlign-Transformer
The MAT architecture comprises three primary components: the Transformer Encoder, the Latent-Structure Alignment (LSA) module, and the Relation Extraction Head.

#### 4.1 Latent-Structure Alignment Module
The core innovation of MAT is the LSA module. Rather than integrating a static syntactic tree, the LSA module computes a dynamic mapping between the hidden states $H$ and the dependency matrix $S$. We formulate the alignment loss as the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the attention matrix $A^{(l)}$ and the structure mask $S$:
$$\mathcal{L}_{align} = \sum_{i,j} S_{ij} \log \frac{S_{ij}}{A_{ij}^{(l)}} + (1 - S_{ij}) \log \frac{1 - S_{ij}}{1 - A_{ij}^{(l)}}$$
This formulation encourages the self-attention weights to reflect the underlying syntactic dependencies, ensuring that the model "attends" to semantically related entities even in the presence of distracting clinical tokens [13].

#### 4.2 Algorithm Pseudocode
The training procedure is summarized in Algorithm 1.

```python
Algorithm 1: Training MedAlign-Transformer
Input: Clinical text D, Labels Y, Dependency Mask S
Output: Trained Parameters theta
Initialize model with pre-trained BioClinicalBERT weights
For batch in TrainingData:
    H = TransformerEncoder(D)
    A = GetAttentionWeights(H)
    Predictions = RelationExtractionHead(H)
    L_ADE = CrossEntropy(Predictions, Y)
    L_align = KL_Divergence(A, S)
    Loss = L_ADE + lambda * L_align
    Backpropagate(Loss)
    Update(theta)
```

#### 4.3 Computational Complexity
The inclusion of the alignment module adds an $O(n^2)$ component to the training phase due to the comparison of the attention matrix with the dependency mask. However, since the dependency mask $S$ is computed once per document during pre-processing, the inference complexity remains $O(n^2)$, identical to the standard Transformer backbone. This renders MAT highly efficient for large-scale clinical deployment [14].

### 5. Experiments
#### 5.1 Datasets and Metrics
We evaluated MAT on the N2C2 2018 ADE extraction dataset, comprising 505 de-identified clinical records annotated for drug-reaction pairs [15]. Evaluation was conducted using micro-averaged Precision, Recall, and F1-score to account for class imbalance, as ADE mentions are relatively sparse compared to the total clinical vocabulary [16].

#### 5.2 Baselines
We compared MAT against the following state-of-the-art baselines:
1. **Bi-LSTM-CRF:** Standard sequence labeling baseline [17].
2. **ClinicalBERT:** Transformer-based base model [2].
3. **BioLinkBERT:** Document-level pre-trained Transformer [18].
4. **GCN-Transformer:** Explicit syntactic graph model [11].
5. **SpanBERT:** Span-oriented extraction model [19].

#### 5.3 Experimental Setup
All models were implemented in PyTorch and trained on NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Hyperparameters included a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$, weight decay of $0.01$, and a maximum sequence length of 512. We utilized the Stanza dependency parser to generate the initial structural masks for the alignment module [20].

The significant drop in F1-score when removing the alignment loss suggests that the structural guidance is the primary driver of performance gains. ### 8. Discussion
The empirical success of the MAT framework underscores the necessity of incorporating inductive biases into self-attention mechanisms when handling specialized text domains. In clinical narratives, the distance between a drug mention and its associated adverse reaction can often span several sentences, causing standard Transformer models to lose track of relational dependencies [22]. By constraining the attention mechanism to prioritize syntactic pathways, MAT effectively "shortcuts" the noise, allowing for the precise extraction of distant entities.

It is worth noting that the performance gains were most pronounced in instances involving complex, multi-clause sentences with embedded negations. Conventional models often misattribute symptoms to the wrong medication in these scenarios. MAT’s ability to leverage the dependency tree allows it to maintain the scope of the negation, thus reducing false positives. This finding aligns with recent theories suggesting that "syntax-aware" attention is a prerequisite for high-fidelity clinical NLP [23].

Furthermore, the integration of the LSA module provides a degree of interpretability that is frequently lacking in deep learning models. By analyzing the attention matrices, researchers can visualize which syntactic connections the model deems most significant. This transparency is critical in healthcare, where clinician trust in automated systems is mediated by the ability to verify the system's reasoning processes [24].

---

### 9. Limitations and Future Work
Despite the performance gains, several limitations merit discussion. First, the dependency on a pre-trained clinical parser introduces a bottleneck. If the parser encounters an error (e.g., misidentification of a medical abbreviation), the alignment module is provided with incorrect structural information, potentially leading to performance degradation [25]. Future work will investigate end-to-end learning of the structure mask, potentially utilizing a reinforcement learning paradigm to jointly optimize the structural parser and the extraction head.

Second, the current implementation of the LSA module assumes a static dependency tree derived from a single parser. Clinical notes often contain ambiguous grammatical structures that do not map cleanly to standard dependency trees. Incorporating multiple parsers or an ensemble of structural representations could provide a more robust basis for alignment. 

Finally, while MAT exhibits computational efficiency, its performance on extremely long documents—such as multi-page discharge summaries—remains to be tested. Future research will explore the application of MAT within the context of hierarchical Transformers to handle clinical documents that exceed the 512-token limit.

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we presented *MedAlign-Transformer* (MAT), a novel approach to ADE extraction that integrates dynamic latent-structure alignment into the standard Transformer architecture. By enforcing syntactic consistency through a learned alignment objective, MAT significantly improves the extraction of relational drug-event information in complex clinical narratives. Our results on the N2C2 2018 dataset confirm that structural guidance is a powerful tool for clinical IE, consistently outperforming standard attention-based baselines. We believe this framework provides a robust foundation for future efforts in automated pharmacovigilance, ultimately contributing to safer and more efficient patient care.
