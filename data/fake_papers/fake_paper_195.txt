### Abstract
Offline Reinforcement Learning (RL) faces a fundamental challenge: the overestimation of Q-values due to out-of-distribution (OOD) actions, leading to catastrophic failure when the learned policy deviates from the behavior policy. While existing Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) methods mitigate this by penalizing OOD actions, they often overlook the inherent aleatoric uncertainty present in stochastic environments. In this paper, we propose Distributional Conservative Policy Optimization (DCPO), a novel framework that integrates distributional value estimation with uncertainty-aware constraint regularization. By modeling the full return distribution rather than scalar expectations, DCPO provides a richer signal for conservatism. We derive a theoretical bound showing that DCPO minimizes the upper tail of the value distribution, effectively suppressing optimistic bias. Empirical evaluations on the D4RL benchmark suite demonstrate that DCPO outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving significant improvements in both sparse-reward and high-variance settings.

**Keywords:** Offline Reinforcement Learning, Distributional RL, Conservative Estimation, Uncertainty Quantification, Policy Optimization.

### 1. Introduction
The promise of Reinforcement Learning (RL) lies in its ability to solve sequential decision-making problems without explicit supervision [1], [2]. However, the requirement for active environment interaction often renders RL impractical in high-stakes domains such as autonomous driving, healthcare, and robotics [3], [4]. Offline RL attempts to bridge this gap by learning optimal policies from static, pre-collected datasets [5], [6]. Despite its potential, offline RL is severely limited by the "distributional shift" problem, where the agent’s learned policy produces actions outside the support of the behavior policy, leading to erroneously high Q-value estimates [7], [8].

Current approaches, primarily centered around Conservative Q-Learning (CQL), address this by adding a penalty term to the Bellman operator [9], [10]. However, most extant methods rely on point estimates of the value function, which are inherently incapable of capturing the aleatoric uncertainty arising from stochastic transition dynamics [11], [12]. This ignorance of variance leads to suboptimal behavior, as the agent fails to distinguish between high-reward states and high-variance, risky states [13], [14].

In this work, we introduce Distributional Conservative Policy Optimization (DCPO). Unlike existing methods that regularize scalar value estimates, DCPO estimates the distribution of returns using a Quantile Regression framework [15], [16]. We incorporate a novel conservative regularization mechanism that penalizes the upper quantiles of the value distribution, ensuring that the agent remains pessimistic regarding potential catastrophic outcomes while exploiting high-confidence regions [17], [18]. We provide theoretical guarantees concerning the convergence of our policy under the proposed conservative objective and demonstrate that DCPO mitigates the overestimation bias more effectively than standard CQL approaches [19], [20]. Our contributions are as follows:
1. A novel distributional conservative objective that incorporates risk-aware penalties.
2. A theoretical analysis proving that our conservative constraint bounds the approximation error.
3. Extensive experiments on D4RL datasets showing superiority over leading offline RL algorithms [21], [22].

---

### 2. Related Work
Offline RL has evolved significantly since the early formulations of batch reinforcement learning [23], [24]. The most prominent line of research focuses on constraining the policy to remain close to the behavior policy via KL-divergence or MMD constraints [25], [26]. Methods such as BRAC [27] and TD3+BC [28] explicitly regularize the policy against the behavioral data. In contrast, value-based methods like CQL [9] modify the value objective directly to be conservative, avoiding the need for explicit policy constraints.

Distributional RL, initiated by Bellemare et al. [15], replaces scalar values with distributions, yielding significant improvements in sample efficiency and stability [29], [30]. The integration of distributional approaches into offline settings has been explored in works like REM [31] and IQL [32], though these often lack a formal mechanism for incorporating uncertainty-aware conservatism. Recent efforts in uncertainty quantification, such as those using ensembles [33] or Bayesian inference [34], provide a mechanism for quantifying epistemic uncertainty, yet they often suffer from high computational complexity [35], [36]. DCPO uniquely differentiates itself by combining the strengths of distributional modeling with an analytically tractable conservative constraint.

### 3. Preliminaries
We define the Offline RL problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \gamma, \rho_0)$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space, $\mathcal{A}$ the action space, $P(s'|s, a)$ the transition probability, $r(s, a)$ the reward function, $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ the discount factor, and $\rho_0$ the initial state distribution [37], [38]. We are given a static dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(s_i, a_i, r_i, s'_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ generated by an unknown behavior policy $\pi_\beta(a|s)$ [39], [40].

The distributional Bellman operator for a return distribution $Z^\pi(s, a)$ is given by [15]:
$$ \mathcal{T}^\pi Z(s, a) \stackrel{D}{=} R(s, a) + \gamma Z(s', \pi(s')) \quad (1) $$
where $\stackrel{D}{=}$ denotes equality in distribution. In our approach, we parameterize the distribution using $N_q$ quantiles $\{\theta_k(s, a)\}_{k=1}^{N_q}$. The quantile regression loss for state-action pairs is defined as [16]:
$$ L_{QR}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(s,a,s') \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sum_{i,j} \rho_\tau (\hat{Z}_i(s, a) - \hat{Z}_j(s', a')) \right] \quad (2) $$
where $\rho_\tau$ is the Huber quantile loss function.

---

### 4. Methodology: Distributional Conservative Policy Optimization (DCPO)

#### 4.1 The Conservative Distributional Objective
To ensure pessimism, we define a conservative regularizer that targets the upper tail of the return distribution. Let $Z_\theta(s, a)$ denote the predicted distribution. The DCPO objective is:
$$ \min_{\theta} \max_{\pi} \left[ L_{QR}(\theta) + \alpha \cdot \Psi(Z_\theta(s, a), \pi) \right] \quad (3) $$
where $\Psi$ represents the uncertainty-aware penalty. Unlike CQL which uses the expectation, we define $\Psi$ as the conditional value-at-risk (CVaR) of the distribution, which forces the agent to estimate the worst-case returns:
$$ \Psi(Z_\theta, \pi) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D}, a \sim \pi} \left[ \text{CVaR}_\beta(Z_\theta(s, a)) - \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \beta} [Z_\theta(s, a)] \right] \quad (4) $$
By minimizing the CVaR, the agent inherently becomes more conservative towards actions that exhibit high variance in the upper tail, effectively filtering out OOD actions that appear erroneously attractive due to aleatoric noise.

#### 4.2 Algorithm
The pseudocode for DCPO is summarized in Algorithm 1.

**Algorithm 1: DCPO**
1. Initialize critic networks $\theta_1, \theta_2$ and policy $\pi_\phi$.
2. For each iteration:
3.   Sample batch $\mathcal{B} = \{(s, a, r, s')\} \subset \mathcal{D}$.
4.   Compute $L_{QR}$ (Eq. 2) and the penalty $\Psi$ (Eq. 4).
5.   Update critic parameters: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \nabla_\theta (L_{QR} + \alpha \Psi)$.
6.   Update policy $\pi_\phi$ to maximize the lower quantile $\hat{Z}_{\theta_1}(s, a)$.
7.   Target networks update: $\bar{\theta} \leftarrow \tau \theta + (1-\tau) \bar{\theta}$.

#### 4.3 Complexity Analysis
The computational complexity is $O(N \cdot N_q \cdot |\mathcal{A}|)$, where $N_q$ is the number of quantiles. While this is more expensive than standard Q-learning, the use of vectorization on modern GPUs allows for performance competitive with ensemble-based approaches while offering more robust uncertainty estimation [41], [42].

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
**Theorem 1.** *Given an MDP with bounded rewards $R_{max}$, the distribution $Z_\theta$ learned by DCPO converges to a value function $V^\pi$ such that the overestimation bias is bounded by $\frac{\alpha}{1-\gamma} \Delta_{OOD}$, where $\Delta_{OOD}$ denotes the KL divergence between the learned policy and the behavior policy.*

*Proof Sketch.* The distributional Bellman operator is a contraction mapping in the Wasserstein metric space [43], [44]. By incorporating the $\Psi$ penalty, we enforce a contraction towards the support of $\mathcal{D}$. Since $\Psi$ increases with the divergence from $\mathcal{D}$, the fixed point of the operator is shifted downward, restricting optimistic estimations in state-action regions with low data density [45], [46].

### 8. Discussion
The empirical success of DCPO can be attributed to its ability to differentiate between aleatoric uncertainty inherent in the environment and epistemic uncertainty arising from the lack of data. By regularizing the tail of the distribution, the agent is effectively incentivized to avoid high-variance transitions that often characterize failure modes in offline environments [47], [48].

Compared to standard CQL, DCPO provides a more nuanced approach to conservatism. While CQL applies a uniform penalty across the value estimation, our framework uses the distributional shape to tailor the magnitude of the penalty dynamically [49], [50]. This is especially beneficial in AntMaze tasks, where the agent must navigate high-risk pathways that may appear optimal in a deterministic setting but are fraught with potential failure in a stochastic environment [51], [52].

Furthermore, the integration of quantile regression ensures that DCPO remains computationally efficient compared to methods relying on ensembles of neural networks [53], [54]. The use of a single, albeit quantile-structured, critic reduces the memory overhead while simultaneously capturing a more comprehensive view of the potential value distribution [55], [56].

---

### 9. Limitations and Future Work
Despite its advantages, DCPO has limitations. First, the hyperparameter $\alpha$ (the penalty coefficient) requires careful tuning, as an excessively large $\alpha$ may lead to extreme pessimism and prevent the agent from learning meaningful policies in data-poor regions [57], [58]. Second, the current implementation uses a fixed quantile grid, which may not capture complex, multimodal distributions efficiently compared to more sophisticated density estimation techniques [59], [60].

Future work will investigate adaptive strategies for setting the penalty coefficient using meta-learning approaches to minimize the need for manual tuning [61], [62]. Additionally, we intend to explore extending DCPO to continuous control tasks where the transition dynamics are non-Gaussian, necessitating the use of normalizing flows for distribution modeling [63], [64].

Finally, we aim to investigate the theoretical robustness of DCPO in settings with multi-agent coordination, where the distributional shift is compounded by the behavior of other agents [65], [66]. We believe that the framework presented here provides a solid foundation for advancing the robustness of offline RL in complex, real-world deployment scenarios [67], [68].

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we proposed Distributional Conservative Policy Optimization (DCPO), a method designed to address the challenges of overestimation bias and distributional shift in offline RL. By adopting a distributional perspective and introducing a risk-aware conservative regularizer, we successfully demonstrated superior performance across diverse benchmarks. Our theoretical analysis confirms that the DCPO objective provides a formal bound on value estimation error. We believe that DCPO represents a significant step forward in making offline RL both safer and more performant, paving the way for applications in high-uncertainty real-world domains.
