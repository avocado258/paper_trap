### Abstract
Offline reinforcement learning (RL) holds the promise of learning optimal policies from static datasets without environment interaction, yet it remains fundamentally constrained by the distributional shift between the behavior policy and the learned policy. Existing conservative methods, such as Conservative Q-Learning (CQL), mitigate overestimation bias by penalizing Q-values on out-of-distribution (OOD) actions. However, these methods often struggle with high-variance return distributions and the "conservative trap," where overly pessimistic lower bounds stifle exploration. We propose **Distributional Constraint-Aware Conservative Q-Learning (DCA-CQL)**, a novel framework that incorporates distributional reinforcement learning into a conservative objective, allowing for a more nuanced quantification of uncertainty. By modeling the full return distribution rather than just the expectation, DCA-CQL dynamically adjusts pessimism based on the aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty of the action-value estimate. Empirical results on the D4RL benchmark demonstrate that DCA-CQL consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, particularly in sparse-reward environments, while maintaining robust performance across diverse dataset qualities.

**Keywords:** Offline Reinforcement Learning, Distributional RL, Policy Optimization, Uncertainty Estimation, OOD Generalization.

### I. Introduction

The paradigm of offline reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a cornerstone for data-driven decision-making, offering the potential to extract policies from pre-existing logs without the risks associated with online exploration [1]. Despite the proliferation of algorithms—ranging from behavior cloning to value-function regularization—a persistent challenge remains: the compounding overestimation of Q-values for actions not present in the logged dataset, commonly referred to as the "deadly triad" of overestimation bias, bootstrapping, and function approximation [2]. While recent advancements such as Conservative Q-Learning (CQL) have sought to alleviate this via Lagrangian-constrained minimization of Q-values on OOD actions, these methods typically operate on scalar expectations, potentially masking the underlying volatility of the learned policy [3].

It is our contention that the reliance on scalar expectations in offline RL limits the capacity of agents to distinguish between high-variance rewards and genuine OOD failures. By adopting a distributional perspective, we can decompose uncertainty into aleatoric components, inherent to the environment, and epistemic components, arising from limited data coverage [4]. This paper introduces DCA-CQL, a framework that integrates quantile regression into a conservative value-learning objective. By regularizing the distribution of Q-values, DCA-CQL provides a more granular control over the degree of pessimism, effectively relaxing the constraint in regions of high confidence and tightening it where the data support is sparse.

The subsequent sections detail the formulation of our proposed method, the theoretical underpinnings of its convergence properties, and an extensive suite of experiments evaluating its performance relative to current benchmarks. We demonstrate that by accounting for the distributional variance, DCA-CQL mitigates the sub-optimality inherent in uniform conservative penalties.

### II. Related Work

The literature on offline RL is vast, largely bifurcated into methods utilizing policy constraints and those employing value-function regularization. Policy constraint methods, such as BRAC [5] and TD3+BC [6], restrict the learned policy to remain close to the behavior policy via KL-divergence penalties. While theoretically sound, these methods often suffer from limited exploration capacity, as they are tethered to the potentially sub-optimal behavior policy.

Value-regularization approaches, most notably CQL [3], have gained prominence for their ability to learn directly from the Q-function by penalizing OOD actions. However, standard CQL employs a uniform penalty, which can be overly restrictive in diverse datasets [7]. Distributional RL, pioneered by C51 [8] and QR-DQN [9], has shown success in online settings by modeling the probability distribution of returns. Attempts to bridge this with offline RL, such as IQN-based conservative methods [10], have demonstrated the utility of distributional representations in mitigating estimation bias. Our approach distinguishes itself by explicitly coupling the quantile-based distribution with a dynamic constraint-weighting mechanism that scales with the epistemic uncertainty derived from an ensemble of Q-networks.

### III. Background / Preliminaries

We consider the standard Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \gamma, \rho_0)$. In the offline setting, we are provided with a fixed dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{(s_i, a_i, s'_i, r_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ collected by a behavior policy $\pi_\beta$. The objective is to learn a policy $\pi$ that maximizes the expected return $J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t r_t]$.

In distributional RL, we define the random variable $Z^\pi(s, a)$ representing the return under policy $\pi$. The Bellman operator $\mathcal{T}^\pi$ acts on the distribution:
$Z^\pi(s, a) \stackrel{D}{=} R(s, a) + \gamma Z^\pi(s', a')$ (1)
where $R$ is the reward distribution. In DCA-CQL, we represent $Z^\pi$ using a set of $K$ quantiles, $\theta_k(s, a)$, approximating the cumulative distribution function (CDF).

### IV. Methodology / Proposed Approach

DCA-CQL enhances the CQL framework by incorporating the distributional quantile representation. We define the objective function as a combination of the distributional Bellman error and the conservative constraint.

**Algorithm 1: DCA-CQL Training**
1. Initialize ensemble of $M$ quantile networks $\{Q_{\phi_m}\}_{m=1}^M$.
2. For each iteration:
   a. Sample batch $\{(s, a, s', r)\}$ from $\mathcal{D}$.
   b. Compute target quantile distribution $T_\pi Z(s, a)$.
   c. Update $Q_\phi$ by minimizing the quantile Huber loss:
      $L_Q(\phi) = \mathbb{E} [\sum_{k=1}^K \rho_\tau(T_\pi Z - Q_\phi(s, a))]$.
   d. Compute the conservative penalty $C(\phi) = \alpha \cdot \log (\sum \exp(Q_\phi(s, a_{OOD})))$.
   e. Update $\phi \leftarrow \phi - \nabla_\phi (L_Q + C(\phi))$.

The conservative penalty $C(\phi)$ is dynamically scaled by the variance of the ensemble $\sigma^2(s, a) = \frac{1}{M} \sum \|Q_{\phi_m} - \bar{Q}\|^2$, ensuring that regions with high epistemic uncertainty receive higher penalties.

---

### V. Theoretical Analysis

**Proposition 1.** *The value function $Q_\phi^\pi$ learned via DCA-CQL is a pointwise lower bound on the true value function $Q^\pi$ under the assumption of sufficient ensemble diversity.*
*Proof Sketch:* By the contraction property of the Bellman operator under the Wasserstein metric [11], and given that the penalty term $C(\phi)$ dominates the estimation error in low-density state-action regions, the operator remains a contraction, ensuring convergence to a fixed point $\tilde{Q} \leq Q^\pi$.

The computational complexity of DCA-CQL is $O(M \cdot K \cdot |\mathcal{D}|)$, where $M$ is the ensemble size and $K$ the number of quantiles. This is linearly scalable but necessitates careful hyperparameter tuning of the quantile count $K$.

### VIII. Discussion / Insights

The results indicate that the distributional approach significantly stabilizes the learning process in offline settings. By explicitly modeling the variance, the model avoids the "mean-averaging" trap where high-reward/high-risk actions are incorrectly valued. 

Furthermore, our observations suggest that the dynamic weighting mechanism is crucial in environments with heterogeneous data quality. In datasets where only a small subset of transitions are optimal, the epistemic uncertainty acts as a signal to guide the agent toward more reliable trajectories.

The trade-off observed between training time and performance suggests that while $M=5$ ensembles are sufficient for stability, larger ensembles yield marginal gains at significant computational cost.

---

### IX. Limitations & Future Work

Despite its efficacy, DCA-CQL is limited by the distributional approximation error, particularly in high-dimensional continuous action spaces where quantile resolution is restricted. Future work will investigate the use of normalizing flows for continuous distribution modeling.

Additionally, the reliance on an ensemble for uncertainty estimation introduces a sensitivity to hyperparameter initialization. We aim to explore Bayesian neural networks as a more principled alternative to ensembling in future iterations.

Finally, while we have validated our method on locomotion tasks, its applicability to complex multi-task offline RL scenarios remains an open question requiring further empirical verification.

---

### X. Conclusion

DCA-CQL provides a robust framework for offline RL by unifying distributional value estimation with uncertainty-aware conservative constraints. Through our experiments, we have demonstrated that this approach effectively navigates the tension between overestimation and under-exploration. We believe this contribution marks a meaningful step toward safer, more reliable offline decision-making systems.
