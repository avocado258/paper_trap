# Adaptive Latent-Space Routing: A Stochastic Gating Framework for Sparse Vision Transformers

**Authors:**
Elena V. Sterling, Department of Computer Science, MIT; Marcus T. Thorne, AI Research Lab, ETH Zurich; Julian R. Vance, Institute for Intelligent Systems, Stanford University.

---

### Abstract
The computational overhead associated with dense Vision Transformers (ViTs) significantly hinders their deployment in resource-constrained environments. While Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures offer a pathway toward conditional computation, traditional top-$k$ routing mechanisms often suffer from load imbalance and suboptimal expert specialization. We propose **Stochastic Latent-Space Routing (SLR)**, a novel gating framework that utilizes a continuous relaxation of the routing objective to enable differentiable, sparse activation of expert blocks. By introducing a temperature-controlled Gumbel-Softmax bottleneck, SLR decouples expert selection from input token saliency, fostering improved representation diversity. We demonstrate that our approach achieves competitive performance on ImageNet-1K with a 40% reduction in FLOPs compared to dense baselines. Furthermore, we provide a theoretical analysis of the routing entropy, suggesting that our stochastic gating mechanism mitigates the "expert collapse" phenomenon prevalent in static routing regimes.

**Keywords:** Vision Transformers, Mixture of Experts, Conditional Computation, Sparse Gating, Stochastic Optimization.

---

### I. Introduction
The advent of the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture has fundamentally reshaped the landscape of computer vision, shifting the paradigm from inductive bias-heavy convolutional architectures to global dependency modeling [1]. Despite their impressive representational capacity, ViTs scale quadratically with token sequence length, leading to prohibitive computational costs in high-resolution regimes [2]. To mitigate this, conditional computation via Mixture of Experts (MoE) has emerged as a promising avenue, allowing for model capacity to grow while keeping per-token inference cost constant [3].

However, the efficacy of sparse models is intrinsically tied to the gating mechanism that mediates expert selection. Current state-of-the-art routers typically rely on deterministic top-$k$ selection, which often leads to the under-utilization of certain experts—a phenomenon colloquially referred to as "expert collapse" [4]. Such deterministic routing strategies are prone to overfitting on specific token patterns, thereby limiting the model’s generalization capabilities across diverse visual domains.

In this paper, we introduce Stochastic Latent-Space Routing (SLR), a framework designed to optimize the trade-off between expert specialization and global load balancing. By reformulating the gating function as a stochastic optimization problem, we allow the model to explore a wider configuration space during training. We argue that by injecting controlled noise into the gating logits, we can regularize the expert activation distribution, forcing the network to distribute information across a broader set of parameters. Our contributions are threefold: 
1. We propose the SLR gating mechanism, which leverages a differentiable Gumbel-Softmax relaxation to optimize expert routing.
2. We derive a novel auxiliary loss function that penalizes expert imbalance without sacrificing the performance of high-saliency tokens.
3. We conduct extensive empirical evaluations on ImageNet-1K, demonstrating that SLR outperforms conventional top-$k$ routing in both accuracy and parameter efficiency.

---

### II. Related Work
The field of conditional computation dates back to the seminal work on adaptive neural networks, which sought to activate only a subset of parameters per input [5]. In the context of large-scale language models, the Switch Transformer popularized the use of sparse MoE layers, utilizing a simple token-wise router to select a single expert [6]. While effective, the rigidity of the Switch router often leads to training instability, necessitating complex load-balancing auxiliary losses [7].

Vision Transformers have recently adopted these MoE paradigms to scale model size without commensurate increases in FLOPs [8]. Methods such as V-MoE utilize a spatial-aware gating mechanism, yet they remain susceptible to the inherent biases of the underlying attention layers [9]. Recent advancements in soft-gating suggest that continuous distributions over expert weights can lead to smoother loss landscapes [10]. However, these approaches often require significant memory overhead during the backward pass, as they necessitate the computation of gradients through all expert combinations [11].

Contrastingly, our work builds upon the literature of stochastic neural networks and variational inference [12]. By utilizing the Gumbel-Softmax trick, we approximate categorical sampling in a differentiable manner, which has been previously explored in the context of neural architecture search (NAS) [13]. We extend this to the domain of MoE-ViTs, providing a robust theoretical framework for latent-space routing that balances exploration and exploitation.

### III. Background / Preliminaries
Let $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$ be the input sequence of $N$ tokens, where $D$ is the embedding dimension. A standard MoE layer consists of $M$ experts $\{E_1, E_2, \dots, E_M\}$, where each expert $E_i$ is typically a feed-forward network (FFN). The gating function $G(\mathbf{x})$ determines the contribution of each expert to the output:
$$y = \sum_{i=1}^M G_i(\mathbf{x}) E_i(\mathbf{x})$$
where $\sum G_i(\mathbf{x}) = 1$. In top-$k$ routing, $G_i(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ for all but the $k$ highest-scoring experts. This non-differentiable selection forces practitioners to use reinforcement learning or heuristic-based auxiliary losses to train the router [14]. We define our goal as finding a gating function $G_\theta$ parameterized by $\theta$ that maximizes task performance while minimizing the expected computational budget $C = \sum_{i} \mathbb{I}(G_i > 0) \cdot \text{FLOPs}(E_i)$.

---

### IV. Methodology / Proposed Approach
The core of our SLR framework lies in the stochastic transformation of the gating logits. Given a raw logit vector $z = W_g \mathbf{x}$, where $W_g$ is the gate weight matrix, we define the gated activation as:
$$G_i(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\exp((\log(\pi_i) + g_i) / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^M \exp((\log(\pi_j) + g_j) / \tau)}$$
where $\pi_i$ is the normalized input-dependent probability, $g_i \sim \text{Gumbel}(0, 1)$ is the Gumbel noise, and $\tau$ is the temperature parameter [15]. 

**Algorithm 1: Stochastic Latent-Space Routing**
1. **Input:** Token $\mathbf{x}$, Gate weights $W_g$, Experts $\{E_i\}$, Temperature $\tau$.
2. **Compute Logits:** $z \leftarrow W_g \mathbf{x}$.
3. **Inject Noise:** $s_i \leftarrow z_i + \text{Gumbel}(0, 1)$.
4. **Soft-Gating:** $\alpha_i \leftarrow \text{softmax}(s_i / \tau)$.
5. **Thresholding:** Select indices $\mathcal{I} = \{i \mid \alpha_i > \text{threshold}\}$.
6. **Output:** $y = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \alpha_i E_i(\mathbf{x}) / \sum_{j \in \mathcal{I}} \alpha_j$.

By modulating $\tau$, we control the sparsity of the expert selection. At low $\tau$, the distribution approaches a categorical distribution, while high $\tau$ encourages a more uniform, ensemble-like behavior. We employ a dynamic schedule for $\tau$, annealing it throughout the training process to transition from exploration to fine-grained exploitation [16].

### V. Theoretical Analysis
To analyze the routing efficiency, we consider the entropy of the gating distribution $H(G) = -\sum_{i=1}^M p_i \log p_i$. In static top-$k$ models, $H(G)$ is often minimized prematurely, leading to expert specialization on noisy or irrelevant features [17]. In SLR, we introduce an entropy regularization term $\mathcal{L}_{ent} = -\lambda H(G)$ to the objective function, which forces the router to maintain a minimum level of stochasticity.

**Theorem 1.** *The expected gradient variance of the SLR gate decreases monotonically with the annealing of $\tau$, provided the gating logit space is Lipschitz continuous.* 
*Proof Sketch:* Given that the Gumbel-Softmax estimator is a biased estimator of the categorical distribution, the variance is bounded by the temperature $\tau$. As $\tau \to 0$, the estimator converges to the discrete selection, and the gradient variance is governed by the sensitivity of $W_g$ to the input $\mathbf{x}$. Assuming $W_g$ is constrained, the Lipschitz bound on the softmax function ensures stability [18].

### VI. Experiments
We evaluate SLR on the ImageNet-1K dataset (1.28M training images, 50k validation images). We utilize the ViT-Base architecture (12 layers, 768 hidden dimension) as our backbone, replacing every second FFN block with an MoE block containing 8 experts.

**Experimental Setup:**
- **Optimizer:** AdamW with a weight decay of 0.05.
- **Learning Rate:** Cosine schedule starting at $10^{-3}$.
- **Batch Size:** 1024 across 32 A100 GPUs.
- **Baselines:** 
  1. Dense ViT (No MoE) [1].
  2. Switch Transformer (Top-1) [6].
  3. V-MoE (Top-2) [9].
  4. Hash-based Routing [19].
  5. Soft-MoE [20].

**Table 1: Performance Comparison on ImageNet-1K**
| Model | Top-1 Acc (%) | FLOPs (G) | Params (M) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Dense ViT | 81.2 | 17.5 | 86 |
| Switch (Top-1) | 80.5 | 9.2 | 140 |
| V-MoE (Top-2) | 81.8 | 11.4 | 160 |
| Hash-Routing | 80.9 | 10.1 | 145 |
| Soft-MoE | 82.1 | 12.8 | 180 |
| **SLR (Ours)** | **82.5** | **10.5** | **150** |

### VII. Ablation Study
To understand the contribution of each component, we performed an ablation study on the SLR mechanism.

**Table 2: Ablation Study Results**
| Configuration | Top-1 Acc | Avg. Load Balance |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| SLR (Full) | 82.5 | 0.98 |
| w/o Entropy Loss | 81.4 | 0.82 |
| w/o Gumbel Noise | 80.8 | 0.75 |
| Static Temp ($\tau=1$) | 81.9 | 0.91 |

Observations indicate that the Gumbel noise is critical for maintaining load balance. Without it, the model collapses into a suboptimal set of experts early in training. The entropy loss further refines the balance, ensuring that all experts remain "alive" throughout the training lifecycle [21].

### VIII. Discussion
The empirical results suggest that SLR provides a robust mechanism for balancing performance and compute efficiency. Unlike deterministic routers, the stochastic nature of SLR allows it to "sample" the expert space during training, effectively performing a form of implicit regularization that prevents the model from overfitting to the initial weights of the gating function. 

It is worth noting that the computational complexity of the router itself remains $O(N \cdot M)$, which is negligible compared to the $O(N^2)$ complexity of the self-attention layers. However, the memory overhead associated with maintaining multiple expert weights in VRAM remains a significant bottleneck for extremely large expert counts. Future work could explore weight-sharing strategies or low-rank factorization (LoRA) within the experts to further reduce the memory footprint [22].

Furthermore, the sensitivity to the temperature schedule $\tau(t)$ suggests that there is an optimal annealing rate that depends on the dataset size and the complexity of the visual tasks. Our current implementation uses a linear schedule, but we hypothesize that a data-dependent adaptive schedule could yield even better convergence properties in heterogeneous data environments [23].

---

### IX. Limitations & Future Work
Despite the advantages, SLR is not without limitations. First, the reliance on Gumbel-Softmax introduces a reliance on the quality of the gradient estimation, which can become unstable if the number of experts $M$ is excessively high. Second, the current implementation assumes a uniform cost per expert; however, in hardware-constrained environments, experts with different architectures may have varying latency, which is not currently accounted for in the gating objective.

In future work, we plan to extend SLR to multi-modal transformers where the gating mechanism must handle both visual and textual inputs. Additionally, integrating differentiable neural architecture search into the expert design process could allow the model to autonomously evolve the expert capacity based on the task at hand [24]. We also intend to explore hardware-aware routing, where the gate is conditioned on the current memory bandwidth and cache occupancy of the target platform [25].

---

### X. Conclusion
This paper has presented Stochastic Latent-Space Routing, an innovative approach to conditional computation in Vision Transformers. By leveraging the Gumbel-Softmax distribution, we have demonstrated that stochastic gating effectively mitigates expert collapse and improves overall classification performance. Our experimental results on ImageNet-1K indicate that SLR achieves a superior trade-off between accuracy and computational cost compared to traditional top-$k$ routing baselines. We believe this work provides a foundational step toward more flexible and efficient sparse architectures for the next generation of visual intelligence.
