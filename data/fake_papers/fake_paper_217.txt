# DynaPrune: Entropy-Driven Dynamic Token Pruning for Efficient Transformer Inference

**Dr. Aris Thorne, Lead Researcher, Institute for Neural Computation; Dr. Elena V. Vance, Senior AI Scientist, NexaCore Labs; Dr. Julian H. Sterling, Professor of Computer Science, Tech University.**

### Abstract
The computational overhead of Transformer-based models remains a significant barrier to their deployment in resource-constrained environments. While static pruning and distillation techniques have demonstrated efficacy, they often suffer from information loss across diverse input distributions. We propose **DynaPrune**, a novel framework for dynamic token pruning that leverages localized entropy-based importance scoring to identify and discard redundant tokens during inference. By integrating a lightweight gating mechanism into intermediate transformer blocks, DynaPrune adaptively reduces sequence length based on the specific input complexity. Our experiments on the GLUE benchmark and long-document summarization tasks demonstrate that DynaPrune achieves a 2.4x speedup in wall-clock inference time while maintaining 98.7% of the performance of a dense BERT-large baseline. We provide a theoretical analysis of the error bounds introduced by pruning and demonstrate that our entropy-aware approach minimizes the divergence between pruned and full-attention distributions.

**Keywords:** Transformers, Efficiency, Dynamic Pruning, Entropy, Model Compression, NLP.

### 1. Introduction
The advent of Transformer architectures has revolutionized natural language processing, setting new benchmarks across a spectrum of tasks [1], [2], [3]. However, the quadratic complexity of self-attention with respect to sequence length imposes substantial computational costs, limiting their application in latency-sensitive settings [4], [5], [6]. Current strategies, such as weight pruning and knowledge distillation, often sacrifice structural flexibility, resulting in models that are optimized for static data distributions but fail under distributional shifts [7], [8], [9].

Dynamic token pruning has emerged as a promising alternative, focusing on the removal of "redundant" tokens that contribute minimally to the semantic representation of a sequence [10], [11], [12]. Despite this progress, existing methods often rely on heuristic-based thresholding that ignores the informational value of individual tokens relative to the broader context [13], [14], [15]. This leads to catastrophic information loss when processing complex, high-variance inputs.

In this paper, we introduce **DynaPrune**, an architectural framework designed to mitigate this issue. Unlike previous approaches, DynaPrune calculates token importance using a localized entropy metric, allowing the model to dynamically evaluate the information gain of each token within specific transformer layers. By learning a gating function that predicts which tokens to discard, we significantly reduce the computational footprint without compromising linguistic integrity. Our contributions are threefold:
1. We define an entropy-based pruning objective that optimizes for information retention.
2. We implement a differentiable gating module that ensures stable gradient flow during training.
3. We provide empirical evidence that DynaPrune outperforms existing dynamic pruning baselines across GLUE and summarization tasks.

---

### 2. Related Work
Efficiency in Transformer models has been extensively studied through various lenses. Static model compression, including weight pruning and quantization, focuses on minimizing the number of parameters without altering the architecture [16], [17], [18]. While successful in reducing storage requirements, these methods do not inherently address the inference-time latency induced by long input sequences.

Recent efforts have pivoted toward dynamic computation and sequence length reduction. Methods such as Learned Token Pruning [19] and "Dynamic BERT" [20] employ reinforcement learning or auxiliary classifiers to prune tokens. However, these methods often necessitate complex training pipelines and struggle with non-differentiable objectives [21], [22]. Distillation-based approaches, such as TinyBERT [23] and DistilBERT [24], provide significant gains but lack the adaptability required for varying input complexity.

The intersection of information theory and NLP has yielded promising results in quantifying token importance [25]. Entropy-based measures have been utilized in data cleaning and feature selection [26], [27], yet their integration into the dynamic forward pass of a Transformer remains under-explored. DynaPrune fills this gap by leveraging the self-attention mechanism itself as a proxy for informational entropy, ensuring that pruning decisions are grounded in the model's internal representation state [28], [29].

### 3. Background and Preliminaries
Let $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_n\}$ be an input sequence of $n$ tokens. A standard Transformer block computes the hidden representation $H^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ at layer $l$. The self-attention mechanism is defined as:
$$ \text{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$
where $Q, K, V$ are the query, key, and value matrices [1]. We assume that many tokens in $X$ are redundant or contribute minimally to the final representation [30], [31].

We define the token importance vector $\mathbf{s} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ as a measure of the contribution of each token to the output. Our objective is to learn a binary mask $M \in \{0, 1\}^n$ such that $M_i = 1$ if $x_i$ is retained and $M_i = 0$ otherwise, minimizing:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{task}(f(X, M)) + \lambda \sum_{l=1}^L \text{Cost}(M^{(l)}) $$
where $\text{Cost}(M^{(l)})$ is the computational cost of the pruned sequence at layer $l$, and $\lambda$ is a hyperparameter balancing accuracy and efficiency [32], [33].

---

### 4. Methodology
DynaPrune introduces a gating mechanism $G_\theta$ integrated into each Transformer block. The mechanism computes a saliency score for each token using the entropy of the attention distribution.

#### 4.1 Entropy-Based Importance Scoring
We define the importance of token $i$ as the inverse of its attention entropy:
$$ H(i) = -\sum_{j=1}^n P_{ij} \log P_{ij} $$
where $P_{ij}$ is the attention weight of token $i$ on token $j$. High entropy implies a uniform distribution (low information contribution), whereas low entropy suggests a highly focused token (high information contribution).

#### 4.2 Differentiable Gating
To enable end-to-end training, we approximate the discrete selection process using the Gumbel-Softmax distribution [34], [35]. The mask $M$ is generated as:
$$ M_i = \text{sigmoid}\left(\frac{\log(\pi_i) + \epsilon}{\tau}\right) $$
where $\pi_i$ is the output of a lightweight MLP predicting the retention probability, and $\tau$ is the temperature parameter.

#### Algorithm 1: DynaPrune Forward Pass
```python
# Pseudocode for the DynaPrune block
def forward(H_in):
    # Calculate attention scores
    attn_weights = compute_attention(H_in)
    # Calculate token entropy
    entropy = -sum(attn_weights * log(attn_weights))
    # Predict pruning mask
    logits = MLP(H_in)
    mask = Gumbel_Softmax(logits, temperature=0.1)
    # Filter tokens
    H_pruned = H_in[mask > 0.5]
    return Transformer_Layer(H_pruned)
```

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
**Theorem 1.** *Given an attention distribution $P$, the information loss incurred by pruning tokens with $H(i) > \gamma$ is bounded by the variance of the value projections $V$.*

*Proof sketch:* Let $V$ be the value matrix. The approximation error $\epsilon = \|V - \hat{V}\|$ is proportional to the probability mass of the discarded tokens. Since $H(i)$ captures the dispersion of attention, tokens with high entropy contribute minimally to the weighted sum $V$. By the convexity of the entropy function, minimizing total entropy subject to a sequence length constraint $K$ provides an optimal lower bound on the information loss [36], [37].

The computational complexity of the standard Transformer is $O(n^2 \cdot d)$. With DynaPrune, the complexity becomes $O(\sum_{l=1}^L (n_l^2 \cdot d))$, where $n_l$ is the number of tokens retained at layer $l$. Since $n_l \ll n$ for deep layers, the efficiency gains are asymptotically significant [38], [39].

### 6. Experiments
#### 6.1 Datasets and Setup
We evaluate DynaPrune on the GLUE benchmark [40] and the CNN/DailyMail summarization dataset [41]. We utilize a BERT-base architecture (12 layers, 768 hidden units). Training is performed on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs with a batch size of 32 for 10 epochs.

#### 6.2 Baselines
1. **Dense BERT**: Standard BERT-base [1].
2. **Static Pruning**: Pruning the last 50% of tokens [19].
3. **Random Pruning**: Randomly discarding 50% of tokens as a baseline [42].
4. **Learn-to-Prune (LTP)**: The method from [20].
5. **DistilBERT**: Standard distillation baseline [24].

#### 6.3 Results
Table 1 shows the performance comparison on GLUE.

| Model | Accuracy (Avg) | Inference Time (ms) | Speedup |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| BERT-base | 84.2 | 125 | 1.0x |
| Static Pruning | 79.1 | 78 | 1.6x |
| Random Pruning | 72.4 | 75 | 1.7x |
| LTP [20] | 81.5 | 68 | 1.8x |
| DistilBERT | 82.0 | 72 | 1.7x |
| **DynaPrune** | **83.1** | **52** | **2.4x** |

Table 2 demonstrates performance on CNN/DailyMail (ROUGE-L scores).

| Model | ROUGE-L | Latency (ms) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| BERT-base | 38.5 | 450 |
| DynaPrune | 37.8 | 190 |

### 7. Ablation Study
We analyze the contribution of the entropy-based gating module.

| Component | Accuracy | Latency (ms) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| DynaPrune (Full) | 83.1 | 52 |
| w/o Entropy Scoring | 80.2 | 58 |
| w/o Gumbel-Softmax | 78.5 | 55 |
| w/o MLP Gating | 76.1 | 60 |

The results indicate that entropy-based scoring is crucial for maintaining performance at high compression rates, contributing 2.9% to the final accuracy.

### 8. Discussion
The empirical results demonstrate that DynaPrune effectively balances the trade-off between computational efficiency and model performance. By utilizing entropy to govern token retention, the model mimics human reading patterns, where attention is directed toward high-information entities [43], [44], [45]. 

The latency gains (2.4x) are particularly pronounced, suggesting that DynaPrune is well-suited for edge deployment. Unlike static methods, our approach adapts to the input sequence length, ensuring that "easy" sentences are processed with minimal computational effort while "difficult" sentences retain sufficient representational capacity [46], [47].

However, we observed that for tasks requiring high linguistic precision (e.g., grammatical error correction), the pruning threshold must be carefully tuned. We suggest that future work could incorporate a multi-objective optimization approach to dynamically adjust the pruning budget based on the specific task requirements [48], [49], [50].

---

### 9. Limitations and Future Work
Despite its effectiveness, DynaPrune faces limitations. First, the overhead of the Gumbel-Softmax gate can become non-negligible for very small sequences. Second, the current implementation relies on sequence-level pruning, which may lose cross-token relationships that are essential for long-range dependency modeling [51], [52].

Future research will explore hierarchical pruning, where tokens are clustered rather than discarded. Additionally, we plan to investigate the impact of DynaPrune on multimodal architectures (e.g., Vision Transformers), where token density is significantly higher [53], [54]. We believe that integrating DynaPrune with hardware-aware kernels will further amplify the observed speedups [55].

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced DynaPrune, an efficient framework for Transformer inference via entropy-based dynamic token pruning. By leveraging information-theoretic measures to guide the removal of redundant tokens, we achieved substantial latency reductions while maintaining competitive performance. Our method outperforms existing dynamic pruning baselines and provides a scalable solution for deploying large-scale models in resource-constrained environments.
