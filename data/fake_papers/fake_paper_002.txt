### Abstract
Few-shot learning (FSL) remains a fundamental challenge in computer vision, where models must generalize to novel classes given minimal supervisory data. Existing prototype-based methods, while efficient, often suffer from distribution shift between the base-class training manifold and novel-class evaluation tasks. This paper proposes the **Manifold-Consistent Prototype Calibration (MCPC)** framework, a novel architecture that employs a Riemannian manifold-aware calibration mechanism to align noisy class prototypes with the global distribution of the feature space. By introducing an adaptive geometry-preserving loss function, MCPC mitigates the catastrophic bias inherent in small-sample estimation. We demonstrate that our approach consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks, including miniImageNet and tieredImageNet, achieving competitive accuracy with reduced variance. Our theoretical analysis provides bounds on the generalization error under the assumption of manifold compactness, establishing the convergence properties of our calibration module.

**Keywords:** Few-shot learning, Prototype calibration, Riemannian geometry, Manifold learning, Meta-learning.

---

### I. Introduction
The objective of few-shot learning (FSL) is to develop models capable of rapid adaptation to new tasks with limited training examples. Traditional deep learning paradigms, predicated on large-scale supervised datasets, fail to generalize when the sample size $N$ is small, leading to overfitting and unstable representations [1]. Prototype-based methods, such as Prototypical Networks (ProtoNets), mitigate this by mapping support samples into a high-dimensional feature space and computing class centroids [2]. However, these centroid-based approaches are highly sensitive to outlier noise within the support set, which is exacerbated when $N$ is small.

In this paper, we address the "Prototype-Shift" problem, wherein the empirical mean of the support set deviates significantly from the true class distribution due to the sparsity of training data. While prior work has explored meta-learning optimization and data augmentation, few have addressed the underlying geometric misalignment between the learned feature manifold and the calculated prototypes [3]. We introduce the Manifold-Consistent Prototype Calibration (MCPC) network, which utilizes a Riemannian manifold-constrained optimization objective to pull support-set prototypes toward a global distribution manifold.

Our primary contributions are summarized as follows: (1) A novel MCPC architecture that integrates Riemannian geometric constraints into the meta-learning loop. (2) A closed-form derivation for the prototype calibration term, which allows for computationally efficient updates. (3) Extensive empirical validation on the miniImageNet and tieredImageNet benchmarks, demonstrating consistent gains over baseline methodologies.

---

### II. Related Work
Prototype-based FSL approaches rely on the assumption that features are clusterable in a latent metric space. Snell et al. [2] introduced Prototypical Networks, utilizing Euclidean distance as a metric, which was later extended by using Mahalanobis distance to account for feature covariance [4]. Relation Networks further improved this by replacing fixed metrics with a learnable deep similarity function [5]. Despite these advancements, these methods fail to account for the geometric structure of the feature space.

Metric-based approaches have been complemented by optimization-based meta-learning, such as MAML, which optimizes initial parameters for rapid fine-tuning [6]. However, these methods are computationally expensive and prone to catastrophic forgetting [7]. Recently, transductive inference has gained traction, using unlabeled query sets to refine prototype estimates [8]. Our method differs from transductive approaches by focusing on the intrinsic geometry of the feature manifold rather than relying on the distribution of the query set, thereby ensuring robustness in purely inductive settings.

### III. Preliminaries
Consider a dataset $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_{train} \cup \mathcal{D}_{test}$ where classes in $\mathcal{D}_{train}$ and $\mathcal{D}_{test}$ are disjoint. We define a support set $\mathcal{S} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N$ and a query set $\mathcal{Q} = \{(x_j, y_j)\}_{j=1}^M$. The embedding function $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ maps inputs to $d$-dimensional vectors. 

The standard prototype for class $c$ is defined as:
$$c_k = \frac{1}{|S_k|} \sum_{(x_i, y_i) \in S_k} f_\theta(x_i) \quad \text{(1)}$$
where $S_k$ is the subset of $\mathcal{S}$ belonging to class $k$. The probability of assigning $x \in \mathcal{Q}$ to class $k$ is given by a softmax over distance metrics: $P(y=k|x) = \frac{\exp(-d(f_\theta(x), c_k))}{\sum_{k'} \exp(-d(f_\theta(x), c_{k'}))}$.

---

### IV. Proposed Approach: MCPC
The core innovation of MCPC is the calibration of $c_k$ using a geometric regularizer that embeds the manifold structure of the base classes. We define the calibrated prototype $\hat{c}_k$ as:
$$\hat{c}_k = c_k + \lambda \cdot \nabla_{\mathcal{M}} \mathcal{L}_{geo}(c_k, \Phi) \quad \text{(2)}$$
where $\mathcal{M}$ represents the learned feature manifold, $\Phi$ is the set of global class prototypes, and $\lambda$ is a learnable scaling factor.

#### Algorithm 1: MCPC Training Procedure
1. Initialize feature extractor $f_\theta$ and manifold projector $P_\psi$.
2. For each episode:
    a. Sample support set $\mathcal{S}$ and query set $\mathcal{Q}$.
    b. Compute initial prototypes $c_k$ using (1).
    c. Project $c_k$ into latent space $\hat{c}_k$ via $P_\psi$.
    d. Minimize objective $\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{manifold}$, where $\mathcal{L}_{manifold}$ measures the distortion on the manifold.
    e. Update $\theta$ and $\psi$ via gradient descent.

### V. Theoretical Analysis
**Lemma 1.** *Given a feature space with curvature $\kappa$, the variance of the prototype estimate $c_k$ is bounded by $\frac{\sigma^2}{N} + \mathcal{O}(\kappa^2)$.*
*Proof Sketch:* Expanding the Taylor series of the distance metric on a Riemannian manifold allows us to account for the geodesic deviation, showing that as $N \to \infty$, the bias term converges to the manifold tangent space, validating our calibration approach.

**Complexity Analysis.** The computational overhead of MCPC is $\mathcal{O}(d^2 + |S|d)$, where $d$ is the feature dimensionality. This is linear with respect to the support set size, ensuring scalability.

### VIII. Discussion
The experimental results indicate that MCPC consistently outperforms baseline methods. The gains are most pronounced in the 1-shot setting, where prototype instability is at its peak. Our observation suggests that explicitly modeling the manifold geometry allows the network to "hallucinate" the missing data density effectively.

Furthermore, the integration of the calibration module does not significantly increase training time, as the manifold projection involves only a small-scale MLP. This confirms that the geometric priors are sufficiently captured by the feature extractor, necessitating only minor adjustments during inference.

One notable insight is the reliance on the quality of the base-class manifold. When base classes are sparse, the performance of MCPC degrades slightly, suggesting that the effectiveness of the calibration is strictly tethered to the diversity of the pre-training data.

---

### IX. Limitations and Future Work
Despite the robust performance, MCPC is limited by its reliance on the assumption of a smooth manifold. In scenarios where the data is highly disconnected or multi-modal, the Riemannian approximation may fail. Future work will focus on non-parametric manifold estimation, potentially using diffusion maps to characterize the feature space.

Additionally, while we focused on classification, extending MCPC to few-shot object detection or segmentation presents a promising avenue for research. The integration of spatial attention mechanisms could further refine prototype localization within high-resolution images.

Finally, we intend to explore the robustness of MCPC against adversarial perturbations. Preliminary tests suggest that manifold-aligned prototypes are more resistant to small-norm noise, a hypothesis we aim to formalize in subsequent studies.

---

### X. Conclusion
We presented the Manifold-Consistent Prototype Calibration (MCPC) framework, a theoretically grounded approach to enhancing few-shot classification. By incorporating Riemannian geometric priors into the prototype generation process, we effectively mitigate the bias introduced by small sample sizes. Our results demonstrate superior performance and suggest that geometric awareness is a critical component for future developments in meta-learning architectures.
