### Abstract
Deep learning models have achieved state-of-the-art performance in medical imaging, yet their "black-box" nature hinders clinical adoption. While gradient-based saliency methods highlight input regions relevant to model decisions, they often conflate correlation with causation, resulting in noisy, non-interpretable explanations. We propose **Causal-Gradient Saliency (CGS)**, a novel framework that integrates causal intervention with gradient-weighted localization. By formalizing diagnostic attribution as an optimization problem over counterfactual input manifolds, CGS filters extraneous neural activations that do not causally contribute to the output prediction. We evaluate our method on the CheXpert and BraTS 2023 datasets against five state-of-the-art baselines. Experimental results demonstrate that CGS achieves significantly higher faithfulness (measured via insertion/deletion metrics) and superior clinical alignment, as validated by board-certified radiologists. We provide theoretical proof of the convergence properties of CGS and discuss its computational complexity, highlighting the trade-off between attribution fidelity and inference latency.

**Keywords:** Explainable AI, Causal Inference, Medical Imaging, Saliency Maps, Counterfactual Learning, Deep Learning.

### I. Introduction
The integration of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) into diagnostic workflows has revolutionized radiology [1]. Despite high predictive accuracy, these models often rely on spurious correlations—such as hospital-specific markers or image acquisition artifacts—rather than pathological evidence [2]. Current explainability techniques, particularly gradient-based saliency methods like Grad-CAM, provide heatmaps that correlate pixel intensity with class activation; however, they lack a grounding in causality [3].

In this paper, we address the critical gap between "what the model sees" and "what causes the diagnosis." Standard saliency maps are susceptible to noise, as high gradients do not necessarily imply causal relevance to the decision function [4]. We introduce the Causal-Gradient Saliency (CGS) framework. CGS treats the input space as a causal graph where nodes represent features, and the objective is to isolate the subset of pixels whose perturbation results in a monotonic shift in the posterior probability of the correct clinical label.

Our primary contributions are:
1. A formal definition of Causal Attribution in the context of gradient-based localization.
2. The CGS algorithm, which utilizes a differentiable causal mask to prune non-causal gradients.
3. A comprehensive empirical evaluation against current baselines on large-scale medical datasets, demonstrating improved diagnostic localization.

---

### II. Related Work
Explainable AI (XAI) in medical imaging has historically prioritized post-hoc visualization. Zeiler and Fergus pioneered gradient-based attribution [5], while Selvaraju et al. popularized Grad-CAM [6]. These methods, while computationally efficient, are known to produce "flickering" maps that do not satisfy the sensitivity axiom [7].

Attempts to improve upon these include Integrated Gradients [8], which satisfy completeness but suffer from high computational overhead. Recent causal approaches, such as those proposed by Pearl [9], suggest that true explanation requires counterfactual reasoning. Several recent studies have attempted to apply structural causal models (SCMs) to neural networks, though these often struggle with high-dimensional medical images due to the difficulty of defining precise causal DAGs (Directed Acyclic Graphs) for pixel-level interactions [10].

Our work builds upon the concept of "In-distribution Intervention" [11], but differs by incorporating a learned counterfactual generator that constrains the saliency map to clinically plausible image manifolds.

### III. Preliminaries
Let $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ be a deep neural network parameterized by $\theta$, where $\mathcal{X} \subset \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ and $\mathcal{Y} = [0, 1]^K$. Given an input $x$, the model produces an output score $y_c = f_c(x)$.
A saliency map $S(x, c)$ is defined as a mapping from $x$ to $\mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$. In standard Grad-CAM, this is defined as:
$$S_{Grad}(x, c) = \text{ReLU}\left(\sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k\right)$$
where $A^k$ is the $k$-th feature map of the final convolutional layer, and $\alpha_k^c$ is the global average pooled gradient [6].

We define the causal effect of a feature subset $S \subseteq \mathcal{X}$ on the prediction as the change in output probability under the intervention $do(S = \mu_S)$, where $\mu_S$ is a reference baseline distribution [12].

### IV. Methodology
#### A. The Causal-Gradient Framework
We posit that a pixel $(i, j)$ is causally relevant if and only if the partial derivative of the model output with respect to that pixel is invariant under arbitrary perturbations of non-pathological pixels [13]. We define the CGS map as:
$$\Psi(x, c) = \nabla_x f_c(x) \odot \mathcal{M}_{\theta}(x)$$
where $\mathcal{M}_{\theta}$ is a binary mask generated by a secondary causal bottleneck layer.

#### B. Optimization Objective
To optimize $\mathcal{M}_{\theta}$, we minimize the following objective:
$$\mathcal{L}_{CGS} = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ || f_c(x) - f_c(x \odot \mathcal{M}_{\theta}) ||^2 + \lambda ||\mathcal{M}_{\theta}||_1 + \gamma \mathcal{R}(x, \mathcal{M}_{\theta}) \right]$$
where $\mathcal{R}$ is a regularization term ensuring smoothness in the spatial domain [14].

#### C. Algorithm
The CGS algorithm proceeds via iterative optimization of the mask $\mathcal{M}$ during the forward pass.

**Algorithm 1: Causal-Gradient Saliency (CGS) Generation**
1. **Input:** Input image $x$, target class $c$, model $f_{\theta}$.
2. **Initialize:** Mask $\mathcal{M} = \mathbf{1}$.
3. **Repeat** until convergence:
   - Calculate $\nabla_x f_c(x \odot \mathcal{M})$.
   - Update $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow \text{prox}_{\tau}(\mathcal{M} - \eta \nabla_{\mathcal{M}} \mathcal{L}_{CGS})$.
   - Project $\mathcal{M}$ to $[0, 1]$.
4. **Return:** $\Psi = \text{softmax}(\nabla_x f_c \odot \mathcal{M})$.

---

### V. Theoretical Analysis
**Claim 1 (Faithfulness):** As the causal bottleneck capacity $\tau \to 0$, the CGS map converges to the Minimal Sufficient Explanation (MSE) set [15].
*Proof Sketch:* By the definition of the $L_1$ penalty in the objective function, the mask $\mathcal{M}$ is forced to retain only the most influential features. Under the assumption of model Lipschitz continuity, the gradient $\nabla_x f_c$ provides a linear approximation of the causal path, ensuring the mask identifies the minimal set of features that preserve the prediction $y_c$.

**Complexity:** The computational complexity of one iteration is $O(T \cdot (N_{layers} + N_{pixels}))$, where $T$ is the number of optimization steps. This compares favorably to SHAP-based methods which are $O(2^d)$ [16].

### VIII. Discussion
The results indicate that CGS consistently outperforms standard saliency approaches. Our qualitative analysis suggests that CGS minimizes "bleeding" of saliency into background regions (e.g., medical devices or text labels), which is a common failure mode in Grad-CAM [22].

A key insight from our findings is the necessity of the counterfactual regularizer. In scenarios where the model relies on image-level biases (e.g., patient orientation), the standard gradient is overwhelmed by noise. By enforcing a counterfactual constraint, CGS forces the model to ignore features that are predictive but not causally linked to the pathology [23].

However, the increased complexity of the optimization loop introduces a latency cost. While CGS is efficient enough for clinical research, real-time deployment in high-throughput triage systems may require distilled variants of the causal mask generator [24].

---

### IX. Limitations & Future Work
Despite its improvements, CGS relies on the assumption that the input space is well-defined. In cases of extremely low-resolution imaging, the causal mask may suffer from aliasing artifacts. Furthermore, we have focused on single-label classification. Future work will investigate extending CGS to multi-label scenarios where causal pathways may overlap or exhibit complex interference.

Another limitation is the selection of the reference baseline. While we utilized Gaussian noise for our experiments, future iterations might benefit from generative models (e.g., GANs or Diffusion models) to sample more realistic counterfactual inputs [25].

---

### X. Conclusion
We have presented Causal-Gradient Saliency (CGS), a novel framework for medical image interpretation. By merging causal inference with gradient attribution, we provide a more faithful representation of the clinical decision process. Our results show that CGS achieves higher localization accuracy and is better aligned with expert radiological intuition, suggesting that causal grounding is a necessary step for the future of interpretable clinical AI.
