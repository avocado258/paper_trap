### Abstract
Time series forecasting in volatile environments necessitates not only point estimates but also reliable uncertainty quantification. While Transformer-based architectures have achieved state-of-the-art performance in point forecasting, their probabilistic outputs often suffer from overconfidence and miscalibration. In this work, we propose **Conformalized Temporal Quantile Transformer (CTQT)**, a novel framework that integrates multi-head attention mechanisms with inductive conformal prediction to produce distribution-free, valid prediction intervals. By optimizing a pinball loss function constrained by a coverage-guaranteeing calibration layer, our model achieves rigorous coverage properties without sacrificing the representational power of deep autoregressive models. We provide theoretical bounds on the coverage error and demonstrate through extensive experiments on public benchmark datasets (ETTh1, ETTm2, Weather, and Electricity) that CTQT consistently outperforms standard probabilistic baselines, yielding tighter prediction intervals while maintaining nominal coverage under non-stationary regimes.

### Keywords
Time Series Forecasting, Transformer Networks, Conformal Prediction, Uncertainty Quantification, Quantile Regression.

### Introduction
The challenge of time series forecasting has evolved from simple autoregressive modeling to complex, sequence-to-sequence neural architectures [1, 2]. Transformers, originally designed for natural language processing, have demonstrated superior capability in capturing long-range temporal dependencies [3, 4]. However, in domains such as quantitative finance, energy grid management, and supply chain logistics, point forecasts are insufficient. Decision-makers require probabilistic forecasts—specifically, prediction intervals—to assess risk and plan for contingencies [5, 6].

Existing approaches for probabilistic forecasting, such as DeepAR [7] or Quantile Regression Transformers [8], often rely on the assumption that the underlying distribution of the data is known or can be parameterized by a simple Gaussian or student-t process. These assumptions are frequently violated in real-world settings characterized by heteroscedasticity, non-stationarity, and "black swan" events [9, 10]. Consequently, these models exhibit poor calibration, leading to intervals that are either too narrow (under-coverage) or unnecessarily wide (lack of precision) [11, 12].

To address these limitations, we introduce the Conformalized Temporal Quantile Transformer (CTQT). Our approach leverages the expressive power of the Transformer architecture to learn conditional quantiles, while employing conformal prediction—a statistical framework for uncertainty quantification—to post-process these predictions [13]. Unlike traditional methods that assume specific noise models, conformal prediction provides finite-sample coverage guarantees under the exchangeability assumption [14]. This paper contributes: (1) A hybrid architecture combining pinball-loss-driven Transformer heads with a post-hoc calibration module; (2) A theoretical analysis of the coverage error bounds for the proposed method; and (3) A comprehensive empirical evaluation against five state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating superior interval score metrics.

---

### Related Work
The literature on time series forecasting has bifurcated into statistical methods and deep learning approaches. Classical methods, such as ARIMA and ETS, offer interpretability but struggle with high-dimensional, non-linear dynamics [15]. Deep learning models, particularly RNNs and LSTMs, were the standard for years until the emergence of the Transformer architecture [16].

Recent Transformer-based models, such as Informer [17] and Autoformer [18], have pushed the boundaries of efficiency in long-sequence forecasting. However, their probabilistic extensions, such as the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) [19], often require significant hyperparameter tuning for quantile calibration. Research into uncertainty quantification has increasingly focused on Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) [20] and Monte Carlo Dropout [21]. While robust, BNNs are computationally expensive to sample from, and MC Dropout provides only heuristic-based approximations of model uncertainty.

Conformal prediction has recently gained traction as a model-agnostic technique for uncertainty quantification [22]. Split conformal prediction [23] allows for the construction of intervals with a user-defined coverage probability $(1-\alpha)$. Recent works, such as those by Vovk et al. [24] and Angelopoulos et al. [25], have extended this to regression tasks. However, applying these to time series remains non-trivial due to temporal dependencies. Our work bridges this gap by integrating conformal calibration directly into the transformer decoding pipeline.

### Background
Let $\{z_1, z_2, \dots, z_T\}$ be a univariate time series. Given a history of length $L$, the goal is to forecast the future horizon $H$, i.e., $Y_{t+1:t+H} = \{z_{t+1}, \dots, z_{t+H}\}$. We define a quantile regression model $f_{\theta}$ that predicts the $\tau$-th quantile of the future values, denoted by $\hat{q}_{\tau}(t+k)$.

The standard objective for quantile regression is the pinball loss (or quantile loss), defined as:
$$L_{\tau}(y, \hat{q}_{\tau}) = \max(\tau(y - \hat{q}_{\tau}), (1-\tau)(\hat{q}_{\tau} - y)) \quad (1)$$
where $\tau \in (0, 1)$. To construct a $(1-\alpha)$ prediction interval, we estimate the $\alpha/2$ and $1-\alpha/2$ quantiles to define the lower and upper bounds, $[\hat{q}_{\alpha/2}, \hat{q}_{1-\alpha/2}]$.

---

### Methodology
The CTQT architecture consists of three core components: (1) A Temporal Transformer Encoder-Decoder, (2) A Multi-Head Quantile Regression Layer, and (3) An Inductive Conformal Calibration Layer.

#### Architecture
The encoder processes input history $X_{t-L:t}$ using self-attention to capture long-range dependencies. The decoder employs cross-attention over the encoder states. We replace the final softmax layer with a projection layer producing $2K$ outputs, representing the quantiles $\{\tau_1, \tau_2, \dots, \tau_K\}$.

#### Calibration
Given a calibration set $S_{cal} = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, we calculate the non-conformity scores:
$$E_i = \max(\hat{q}_{\alpha/2}(x_i) - y_i, y_i - \hat{q}_{1-\alpha/2}(x_i)) \quad (2)$$
We compute the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile of these scores, denoted by $\hat{Q}_{1-\alpha}(E, S_{cal})$. The final adjusted prediction interval is:
$$C(x) = [\hat{q}_{\alpha/2}(x) - \hat{Q}_{1-\alpha}, \hat{q}_{1-\alpha/2}(x) + \hat{Q}_{1-\alpha}] \quad (3)$$

#### Algorithm
The following algorithm describes the CTQT pipeline:

```python
Algorithm: Conformalized Temporal Quantile Transformer (CTQT)
Input: Training data D_train, Calibration data D_cal, Alpha (error rate)
Output: Calibrated interval [L, U]

1. Train Transformer f_theta on D_train using Pinball Loss (Eq. 1)
2. Predict quantiles on D_cal: q_lower = f_theta(x_i, alpha/2), q_upper = f_theta(x_i, 1-alpha/2)
3. Calculate scores: E_i = max(q_lower_i - y_i, y_i - q_upper_i)
4. Compute quantile q_hat = Percentile(E, (1-alpha)*(1 + 1/|D_cal|))
5. For new input x_new:
     L = f_theta(x_new, alpha/2) - q_hat
     U = f_theta(x_new, 1-alpha/2) + q_hat
6. Return [L, U]
```

### Theoretical Analysis
**Theorem 1 (Coverage Guarantee):** Assuming the calibration samples are exchangeable, the probability that the true value $y_{t+k}$ falls within the interval $C(x)$ satisfies:
$$P(y_{t+k} \in C(x)) \ge 1 - \alpha \quad (4)$$
*Proof Sketch:* By the property of the rank of the calibration scores, the quantile $\hat{Q}_{1-\alpha}$ ensures that at least $1-\alpha$ of the calibration samples have non-conformity scores less than or equal to the empirical quantile threshold. Since the test data is exchangeable with the calibration data, the coverage property holds under marginal distribution shift [14].

**Complexity Analysis:** The Transformer encoder has a complexity of $O(L^2 d)$, where $L$ is history length and $d$ is model dimension. The conformal adjustment is $O(n \log n)$ for sorting calibration scores, where $n$ is the size of the calibration set. Since $n \ll$ training iterations, the overhead is negligible.

### Discussion
The empirical results confirm that CTQT consistently achieves the target coverage, whereas uncalibrated models (Informer-QR) consistently underestimate uncertainty, especially in volatile periods. The ablation study demonstrates that the conformal layer is the primary driver of coverage, while the Transformer backbone provides the necessary representational capacity to reduce the interval width.

We observed that the effectiveness of the conformal layer is sensitive to the size of the calibration set. In datasets like Electricity, where temporal patterns are highly non-stationary, using a sliding window calibration approach (i.e., updating the calibration set every $T$ steps) yielded superior performance compared to a static hold-out set.

Finally, the computational overhead is minimal. The model trains in similar time to standard Transformers, and the post-hoc calibration is computed in milliseconds. This makes CTQT suitable for real-time deployment in latency-sensitive forecasting applications.

---

### Limitations & Future Work
While CTQT offers robust coverage, it assumes exchangeability, which may be violated during significant structural breaks (e.g., policy shifts or market crashes). Future work will explore "Adaptive Conformal Prediction" [26], which adjusts the calibration threshold dynamically based on recent errors.

Furthermore, we treated each time series dimension independently for simplicity. Future iterations could explore multivariate conformal prediction, where the interval is defined in the joint space of multi-horizon forecasts, accounting for the correlations between different time steps.

Lastly, while the Transformer backbone is powerful, it is data-hungry. We intend to investigate the performance of CTQT in low-data regimes by incorporating pre-trained weights from larger foundational time-series models (e.g., TimeGPT-based approaches) as feature extractors.

---

### Conclusion
We have presented the Conformalized Temporal Quantile Transformer (CTQT), a robust framework for probabilistic time series forecasting. By integrating a deep Transformer architecture with inductive conformal prediction, we provide a method that guarantees valid uncertainty intervals without sacrificing point-forecast accuracy. Extensive experiments confirm that CTQT delivers superior calibration across diverse datasets, marking a significant advancement in reliable time series analysis.
