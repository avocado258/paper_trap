### Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in natural language understanding and generation, yet they frequently inherit and amplify societal biases present in their training corpora. Existing mitigation strategies, such as post-hoc prompting or standard fine-tuning, often fail to address the underlying latent representations that encode demographic stereotypes. In this paper, we introduce **Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA)**, a novel fine-tuning framework that leverages contrastive learning to decouple protected attribute information from semantic representations. By enforcing a geometric constraint in the latent space, CDA minimizes the mutual information between token embeddings and sensitive demographic markers while preserving task-specific utility. We demonstrate the efficacy of our approach across four benchmark datasets—CrowS-Pairs, StereoSet, GLUE, and a custom demographic-sensitive corpus. Our experiments indicate that CDA achieves a significant reduction in bias metrics (up to 28% improvement in log-likelihood parity) while maintaining performance on downstream NLP tasks. We provide a rigorous analysis of the trade-offs between bias mitigation and linguistic fluency, offering a pathway toward more equitable deployment of generative models.

**Keywords:** Large Language Models, Demographic Bias, Contrastive Learning, Fairness in NLP, Representation Learning, Algorithmic Ethics.

### 1. Introduction
The rapid proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) into critical social infrastructures—ranging from automated hiring pipelines to clinical decision support—has catalyzed an urgent discourse regarding algorithmic fairness. Despite their impressive zero-shot capabilities, these models are trained on massive, uncurated web-scale corpora, which inherently contain pervasive historical and demographic biases [1], [2]. When deployed, these biases manifest as toxic outputs, stereotypical associations, and disparate treatment of marginalized groups [3], [4].

Current attempts to mitigate these biases often rely on heuristic-based filtering or instruction tuning. However, such methods frequently act as "surface-level" patches, failing to alter the deep-seated representation layers that propagate bias during inference [5], [6]. This paper posits that demographic bias is a fundamental byproduct of the joint optimization of linguistic utility and spurious statistical correlations between protected attributes (e.g., gender, race, religion) and contextual embeddings. 

To address this, we propose **Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA)**. CDA moves beyond traditional minimization of stereotypical output probabilities by fundamentally reshaping the manifold upon which LLMs operate. By utilizing a contrastive objective that treats demographic-specific input variations as "views" of the same semantic core, our method forces the model to align representations invariant to protected attributes. This approach is motivated by the hypothesis that robust fairness requires the disentanglement of sensitive features from the latent space rather than merely suppressing their manifestation in the decoder layer.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews the landscape of fairness in NLP; Section 3 introduces the mathematical preliminaries; Section 4 details the CDA framework; Section 5 provides theoretical insights; Section 6 outlines our experimental results; and Section 7 discusses the societal implications of our findings.

### 2. Related Work
The study of fairness in machine learning has evolved from simple parity constraints in classification tasks to complex debiasing of generative models. Early work by Bolukbasi et al. [7] identified gendered subspaces in word embeddings, sparking a wave of research into vector-space projection methods. Subsequent studies demonstrated that these projection methods often fail in high-dimensional neural architectures, leading researchers to explore adversarial training [8], [9].

Adversarial debiasing, as proposed by Zhang et al. [10], involves training a "fairness discriminator" that attempts to predict protected attributes from the hidden states of a primary model. While effective for simple discriminative tasks, adversarial approaches are notoriously unstable when applied to the generative objectives of autoregressive LLMs, often leading to mode collapse or catastrophic forgetting of linguistic capabilities [11], [12].

In parallel, contrastive learning—originally popularized in computer vision by SimCLR [13] and CLIP [14]—has recently been adapted for NLP. Approaches like Debias-Contrast [15] utilize augmentation to force the model to ignore specific attributes. However, these methods often lack a formal grounding in the Information Bottleneck principle, which is essential for determining the trade-off between task performance and bias reduction [16], [17]. Our work extends these concepts by integrating a novel contrastive loss function specifically tailored to demographic invariance, bridging the gap between representation learning and ethical AI. 

Furthermore, research into "safety-first" alignment has focused on RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) [18], [19]. While RLHF is a powerful tool for aligning models with human values, it is susceptible to "reward hacking" and does not necessarily eliminate the underlying latent biases [20], [21]. Our framework serves as a complementary mechanism to RLHF, working at the pre-training or fine-tuning stage to structuralize the latent space.

### 3. Preliminaries
Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote the space of input sequences, and $\mathcal{Y}$ the target output space. An LLM is characterized by a parameterized function $f_\theta: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^d$, mapping an input sequence $x$ to a representation vector $h \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The model generates output tokens via a softmax distribution $P_\theta(y|x) = \text{softmax}(W h + b)$.

We define a set of protected attributes $A = \{a_1, a_2, \dots, a_k\}$ (e.g., gender, race). A demographic bias exists if the conditional distribution $P_\theta(y|x, a_i)$ deviates significantly from $P_\theta(y|x, a_j)$ for equivalent semantic contexts $x$. We quantify this bias using the *Association Bias Metric* (ABM), defined as:
$$ABM = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}} [| \log P_\theta(y|x, a_i) - \log P_\theta(y|x, a_j) |]$$
where $\mathcal{D}$ is the distribution of test prompts designed to elicit demographic associations [22]. Our goal is to minimize $ABM$ while maintaining the cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}_{CE}$ on a downstream task $\mathcal{T}$.

---

### 4. Proposed Approach: Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA)
The CDA framework introduces a dual-objective optimization process. We define a contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ that operates on pairs of inputs $(x_i, x_j)$ which are semantically identical but differ in their demographic marker (e.g., "The [doctor/nurse] is busy" vs "The [nurse/doctor] is busy").

#### 4.1 Contrastive Loss Formulation
Let $h_i = f_\theta(x_i)$ and $h_j = f_\theta(x_j)$. We define the alignment loss as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{con} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(h_i, h_j) / \tau)}{\sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}} \exp(\text{sim}(h_i, h_k) / \tau)}$$
where $\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ is the cosine similarity, $\tau$ is a temperature hyperparameter, and $\mathcal{K}$ includes both positive pairs (semantic matches) and negative pairs (demographic-biased distractors).

#### 4.2 Total Objective
The final objective function is:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{con} + \gamma \mathcal{R}(\theta)$$
where $\lambda$ governs the trade-off between utility and fairness, and $\mathcal{R}(\theta)$ is a regularization term preventing the model from collapsing representations of distinct (non-protected) concepts.

**Algorithm 1: Training with CDA**
1. Initialize $\theta$ from pre-trained checkpoint.
2. For each batch of training data:
   a. Sample pairs $(x, x')$ where $x$ and $x'$ differ only by protected attribute $A$.
   b. Compute hidden representations $h$ and $h'$.
   c. Compute cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}_{CE}$ for next-token prediction.
   d. Compute contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ to pull $h$ and $h'$ together.
   e. Backpropagate $\nabla_\theta (\mathcal{L}_{CE} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{con})$.
3. Update $\theta$ using AdamW optimizer.

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
The efficacy of CDA rests on the premise that minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ forces the model to learn a representation $h$ that is a sufficient statistic for the semantic content of $x$, while discarding information about $A$. 

*Claim 1:* Given a sufficiently large capacity $f_\theta$, minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ is equivalent to maximizing the lower bound of the Mutual Information $I(h; X|A)$. 
*Proof Sketch:* By the Data Processing Inequality, as we increase the contrastive pressure between pairs $(x, x')$ differing only in $A$, the gradient descent forces the model to map all variants of $x$ to a single point in the manifold $\mathcal{M}_x$. Consequently, any token decoder $W$ acting on $h \in \mathcal{M}_x$ must rely on features independent of $A$. 

Complexity analysis reveals that the contrastive computation adds an overhead of $O(N_p \cdot d)$, where $N_p$ is the number of contrastive pairs. Given that most LLM fine-tuning is GPU-bound, this remains tractable for modern hardware.

### 6. Experiments

#### 6.1 Datasets and Baselines
We evaluated our method on:
1. **CrowS-Pairs:** Measures stereotypical bias in nine categories [23].
2. **StereoSet:** Measures bias across gender, profession, race, and religion [24].
3. **GLUE Benchmark:** Used to measure retention of linguistic utility [25].

We compared CDA against: (1) Vanilla FT (Standard Fine-Tuning), (2) Adv-Debias (Adversarial Training) [10], (3) Prompt-Engineering (System-level constraints) [26], (4) RLHF-Alignment [18], and (5) Counterfactual Data Augmentation (CDA-aug) [27].

#### 6.2 Results
Tables 1 and 2 summarize the performance metrics.

**Table 1: Bias Reduction (Lower is better for ABM)**
| Model | CrowS-Pairs (Bias Score) | StereoSet (ICAT Score) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Vanilla FT | 0.68 | 0.62 |
| Adv-Debias | 0.61 | 0.58 |
| Prompt-Eng | 0.59 | 0.55 |
| RLHF-Alignment | 0.54 | 0.52 |
| **CDA (Ours)** | **0.48** | **0.46** |

**Table 2: Utility Retention (Higher is better for GLUE Avg)**
| Model | GLUE Accuracy (%) | Perplexity |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Vanilla FT | 84.2 | 12.1 |
| Adv-Debias | 81.5 | 14.8 |
| Prompt-Eng | 83.9 | 12.5 |
| RLHF-Alignment | 82.8 | 13.2 |
| **CDA (Ours)** | **83.6** | **12.4** |

Results show that while Adv-Debias significantly degrades performance (GLUE 81.5%), CDA preserves linguistic utility (83.6%) while achieving superior bias mitigation.

### 7. Ablation Study
We investigated the impact of the contrastive weight $\lambda$.

**Table 3: Ablation of CDA Components**
| Configuration | Bias Score | GLUE |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| No Contrastive Loss ($\lambda=0$) | 0.68 | 84.2 |
| Partial Contrastive ($\lambda=0.1$) | 0.55 | 83.9 |
| Full Contrastive ($\lambda=1.0$) | 0.48 | 83.6 |
| Full + Neg Sampling | 0.47 | 83.1 |

The ablation confirms that the contrastive loss is the primary driver of bias reduction. Negative sampling provides marginal gains but at the cost of slight perplexity increases.

### 8. Discussion
The findings highlight a critical insight: fairness in LLMs is not merely a matter of filtering output, but of internal representation structure. Our results suggest that by forcing the latent space to collapse demographic differences into a unified semantic space, we can achieve robust fairness without the instability of adversarial training.

One limitation is the dependency on the quality of demographic-swapped pairs. If the dataset used for contrastive learning contains poor-quality swaps, the model may learn to associate wrong semantic meanings with demographic markers. Additionally, while we achieved significant reductions in bias, the model is not "bias-free." Residual bias remains, likely stemming from long-range dependencies in the training data that are not captured by local contrastive pairs.

Future work will explore multi-modal fairness, specifically whether CDA can be applied to vision-language models where demographic bias manifests in both text and image generation. Furthermore, we intend to investigate the scalability of this method to models exceeding 100B parameters, where the memory requirements for contrastive caching may require gradient checkpointing or model parallelism.

---

### 9. Limitations & Future Work
Despite the success of CDA, several limitations persist. First, the framework assumes that protected attributes are explicitly known. In many real-world scenarios, these attributes are intersectional (e.g., race and gender combined), making the construction of contrastive pairs combinatorially explosive. Second, the computational overhead, while manageable, scales with the size of the contrastive batch.

Future research should focus on "unsupervised" demographic alignment, where the model learns to identify and neutralize demographic-sensitive subspaces without explicit labeling. We also acknowledge the risk of "fairness gerrymandering," where a model is optimized for one demographic group at the expense of others. Future iterations will incorporate a Pareto-optimal objective to balance fairness across multiple protected attribute groups simultaneously.

---

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced Contrastive Demographic Alignment (CDA), a novel approach to mitigating demographic bias in LLMs. By leveraging contrastive learning to enforce demographic invariance within the latent representation space, we have demonstrated that it is possible to achieve significant bias reduction without compromising downstream task performance. Our framework provides a scalable and mathematically grounded alternative to existing heuristic-based debiasing methods. We believe that CDA represents a meaningful step toward the development of equitable and accountable artificial intelligence systems.
