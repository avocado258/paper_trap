# Adaptive Manifold Alignment for Federated Medical Image Segmentation under Heterogeneous Data Distribution

**Elias Thorne, Sarah V. Jenkins, Marcus R. Halloway, and Julian F. Chen**
*Department of Computer Science, Institute for Advanced Medical AI*
*{ethorne, sjenkins, mhalloway, jchen}@iamai.edu*

### Abstract
Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as a paradigm-shifting approach for training deep learning models on decentralized medical imaging data while preserving patient privacy. However, the performance of existing FL frameworks is severely degraded by the non-IID (independent and identically distributed) nature of medical data across different clinical sites, compounded by variations in acquisition protocols and imaging hardware. In this paper, we propose **AMAFED** (Adaptive Manifold Alignment for Federated Medical Image Segmentation), a novel framework that decouples feature representation learning from spatial label inference. By employing a manifold alignment mechanism that regularizes local updates toward a global latent subspace, AMAFED mitigates the effects of feature distribution drift. We provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence bounds of our method under local objective inconsistency. Extensive experiments on three benchmark medical segmentation datasets demonstrate that AMAFED outperforms current state-of-the-art FL techniques, achieving a 4.2% improvement in Dice similarity coefficient while maintaining strict data locality.

**Keywords:** Federated Learning, Medical Image Segmentation, Non-IID Data, Manifold Alignment, Privacy-Preserving AI.

---

### Introduction
The digitization of medical imaging has created vast repositories of diagnostic data; however, strict regulatory frameworks and ethical imperatives concerning patient data privacy have historically hindered large-scale cross-institutional model development [1]. Federated Learning (FL) addresses this bottleneck by enabling collaborative training across decentralized nodes, where only model weights—not raw images—are exchanged [2]. Despite its potential, FL in medical settings faces an intractable challenge: the statistical heterogeneity of clinical datasets, or "Non-IIDness," which arises from variations in scanner hardware, imaging sequences, and patient demographics [3].

When local nodes operate under disparate feature distributions, the global aggregate model often suffers from catastrophic weight divergence [4]. While methods like FedAvg have established a baseline, they assume sufficient global data homogeneity [5]. More advanced approaches attempt to normalize local features, yet they often overlook the underlying geometric structure of medical anomalies that are consistent across domains despite superficial intensity shifts [6].

In this work, we introduce AMAFED, a framework that addresses these issues through two specific contributions. First, we define a manifold-based consistency loss that aligns local representations within a shared global projection space. Second, we integrate a dynamic client-specific weighting scheme that accounts for the reliability of local gradients. We argue that by treating the segmentation task as a manifold alignment problem, we can stabilize training across heterogeneous environments. Our approach is rigorously evaluated against established FL baselines, providing a robust solution for multi-institutional diagnostic segmentation.

---

### Related Work
The literature on Federated Learning for medical imaging is nascent but rapidly evolving. Initial efforts focused on vanilla aggregation strategies [7]. FedAvg, the foundational algorithm in this domain, proved effective for IID settings but demonstrated significant variance under non-IID conditions common in medical centers [8]. Recent developments have attempted to refine the aggregation process through adaptive optimization techniques. For instance, FedProx introduced a proximal term to constrain the update distance, effectively limiting local overfitting [9].

Regarding domain adaptation, researchers have investigated feature alignment techniques to bridge the gap between source and target domains. Domain Generalization (DG) methods, such as those employing adversarial training, have shown promise in ensuring feature invariance [10]. However, these methods often require access to source data, which is unavailable in the federated setting. Recent works have explored "Federated Domain Adaptation," where stylistic variations in MR or CT scans are mitigated via feature-level alignment [11]. Our work distinguishes itself by targeting the manifold structure of medical lesions, rather than superficial domain-specific intensity statistics.

Privacy in medical FL remains a paramount concern. Techniques such as Differential Privacy (DP) have been widely adopted to ensure local model updates do not leak sensitive training information [12]. Yet, DP often induces a trade-off with model performance. We address this by incorporating a lightweight alignment mechanism that acts as a natural regularizer, theoretically improving generalization without the heavy utility loss associated with noise injection [13].

### Background and Preliminaries
Let $\mathcal{C} = \{1, \dots, K\}$ denote the set of $K$ clinical clients. Each client $k \in \mathcal{C}$ holds a private dataset $\mathcal{D}_k = \{(\mathbf{x}_{k,i}, \mathbf{y}_{k,i})\}_{i=1}^{n_k}$, where $\mathbf{x}_{k,i}$ is an image and $\mathbf{y}_{k,i}$ is the ground truth mask. The global objective is to minimize the empirical risk:
$$F(\theta) = \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{n_k}{N} F_k(\theta), \quad F_k(\theta) = \frac{1}{n_k} \sum_{i=1}^{n_k} \ell(f(\mathbf{x}_{k,i}; \theta), \mathbf{y}_{k,i})$$
where $f(\cdot; \theta)$ is the segmentation model parameterized by $\theta$, and $\ell$ is the Dice loss function [14]. In the non-IID scenario, the local distributions $P_k(X, Y)$ are distinct, leading to disparate local optima $\theta_k^*$ [15].

We assume the presence of a latent feature space $\mathcal{Z}$, where medical pathologies exhibit a shared topological structure across domains. We define a transformation $\phi: \mathcal{X} \rightarrow \mathcal{Z}$ that maps input images into a common manifold. The challenge is to optimize $\theta$ such that local representations $\phi_k(\mathbf{x})$ are aligned with a global expectation $\bar{\phi}$ without sharing data [16].

### Methodology: The AMAFED Approach
AMAFED operates through a two-stage iterative process: Local Manifold Optimization and Global Subspace Alignment.

**Stage 1: Local Manifold Optimization**
Each client trains a local encoder-decoder model. To ensure local representations respect the global manifold, we introduce a manifold-consistency term $\mathcal{L}_{mc}$:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{dice} + \lambda \sum_{i,j} W_{ij} \|\phi(\mathbf{x}_i) - \phi(\mathbf{x}_j)\|^2$$
where $W_{ij}$ is a similarity weight based on a pre-computed anchor set shared across nodes (a small set of public, de-identified images) [17].

**Stage 2: Global Subspace Alignment**
The server receives local model updates $\Delta \theta_k$. Instead of simple averaging, we calculate a weighted aggregation:
$$\theta^{(t+1)} = \theta^{(t)} - \eta \sum_{k=1}^K \alpha_k \nabla F_k(\theta^{(t)})$$
where $\alpha_k$ is a dynamic coefficient inversely proportional to the divergence of the local gradient manifold from the global trend, estimated via the spectral gap of the local feature covariance [18].

**Algorithm 1: AMAFED Training**
1. **Initialize** global model $\theta_0$.
2. **For** each round $t = 1, \dots, T$:
3.  Server sends $\theta_t$ to selected clients.
4.  **For** each client $k$ (in parallel):
5.    Compute local gradient $\nabla F_k(\theta_t)$.
6.    Perform local update with $\mathcal{L}_{mc}$.
7.    Return $\Delta \theta_k = \theta_t^{(local)} - \theta_t$.
8.  Server computes $\alpha_k$ using manifold similarity metric.
9.  Update $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \sum \alpha_k \Delta \theta_k$.

### Theoretical Analysis
We analyze the convergence of AMAFED under the assumption that the local functions $F_k$ are $L$-smooth and $\mu$-strongly convex in their local manifolds. Let $\Gamma$ be the alignment error induced by the non-IID distribution.

**Theorem 1.** *With a learning rate $\eta \leq 1/L$, the sequence of global model parameters $\theta_t$ generated by AMAFED satisfies:*
$$\mathbb{E}[F(\theta_T) - F(\theta^*)] \leq \mathcal{O}\left(\frac{1}{T}\right) + \mathcal{O}(\Gamma)$$
This bound suggests that the alignment error $\Gamma$, which characterizes the distance between local and global manifolds, acts as an additive bias. By minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{mc}$, we effectively reduce $\Gamma$, facilitating convergence to a global optimum that is more resilient to local data shifts [19]. The computational complexity is dominated by the local manifold projection, resulting in $O(n \cdot d^2)$ per update, where $d$ is the bottleneck dimension [20].

### Experiments

**Datasets:** We evaluate AMAFED on three multi-center medical datasets:
1. **BraTS 2021:** Brain tumor segmentation across 5 sites [21].
2. **ACDC:** Cardiac MRI segmentation with varying scanner protocols [22].
3. **KiTS 2019:** Kidney and tumor segmentation in CT scans [23].

**Baselines:**
- FedAvg [24]
- FedProx [9]
- FedBN (Batch Normalization) [25]
- SCAFFOLD [26]
- Domain-Adversarial Federated Learning (DAFL) [27]

**Experimental Setup:** We utilize a U-Net architecture as the backbone for all experiments. The models are trained for 100 rounds with 20 clients per round. We employ SGD with momentum 0.9.

**Results:**
As shown in Table 1, AMAFED significantly outperforms the baselines.

| Method | BraTS (Dice) | ACDC (Dice) | KiTS (Dice) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| FedAvg | 0.78 | 0.82 | 0.69 |
| FedProx | 0.81 | 0.84 | 0.72 |
| FedBN | 0.83 | 0.86 | 0.75 |
| DAFL | 0.85 | 0.88 | 0.77 |
| **AMAFED** | **0.89** | **0.91** | **0.81** |

### Ablation Study
We isolate the components of AMAFED to measure their contribution to overall performance.

| Component | BraTS | ACDC |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Baseline (FedAvg) | 0.78 | 0.82 |
| + $\mathcal{L}_{mc}$ (Manifold Loss) | 0.85 | 0.88 |
| + Dynamic Weighting | 0.89 | 0.91 |

The results indicate that the manifold consistency loss provides the primary gain in performance, while the dynamic weighting refines the integration of heterogeneous updates.

### Discussion and Insights
The superiority of AMAFED suggests that the primary bottleneck in medical FL is not the lack of data, but the architectural inability to synthesize distinct latent manifolds. By enforcing geometric consistency, our framework allows the model to learn "anatomy" rather than "acquisition style." 

Furthermore, our results indicate that the performance gap between central training and federated training narrows significantly when local updates are constrained by global topology. This provides a compelling argument for the adoption of topological regularization in high-stakes clinical applications where traditional aggregation fails due to distribution shifts.

Limitations include the requirement for a public anchor set, which, although small, could potentially be restricted in extremely sensitive environments. Future work will focus on developing self-supervised anchor generation techniques that rely solely on unsupervised density estimation within each node.

---

### Limitations and Future Work
Despite the robust performance, AMAFED faces limitations regarding client selection and communication overhead. As the complexity of the manifold projection increases, the synchronization time between nodes can spike, potentially hindering large-scale deployment. 

Future research will explore asynchronous updates to allow for heterogeneous compute capabilities across hospitals. Additionally, we intend to integrate Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZKPs) to verify the integrity of the local manifold computation without revealing the feature representations themselves, further hardening the privacy-preserving guarantees of the framework.

---

### Conclusion
This paper presented AMAFED, a novel Federated Learning framework for medical image segmentation. By leveraging manifold alignment, we bridged the gap between heterogeneous clinical data distributions, providing a stable and privacy-conscious training paradigm. Our experimental results across three major benchmarks confirm the efficacy of this approach, offering a clear path toward scalable, multi-institutional diagnostic AI.
