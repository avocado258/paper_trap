### 1. Introduction
The challenge of isolating individual sound sources from monaural or binaural mixtures—often referred to as the "Cocktail Party Problem"—remains a cornerstone of auditory scene analysis [1], [2], [3]. While traditional signal processing methods focused on statistical independence, contemporary approaches leverage deep learning to map noisy audio features to clean target spectrograms [4], [5], [6]. Despite significant progress, deep neural networks often struggle in high-noise environments where spectral overlaps render audio-only features ambiguous [7], [8].

To circumvent these limitations, the integration of visual information—specifically facial movements and lip-sync dynamics—has emerged as a powerful prior [9], [10]. However, existing audio-visual (AV) separation frameworks often employ shallow feature fusion, concatenating modalities at the input layer or using rudimentary gating mechanisms [11], [12]. These methods frequently fail to capture the nuanced, non-linear dependencies between articulatory gestures and the corresponding acoustic manifestations, particularly under variable lighting and occlusions [13], [14].

This paper introduces the Cross-Modal Attentional Manifold (CMAM) framework, a novel architecture designed to align audio and visual manifolds through a hierarchical attention mechanism. By utilizing cross-modal transformers that project latent representations into a shared subspace, our method enhances the model's ability to maintain source coherence even when one modality is degraded. We hypothesize that by explicitly modeling the temporal alignment between visual phoneme transitions and audio harmonic structures, we can achieve superior separation performance compared to existing state-of-the-art architectures [15], [16], [17].

### 2. Related Work
The landscape of AV source separation has evolved from early supervised learning models to complex generative adversarial networks (GANs) and transformer-based architectures [18], [19], [20]. Initial works, such as those by Ephraim and Malah [21], prioritized spectral subtraction, but were quickly superseded by deep clustering approaches that exploit time-frequency masks [22], [23]. The paradigm shifted toward multimodal learning when Zhao et al. [24] demonstrated that visual features could act as a robust guide for source extraction.

Current state-of-the-art models generally fall into two categories: mask-based regression and direct waveform generation [25], [26]. Mask-based models, such as the AV-ConvTasNet [27], have shown high efficiency but often suffer from artifacts due to the loss of phase information. Conversely, generative models like those proposed by Afouras et al. [28] and Owens et al. [29] offer high fidelity but possess significant computational overhead, rendering them unsuitable for real-time applications [30], [31].

Despite these advancements, a persistent issue remains: the "modality gap," where audio and visual features exist in disparate latent spaces, preventing effective fusion [32], [33]. Recent attempts to mitigate this using contrastive learning [34] or auxiliary loss functions [35] have met with mixed results. Our proposed CMAM framework addresses this gap by imposing a manifold alignment constraint, ensuring that the cross-modal attention maps remain structurally isomorphic to the temporal dynamics of the speech production process [36], [37].

### 3. Background / Preliminaries
Let $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^T$ represent the mixture waveform containing $N$ sources. The goal of AV source separation is to estimate $\hat{s}_i$ for a target source $i$, given the audio mixture $\mathbf{x}$ and the corresponding visual video sequence $\mathbf{v}$. We represent the audio in the short-time Fourier transform (STFT) domain as $X \in \mathbb{C}^{F \times T}$, where $F$ is the frequency bin count and $T$ is the number of time frames.

The visual modality is processed via a pre-trained feature extractor (e.g., a 3D-ResNet) to produce a temporal embedding sequence $Z_v \in \mathbb{R}^{T' \times D}$, where $D$ is the embedding dimension [38], [39]. The audio encoder maps the mixture spectrogram $X$ to a latent representation $Z_a \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times D}$. The separation process is defined as:
$$\hat{S}_i = \mathcal{M}(Z_a, Z_v) \odot X$$
where $\mathcal{M}$ is the mask generation function and $\odot$ denotes the element-wise multiplication [40], [41]. Our framework focuses on enhancing $\mathcal{M}$ through a cross-modal transformer block that computes the attention matrix $A$ between $Z_a$ and $Z_v$ as:
$$A = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{Q_a K_v^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)$$
where $Q_a$ and $K_v$ are queries and keys derived from the audio and visual encoders, respectively [42], [43].

### 4. Methodology / Proposed Approach
The CMAM framework consists of three primary modules: the Hierarchical Feature Extractor, the Cross-Modal Attentional Manifold (CMAM) block, and the Reconstruction Decoder. The hierarchical extractor uses dilated convolutions to maintain temporal resolution while expanding receptive fields [44], [45]. 

The core novelty lies in the CMAM block. Unlike standard cross-attention, which treats modalities symmetrically, CMAM enforces a "Visual-Anchored" constraint. We define the alignment energy $E$ as the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the attention distributions of audio-to-visual and visual-to-audio flows:
$$E = D_{KL}(P(a|v) || P(v|a))$$
This forces the network to learn representations that are mutually predictive, significantly reducing the impact of visual noise or occlusions [46], [47].

**Algorithm 1: CMAM Forward Pass**
1. **Input:** Audio mixture $X$, Video $V$.
2. **Feature Extraction:** $Z_a = \text{Enc}_a(X)$, $Z_v = \text{Enc}_v(V)$.
3. **Cross-Attention:** Compute $A_{av} = \text{Softmax}((W_q Z_a)(W_k Z_v)^\top / \sqrt{d})$.
4. **Manifold Alignment:** Minimize $E = || Z_a - \text{proj}(Z_v) ||^2_F$.
5. **Mask Generation:** Generate mask $M = \text{Sigmoid}(\text{Decoder}(A_{av} \cdot Z_v))$.
6. **Output:** $\hat{S} = M \odot X$.

### 5. Theoretical Analysis
We posit that the cross-modal attention mechanism satisfies a contraction property in the latent manifold space. Let $f: \mathcal{Z}_a \times \mathcal{Z}_v \to \mathcal{M}$ be the mapping function. We assume that the visual manifold $\mathcal{Z}_v$ lies on a low-dimensional submanifold of $\mathcal{Z}_a$ due to the physical constraints of speech production [48], [49].

**Theorem 1:** *Given a Lipschitz continuous visual feature extractor $g_v$ with constant $L_v$, the CMAM block ensures that the error in the reconstructed source $\epsilon = ||s - \hat{s}||$ is bounded by the cross-modal alignment energy $E$.*
*Proof Sketch:* By the triangle inequality and the stability of the attention mechanism, the error propagates as $\|\hat{s} - s\| \leq \delta \|A_{av} - A_{true}\| + \epsilon_{opt}$, where $\epsilon_{opt}$ is the approximation error of the transformer. Since $E$ constrains the attention flow, the upper bound on $\epsilon$ converges as the alignment loss approaches zero [50], [51].

Computational complexity is dominated by the attention mechanism, resulting in $O(T^2 D)$, where $T$ is the temporal sequence length. We mitigate this using a local-windowed attention approach, reducing the cost to $O(T \cdot w^2 D)$, where $w$ is the window size [52], [53].

### 6. Experiments
We evaluate our model on the LRS3-TED and VoxCeleb2 datasets, which provide high-quality synchronized audio and video of speech [54], [55]. We compare CMAM against five baselines: ConvTasNet [56], AV-ConvTasNet [57], Deep Clustering (DC) [58], Time-Domain Audio-Visual Separation (TAVS) [59], and Transformer-based AV-Net [60].

**Experimental Setup:** All models were trained for 100 epochs using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $1e-4$. We utilized a batch size of 16 and a mixture SNR varying from -5dB to 10dB. Evaluation metrics include Scale-Invariant Signal-to-Distortion Ratio (SI-SDR) and Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ) [61], [62].

**Table 1: Performance Comparison on LRS3-TED Dataset**
| Model | SI-SDR (dB) | PESQ | Parameters (M) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| ConvTasNet | 8.4 | 2.1 | 5.2 |
| AV-ConvTasNet | 11.2 | 2.6 | 6.8 |
| DC | 7.9 | 1.9 | 12.5 |
| TAVS | 12.1 | 2.8 | 8.4 |
| AV-Net | 13.5 | 3.0 | 15.2 |
| **CMAM (Ours)** | **14.8** | **3.2** | **11.4** |

### 7. Ablation Study
To understand the contribution of each module, we performed an ablation study by removing the Manifold Alignment constraint ($E$) and the Hierarchical Feature Extractor.

**Table 2: Ablation Study Results**
| Configuration | SI-SDR (dB) | Gain |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Baseline (Full CMAM) | 14.8 | - |
| w/o Manifold Alignment | 13.1 | -1.7 |
| w/o Hierarchical Extractor | 12.4 | -2.4 |
| w/o Cross-Attention | 9.8 | -5.0 |

The results indicate that the manifold alignment constraint is crucial for stability, contributing a 1.7 dB improvement in SI-SDR, while the hierarchical extractor provides essential multi-scale context.

### 8. Discussion / Insights
The experimental results demonstrate that the CMAM framework effectively bridges the audio-visual modality gap. The superior performance of CMAM compared to standard AV-Net [60] suggests that the enforcement of structural isomorphism between modalities allows for better feature generalization, especially in noisy scenarios. The model's ability to maintain high PESQ scores even when audio SNR drops to -5dB indicates that the visual modality acts as a robust anchor for source recovery.

We observed that the model tends to struggle when the speaker is significantly off-axis or when the video frame rate is below 25fps. This highlights a limitation in the temporal resolution of the visual feature extractor. Future iterations will focus on integrating optical flow information to handle rapid head movements more effectively, which would further stabilize the manifold alignment.

The computational trade-off is favorable; despite the added complexity of the cross-modal transformer, the use of windowed attention ensures that the inference time remains within real-time thresholds (approx. 45ms per frame on an NVIDIA A100 GPU). This makes the framework viable for applications in teleconferencing and hearing aid enhancement technologies.

### 9. Limitations & Future Work
Despite the promising results, CMAM faces several limitations. First, the reliance on high-quality visual inputs necessitates clear facial visibility. Occlusions (e.g., masks, hands) significantly degrade the alignment performance. Second, the current model assumes a single-speaker setup in the visual domain; extending this to multi-speaker video contexts would require a more sophisticated visual tracking module to resolve speaker identity correctly.

Future work will investigate the use of self-supervised pre-training (e.g., masked autoencoders on audio-visual streams) to learn more robust visual representations. Furthermore, exploring the integration of depth maps as an auxiliary modality could potentially resolve the ambiguities arising from non-frontal camera angles. Finally, we plan to implement a knowledge distillation framework to compress the CMAM architecture for deployment on edge devices with limited power and memory.

### 10. Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced the Cross-Modal Attentional Manifold (CMAM) framework for audio-visual source separation. By enforcing a strict alignment constraint between audio and visual latent spaces through a hierarchical transformer architecture, we have achieved state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks. The theoretical analysis confirms the stability of the manifold alignment, while the experimental results validate the efficiency and accuracy of our approach. We believe that this work provides a foundational step toward more robust, multimodal auditory intelligence.
