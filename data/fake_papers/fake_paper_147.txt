# Dynamic Information Bottleneck Attention Flow (DIB-AF) for Interpretable Text Classification

**Authors:**
Elena V. Sterling, Arthur M. Vance, and Hiroshi Tanaka
*Department of Computer Science, Institute for Advanced Machine Learning, Zurich; Vector Institute for AI Research, Toronto*

---

### Abstract
Deep transformer-based architectures have achieved state-of-the-art performance in text classification, yet their internal decision-making processes remain largely opaque. Traditional attention maps often suffer from "attention noise," where the weights do not reliably reflect the linguistic importance of input tokens. In this paper, we propose Dynamic Information Bottleneck Attention Flow (DIB-AF), a novel framework that integrates an information-theoretic bottleneck objective directly into the multi-head attention mechanism. By constraining the mutual information between the hidden representations and the input sequence, DIB-AF forces the model to distill high-fidelity, interpretable attribution flows. We demonstrate through extensive experiments on GLUE and custom sentiment analysis datasets that our approach achieves competitive classification accuracy while significantly outperforming existing interpretability metrics like Integrated Gradients and Attention Rollout. We provide a formal theoretical analysis of the convergence bounds of DIB-AF and demonstrate that our method captures latent semantic dependencies that were previously obscured by standard softmax-normalized attention.

**Keywords:** Explainable AI, Information Bottleneck, Self-Attention, Natural Language Processing, Model Interpretability.

---

### I. Introduction
The proliferation of Large Language Models (LLMs) and transformer architectures has fundamentally altered the landscape of natural language processing (NLP). Despite the empirical success of these models, the "black-box" nature of their inference remains a critical barrier to their deployment in high-stakes domains such as legal adjudication, clinical diagnosis, and financial risk assessment. While self-attention [1] provides a mechanism for models to weigh the relevance of input tokens, recent research suggests that raw attention weights are often poor proxies for feature importance [2].

Existing interpretability techniques, such as post-hoc attribution methods or saliency maps, frequently encounter the "faithfulness-plausibility trade-off." Post-hoc methods often require computationally expensive iterative processes and may produce divergent explanations for the same decision [3]. Furthermore, standard attention mechanisms are prone to "soft attention leakage," where probability mass is distributed across non-informative tokens (e.g., stop words or punctuation), thereby obscuring the true semantic drivers of a classification.

In this work, we address these challenges by introducing the Dynamic Information Bottleneck Attention Flow (DIB-AF). Unlike static interpretability constraints, DIB-AF incorporates an adaptive information bottleneck (IB) loss term that penalizes the entropy of the attention distribution relative to the task-specific prediction error. This approach forces the attention heads to prioritize tokens that maximize mutual information with the target class label. By optimizing this constrained objective, our model generates "Attention Flows"—a trajectory of information propagation that provides both spatial and temporal insights into the model’s reasoning. Our contributions are three-fold: (1) we propose the DIB-AF architecture; (2) we provide a theoretical derivation showing that DIB-AF minimizes the upper bound of the generalization error; and (3) we validate the framework against five state-of-the-art baselines on four standard datasets.

---

### II. Related Work
The pursuit of model transparency in NLP has evolved from simple linear model probes to complex post-hoc diagnostic tools. Early efforts focused on LIME [4] and SHAP [5], which approximate local model behavior using surrogate linear models. While versatile, these methods assume local linearity, which may not hold for highly non-linear transformer architectures.

Attention-based interpretability has been a subject of significant controversy. Jain and Wallace [6] famously argued that attention is not explanation, citing instances where attention weights were uncorrelated with gradient-based feature importance. Conversely, Wiegreffe and Pinter [7] demonstrated that under specific regularization constraints, attention can indeed serve as a faithful explanation. Our work aligns with the latter school of thought, proposing that interpretability should be an inherent property of the model’s training objective rather than an afterthought.

Information Bottleneck (IB) methods, originally proposed by Tishby et al. [8], have recently found applications in deep learning to improve robustness and generalization. Alemi et al. [9] applied the IB principle to variational autoencoders, while we extend this framework to the self-attention mechanism specifically to isolate salient linguistic features. By applying these concepts to NLP classification, we bridge the gap between information theory and interpretability.

---

### III. Background
Let $\mathcal{X} = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ be an input sequence and $y \in \mathcal{Y}$ be the target label. A standard transformer layer computes the attention matrix $A$ as:
$$A = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)$$
where $Q, K \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_k}$ are the query and key projections. The output $Z$ is then defined as $Z = AV$, where $V$ is the value projection.

The limitation of this formulation is that the softmax function, while differentiable, does not explicitly optimize for the relevance of $x_i$ to $y$. The Information Bottleneck principle aims to find a representation $Z$ that maximizes $I(Z; Y)$ while minimizing $I(Z; X)$. In the context of attention, we hypothesize that the attention weights $A$ act as a gating mechanism for $X$. Therefore, we seek to constrain the complexity of $A$ such that it encodes only the minimal sufficient statistics for the classification task.

---

### IV. Methodology
The DIB-AF framework introduces a secondary optimization loop that regulates the attention weights during training. We define the DIB-AF objective function as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{IB}$$
where $\mathcal{L}_{CE}$ is the standard cross-entropy loss and $\mathcal{L}_{IB}$ is the attention-based information bottleneck term:
$$\mathcal{L}_{IB} = \sum_{h=1}^H \text{KL}(P(A_h | X) || P(A_h | Y))$$
Here, $H$ is the number of attention heads. By minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the attention distribution and the label-conditional distribution, the model is penalized for attending to tokens that do not contribute to the reduction of uncertainty in $y$.

**Algorithm 1: DIB-AF Training Step**
1. **Input:** Batch $B$, Model $\theta$, Bottleneck weight $\beta$.
2. **Forward Pass:** Compute $Q, K, V$ for input $X$.
3. **Compute Attention:** Generate $A$ using masked softmax.
4. **IB Constraint:** Calculate the KL divergence between $A$ and the label-conditioned prior.
5. **Loss Calculation:** $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{CE} + \beta \mathcal{L}_{IB}$.
6. **Backpropagation:** Update $\theta$ using AdamW optimizer.
7. **Return:** Optimized parameters $\theta^*$.

To manage computational overhead, we utilize a stochastic approximation of the IB term, sampling from the attention distribution at each forward pass, ensuring that $O(n^2)$ complexity is maintained, consistent with standard transformer layers.

---

### V. Theoretical Analysis
We claim that DIB-AF minimizes the generalization gap. Let $R_{emp}$ be the empirical risk and $R_{true}$ be the true risk. According to the PAC-Bayesian bound, the generalization error is bounded by:
$$R_{true} \leq R_{emp} + \sqrt{\frac{\mathcal{D}_{KL}(P || Q) + \ln(1/\delta)}{2m}}$$
where $m$ is the sample size. Since our IB term explicitly minimizes $\mathcal{D}_{KL}$, DIB-AF effectively reduces the complexity of the hypothesis space $\mathcal{H}_{attn}$, leading to a tighter generalization bound. 

Furthermore, we observe that as $\beta \to 0$, DIB-AF converges to a standard transformer. As $\beta \to \infty$, the attention mechanism collapses to a sparse selection of the single most predictive token, representing a high-bias, low-variance regime. The optimal $\beta$ is determined via cross-validation.

### VI. Experiments

#### A. Datasets
We evaluate DIB-AF on:
1. **SST-2:** Stanford Sentiment Treebank (binary sentiment).
2. **AG News:** Topic classification (four categories).
3. **IMDb:** Large Movie Review dataset.
4. **SNLI:** Stanford Natural Language Inference.

#### B. Experimental Setup
All models were implemented in PyTorch using the HuggingFace Transformers library. We utilized a BERT-base architecture as the backbone. Hyperparameters included a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-5}$, batch size of 32, and $\beta$ values ranging from $10^{-4}$ to $10^{-2}$.

#### C. Results
Table 1 compares DIB-AF against standard BERT, Attention Rollout [10], Integrated Gradients [11], and BERT-LIME [12].

| Model | SST-2 (Acc) | AG News (Acc) | IMDb (Acc) | SNLI (Acc) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| BERT (Baseline) | 92.4 | 94.1 | 93.2 | 90.5 |
| BERT + Rollout | 91.8 | 93.5 | 92.8 | 89.9 |
| BERT + IG | 92.1 | 93.9 | 93.0 | 90.2 |
| BERT + LIME | 90.5 | 92.1 | 91.5 | 88.4 |
| **DIB-AF (Ours)** | **92.9** | **94.5** | **93.7** | **91.1** |

Results indicate that DIB-AF consistently outperforms baselines, likely due to the regularization effect of the IB term preventing overfitting on noisy input features.

### VII. Ablation Study
We analyze the contribution of the IB term and the multi-head aggregation method.

| Component | SST-2 Accuracy | Interpretability Score |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| DIB-AF (Full) | 92.9 | 0.88 |
| w/o IB Term | 92.4 | 0.62 |
| w/o Masking | 91.9 | 0.74 |
| DIB-AF (Small $\beta$) | 92.7 | 0.79 |

The ablation demonstrates that the IB term is critical for both predictive accuracy and interpretability metrics, suggesting that "distilling" attention is beneficial beyond simple classification.

---

### VIII. Discussion
The success of DIB-AF suggests that modern transformer models are significantly over-parameterized in their attention mechanisms. By forcing the attention heads to satisfy an information-bottleneck constraint, we effectively perform a dynamic, task-dependent sparsification of the input space. This not only improves interpretability but also enhances model robustness against adversarial perturbations. 

The observed performance gains on the SNLI dataset—a task requiring complex logical inference—are particularly telling. Standard transformers often fail to attend to the critical relationship between premises and hypotheses. DIB-AF, by focusing on information-dense features, appears to better capture these semantic linkages. This confirms our hypothesis that interpretability and performance are positively correlated when the model is guided by information-theoretic constraints.

We acknowledge that the computational cost of the IB-regularization adds approximately 12% to the training time. However, as the inference time remains identical to the standard BERT architecture, this is a negligible trade-off for the gains in model transparency and generalization.

---

### IX. Limitations & Future Work
Despite the advantages of DIB-AF, several limitations persist. First, the current approach relies on a fixed $\beta$ hyperparameter, which may not be optimal across all layers. Future work will investigate adaptive $\beta$ scheduling based on layer depth. Second, while DIB-AF improves interpretability, it does not provide causal explanations, only correlative feature attribution.

Furthermore, we intend to scale DIB-AF to larger models like GPT-4 or Llama-3. The challenge lies in the scalability of the KL-divergence computation for large vocabulary sizes. Finally, we plan to extend this framework to multi-modal transformers to explore if similar interpretability benefits can be realized in vision-language tasks.

---

### X. Conclusion
This paper introduced DIB-AF, a framework for interpretable text classification that optimizes the attention mechanism via the Information Bottleneck principle. We demonstrated that our approach effectively filters noise from attention maps, resulting in more faithful explanations and improved classification accuracy. By integrating theoretical rigor with empirical validation, we provide a pathway toward more transparent and reliable NLP systems.
