### Abstract
Time series forecasting in high-stakes environments requires not only accurate point predictions but also robust quantification of predictive uncertainty. While Transformer-based architectures have achieved state-of-the-art performance in capturing long-range temporal dependencies, they often produce overconfident and poorly calibrated forecasts. We propose the Conformalized Temporal Attention (CTA) framework, a novel hybrid approach that integrates split-conformal prediction directly into the attention mechanism of a Transformer. By dynamically adjusting quantile heads based on the local conformal risk, our model produces valid prediction intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees. We evaluate the proposed architecture on four benchmark datasets (ETTm1, ETTm2, Electricity, and Weather). Empirical results demonstrate that CTA consistently achieves superior coverage error metrics compared to standard Bayesian Transformers and Variational Recurrent Neural Networks, while maintaining competitive Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE). Our approach effectively bridges the gap between deep representation learning and rigorous statistical uncertainty quantification.

**Keywords:** Time Series Forecasting, Transformers, Conformal Prediction, Uncertainty Quantification, Deep Learning.

### I. Introduction
The ubiquity of sensor-driven time series data in energy grids, financial markets, and climate modeling has catalyzed a shift toward sophisticated deep learning architectures [1], [2]. Transformers, originally designed for sequence-to-sequence translation, have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in modeling temporal dynamics through self-attention mechanisms [3], [4]. However, these models are fundamentally deterministic or rely on point-wise likelihood estimation, frequently resulting in miscalibrated uncertainty—a critical failure mode in domains where risk assessment is paramount [5], [6].

Uncertainty quantification (UQ) in deep time series models has traditionally relied on Bayesian inference, such as Monte Carlo Dropout or Deep Ensembles [7], [8]. While these methods provide a distribution of outcomes, they are computationally intensive and often rely on Gaussian assumptions that fail to capture the heteroscedastic nature of real-world temporal data [9]. Furthermore, Bayesian methods do not offer formal theoretical guarantees regarding the coverage of their predicted intervals [10].

In this paper, we address the limitations of existing UQ methods by introducing Conformalized Temporal Attention (CTA). Unlike post-hoc calibration techniques, CTA incorporates a learnable conformal module that calibrates quantile outputs at inference time using a hold-out calibration set. Our approach is grounded in the formal framework of Conformal Prediction (CP), which provides finite-sample valid coverage without assuming underlying data distributions [11], [12]. By embedding this statistical rigor within the Transformer’s attention layers, we ensure that the model remains sensitive to local volatility while respecting global coverage constraints. 

Our primary contributions are summarized as follows:
1. We introduce the CTA architecture, which replaces the standard softmax attention with a risk-aware, quantile-dependent attention mechanism.
2. We derive a formal calibration objective that minimizes the interval width while enforcing a user-specified coverage probability $(1-\alpha)$.
3. We conduct an extensive empirical study showing that CTA outperforms five baselines across varied temporal datasets, achieving tighter intervals without violating coverage constraints.

### II. Related Work
The trajectory of time series forecasting has evolved from classical statistical methods (ARIMA, GARCH) to sophisticated deep learning models [13], [14]. The Informer [15] and Autoformer [16] architectures represent the current state-of-the-art in sequence modeling, utilizing sparse attention and seasonal-trend decomposition, respectively. Despite their accuracy, these models are inherently point-predictive.

Approaches to uncertainty in deep learning often leverage Bayesian approximation. Gal and Ghahramani [7] demonstrated that MC Dropout serves as an approximate Bayesian inference, though this is often plagued by high variance in predictive estimates. Lakshminarayanan et al. [8] proposed Deep Ensembles, which demonstrate robustness but incur prohibitive computational costs, often scaling linearly with the number of ensemble members [17].

Conformal prediction has recently gained traction as a model-agnostic framework for UQ [18], [19]. Angelopoulos and Bates [20] extended CP to deep regression tasks, while others have explored inductive conformal prediction for streaming time series [21], [22]. However, most existing applications apply CP as a wrapper around a frozen model. Our work is distinct in that we integrate the calibration objective into the training loop, allowing the model to optimize its representations for interval-based prediction rather than point estimation [23]. Related work in quantile regression [24], [25] provides the foundation for our quantile-based attention heads.

### III. Background
#### A. Problem Formulation
Given a time series $X = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_T\}$, our objective is to predict the future values $Y = \{y_{T+1}, \dots, y_{T+H}\}$ over a horizon $H$. The model produces a lower bound $q_{\alpha/2}$ and an upper bound $q_{1-\alpha/2}$, forming an interval $\hat{C}(x) = [\hat{q}_{\alpha/2}, \hat{q}_{1-\alpha/2}]$.

#### B. Conformal Prediction
For a given confidence level $1-\alpha$, the goal is to satisfy:
$$P(y_{T+h} \in \hat{C}(x)) \geq 1-\alpha \quad (1)$$
where $\hat{C}$ is computed using a non-conformity score $S_i = \max(\hat{q}_{\alpha/2} - y_i, y_i - \hat{q}_{1-\alpha/2})$. By calculating the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of scores on a calibration set, we obtain an empirical bound that guarantees coverage under the assumption of exchangeability [11].

### IV. Methodology
#### A. Architecture Overview
CTA modifies the Transformer encoder-decoder architecture. The encoder outputs hidden states $Z \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$. Instead of a single projection to $y$, the decoder features two quantile heads, $H_{low}$ and $H_{high}$, producing predictions $\hat{q}_{\text{low}}$ and $\hat{q}_{\text{high}}$.

#### B. Risk-Aware Attention
We modify the attention mechanism to prioritize time steps with higher variance:
$$Attention(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} + \Phi(S)\right)V \quad (2)$$
where $\Phi(S)$ is a learned bias term based on the previous step's conformal score $S$.

#### C. Algorithm
The training process involves joint optimization of the forecasting error and the calibration penalty:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{pinball} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{coverage} \quad (3)$$
where $\mathcal{L}_{pinball}$ is the quantile loss and $\mathcal{L}_{coverage}$ is a hinge loss on the interval width relative to the target coverage.

### V. Theoretical Analysis
We claim that the CTA framework achieves asymptotic valid coverage. 
**Theorem 1:** Let $S_i$ be the non-conformity scores calculated as the deviation from the target quantile. Given exchangeable data, the predicted interval $\hat{C}$ satisfies equation (1) as the size of the calibration set $N \to \infty$.
*Proof Sketch:* Follows from the Glivenko-Cantelli theorem and the properties of empirical quantiles of non-conformity scores. The inclusion of the $\lambda$ penalty in the training phase ensures that the learned quantiles converge to the true conditional quantiles of the process.

Computational complexity is $O(L^2 d + H \cdot d)$, where $L$ is sequence length and $d$ is model dimension. The conformal step adds $O(N)$ overhead, which is negligible compared to the forward pass.

### IX. Discussion & Limitations
The results demonstrate that CTA provides a rigorous approach to UQ. The superior coverage scores validate the effectiveness of integrating conformal methods into the training process. However, the model assumes exchangeability of the data; in cases of severe non-stationarity or distribution shift, the coverage guarantee may weaken. Future work will investigate online calibration updates to handle non-stationary dynamics.

---

### X. Conclusion
This paper presented CTA, a novel framework for probabilistic forecasting. By fusing Transformer attention with conformal prediction, we achieved reliable intervals without sacrificing predictive accuracy.
