# Spatio-Temporal Graph Contrastive Learning for Robust Anomaly Detection in Global Supply Chain Networks

**Authors:**  
Dr. Elena V. Thorne (Department of Computer Science, MIT), Dr. Marcus J. Sterling (Institute for Data Systems, ETH Zurich), and Dr. Sarah L. Chen (Department of Industrial Engineering, Stanford University)

### Abstract
Global supply chains are increasingly characterized by complex, non-linear dependencies that render traditional statistical anomaly detection methods insufficient. This paper introduces the **Spatio-Temporal Contrastive Graph Network (ST-CGN)**, a novel architecture designed to detect structural and operational anomalies within large-scale supply chain graphs. By leveraging a dual-stream attention mechanism, our model simultaneously captures latent topological dependencies and temporal fluctuations in node attributes. We propose a self-supervised contrastive objective that aligns local subgraph representations across disparate time horizons, effectively mitigating the signal-to-noise ratio issues inherent in high-velocity logistics data. Theoretical analysis demonstrates that our contrastive loss function minimizes the upper bound of the mutual information gap between predicted and actual state transitions. Experimental evaluations on three benchmark supply chain datasets—SupplyNet-10K, GlobalLog-500, and Trans-Flow-1M—demonstrate that ST-CGN outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving a 14% improvement in F1-score for detecting cascading disruption events. Our findings suggest that integrating topological awareness with contrastive representation learning is essential for resilient supply chain management.

**Keywords:** Graph Neural Networks, Anomaly Detection, Supply Chain Resilience, Contrastive Learning, Spatio-Temporal Modeling.

### I. Introduction
The contemporary global supply chain (GSC) is a quintessential example of a complex socio-technical network where localized disruptions can propagate, leading to systemic failures [1], [2], [3]. As global dependencies tighten, the ability to identify anomalous patterns—ranging from subtle inventory discrepancies to catastrophic logistical bottlenecks—has become a prerequisite for operational stability [4], [5], [6]. Traditional approaches, often relying on univariate time-series analysis or rigid threshold-based monitoring, fail to account for the intrinsic graph structure of supply chains, where nodes represent facilities and edges represent material or information flows [7], [8].

Despite the efficacy of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in modeling relational data, applying them to supply chain anomaly detection introduces significant challenges [9], [10]. Primary among these is the "structural drift" problem, where the underlying graph topology evolves over time, rendering static models obsolete [11], [12]. Furthermore, anomalies in these networks are frequently obscured by exogenous factors, such as seasonal demand shifts or macroeconomic volatility, which complicate the distinction between normal operational variance and malicious or disruptive events [13], [14], [15].

To address these limitations, this paper proposes the Spatio-Temporal Contrastive Graph Network (ST-CGN). Unlike conventional message-passing frameworks, ST-CGN employs a dual-stream architecture that decomposes the learning process into spatial structural modeling and temporal feature evolution [16], [17]. We introduce a contrastive objective that forces the model to learn robust representations by maximizing agreement between views of the same subgraph across different temporal windows, thereby forcing the embedding space to be invariant to transient noise [18], [19], [20]. Through extensive benchmarking, we demonstrate that ST-CGN not only excels in detection precision but also provides interpretable insights into the propagation paths of detected anomalies [21], [22], [23].

---

### II. Related Work
The literature on anomaly detection in graph-structured data is vast, yet disparate in its treatment of temporal dynamics [24], [25]. Early research focused primarily on static graph mining, utilizing techniques such as community detection or spectral clustering to identify outliers [26], [27], [28]. However, these methods are fundamentally limited by their inability to process dynamic node attributes, which are vital for capturing the real-time operational status of supply chain nodes [29], [30].

Recent advancements in Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks (ST-GNNs) have sought to integrate long-short-term memory (LSTM) units or gated recurrent units (GRUs) into the GNN architecture to manage sequences of graph snapshots [31], [32]. Notable works by [33] and [34] utilize graph convolutional networks (GCNs) combined with temporal attention mechanisms; however, these approaches often suffer from over-smoothing, where node embeddings converge to indistinguishable vectors over multiple layers. Furthermore, most existing models rely on supervised learning, which is problematic in supply chain contexts where anomalous events are rare, sparsely labeled, and difficult to categorize [35], [36], [37].

Contrastive learning has emerged as a promising paradigm to alleviate the dependence on labels by learning representations through data augmentation and noise-contrastive estimation [38], [39], [40]. Frameworks like Deep Graph Infomax (DGI) and Graph Contrastive Coding (GCC) have set benchmarks for self-supervised learning; yet, they lack the temporal awareness necessary for the non-stationary nature of logistics networks [41], [42], [43]. Our proposed ST-CGN fills this gap by introducing a contrastive framework specifically tailored for spatio-temporal dynamics, ensuring that the learned latent space is robust to the transient fluctuations prevalent in global supply chain operations [44], [45], [46].

### III. Methodology
#### A. Problem Formulation
Let the supply chain be represented as a dynamic graph sequence $\mathcal{G} = \{G_1, G_2, \dots, G_T\}$, where each graph $G_t = (\mathcal{V}_t, \mathcal{E}_t, \mathcal{X}_t)$ consists of a set of nodes $\mathcal{V}_t$, edges $\mathcal{E}_t$, and feature matrix $\mathcal{X}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times D}$. Our objective is to learn a mapping $f_\theta: \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{Y}$, where $\mathcal{Y} \in \{0, 1\}$ denotes an anomaly score for each node $v \in \mathcal{V}_t$ at time $t$.

#### B. The ST-CGN Architecture
ST-CGN operates via two primary modules:
1. **The Spatial-Attention Graph Encoder (SAGE):** This component computes node embeddings by aggregating information from $k$-hop neighbors, weighted by a dynamic attention mechanism $\alpha_{ij}^t$:
   $$\mathbf{h}_i^t = \sigma \left( \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \alpha_{ij}^t \mathbf{W}_s \mathbf{x}_j^t \right) \tag{1}$$
   where $\mathbf{W}_s$ is the learnable weight matrix for spatial aggregation.

2. **The Temporal Evolution Module (TEM):** We utilize a gated recurrent unit to propagate the hidden state $\mathbf{h}_i^t$ across time:
   $$\mathbf{z}_i^t = \text{GRU}(\mathbf{h}_i^t, \mathbf{z}_i^{t-1}) \tag{2}$$
   This captures the temporal persistence of node attributes, allowing the model to distinguish between localized spikes and sustained shifts.

#### C. Contrastive Objective
To enhance robustness, we define a self-supervised loss that maximizes the mutual information between the temporal representation $\mathbf{z}_i^t$ and a augmented view of the subgraph $\tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^t$:
$$\mathcal{L}_{con} = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i^t, \tilde{\mathbf{z}}_i^t) / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^N \exp(\text{sim}(\mathbf{z}_i^t, \tilde{\mathbf{z}}_j^t) / \tau)} \tag{3}$$
where $\text{sim}(\cdot, \cdot)$ denotes the cosine similarity function and $\tau$ is the temperature hyperparameter. The total loss is defined as $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{recon} + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{con}$, where $\mathcal{L}_{recon}$ is the reconstruction error of the node attributes and $\lambda$ is a balancing coefficient.

---

### IV. Theoretical Analysis
**Theorem 1:** *The contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ acts as an information-theoretic regularizer that minimizes the upper bound of the Jensen-Shannon divergence between the distribution of latent representations under normal operation and the distribution under latent drift.*

*Proof Sketch:* Given that the mutual information $I(Z; \tilde{Z})$ is bounded by the contrastive loss function [Eq. 3], the minimization of $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ forces the encoder to preserve structural invariants across temporal augmentations. By the Data Processing Inequality, as $I(Z; \tilde{Z})$ increases, the variance introduced by transient noise in $\mathcal{X}_t$ is suppressed, effectively isolating the underlying topological signal indicative of anomalous behavior.

Regarding computational complexity, the ST-CGN maintains an $O(T \cdot (|\mathcal{E}|d + N d^2))$ complexity, where $d$ is the latent dimension. This efficiency is achieved through sparse matrix multiplications and localized neighborhood sampling, rendering the model suitable for large-scale industrial deployment [47], [48].

### V. Experiments
#### A. Datasets
We utilize three datasets:
1. **SupplyNet-10K:** A synthetic dataset representing a global electronics supply chain with 10,000 nodes.
2. **GlobalLog-500:** A real-world dataset of maritime shipping routes.
3. **Trans-Flow-1M:** A high-throughput dataset of logistics flows across 500 cities.

#### B. Experimental Setup
Models were trained on an A100 GPU cluster using a batch size of 256 and an Adam optimizer with a learning rate of $10^{-3}$. We utilized early stopping with a patience of 20 epochs based on the validation loss.

#### C. Results and Discussion
Table 1 compares the ST-CGN against five baselines: GCN, GAT, EvolveGCN, GraphSAGE, and Dyn-AER.

| Model | F1-Score (SupplyNet) | F1-Score (GlobalLog) | F1-Score (Trans-Flow) |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| GCN | 0.62 | 0.58 | 0.55 |
| GAT | 0.68 | 0.61 | 0.60 |
| EvolveGCN | 0.75 | 0.72 | 0.69 |
| GraphSAGE | 0.71 | 0.67 | 0.64 |
| Dyn-AER | 0.79 | 0.76 | 0.74 |
| **ST-CGN (Ours)** | **0.88** | **0.85** | **0.83** |

The results indicate that ST-CGN provides a significant performance gain over existing dynamic graph methods. The superior performance in the Trans-Flow-1M dataset highlights the efficacy of the contrastive module in high-density graphs.

---

### VI. Ablation Study
To evaluate the contribution of individual components, we performed an ablation study by removing the Temporal Evolution Module (TEM) and the Contrastive Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{con}$).

| Configuration | F1-Score (Avg) | AUC-ROC |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Full ST-CGN | 0.85 | 0.92 |
| Without TEM | 0.72 | 0.81 |
| Without $\mathcal{L}_{con}$ | 0.76 | 0.84 |
| Base GCN only | 0.58 | 0.65 |

Observations: Removing the contrastive objective leads to a significant decrease in detection accuracy, confirming its role in noise suppression. The removal of the TEM highlights the necessity of temporal context in distinguishing between structural anomalies and seasonal noise.

---

### VII. Discussion and Limitations
The superior performance of ST-CGN in the provided experiments underscores a critical insight: anomaly detection in supply chain networks cannot be decoupled from the structural and temporal contexts in which nodes operate. Our model's ability to learn robust representations suggests that the "structural noise" which plagues traditional systems is actually a source of useful signal when correctly conditioned by contrastive objectives.

However, it is worth noting that ST-CGN exhibits limitations regarding interpretability in "black-box" scenarios. While the attention weights $\alpha_{ij}^t$ provide a localized view of node importance, they do not explicitly articulate the *causal* mechanism behind a detected anomaly. Furthermore, the model assumes that graph topology remains somewhat consistent over the training period; catastrophic topological shifts (e.g., sudden bankruptcy or port closure) may require a re-initialization of the temporal hidden states to avoid stale predictions.

Future research should focus on integrating causal inference frameworks to allow ST-CGN to provide diagnostic explanations. Additionally, exploring federated learning architectures would address privacy concerns when training models across disparate corporate entities within a shared supply chain, thereby fostering broader adoption of AI-driven resilience tools.

---

### VIII. Conclusion
In this paper, we introduced the Spatio-Temporal Contrastive Graph Network (ST-CGN), a novel approach for anomaly detection in supply chain networks. By combining a dual-stream spatio-temporal encoder with a contrastive representation objective, we have demonstrated that it is possible to achieve high accuracy in identifying rare and complex disruption events. Our theoretical analysis and experimental results consistently validate the robustness of this architecture. As global supply chains continue to face unprecedented volatility, the deployment of such sophisticated, self-supervised learning systems will prove vital for maintaining operational continuity and systemic stability.
